ABSTRACT
While many modern segmentation models achieve appealing performance, they often come with high
computational costs, such as massive parameter counts, intensive calculations, excessive memory consumption,
and prolonged runtime. These burdens make them impractical for resource-limited medical devices. To address
this challenge, we strategically integrate several established modules, including asymmetric convolution,
dilated convolution, and attention modules, to develop a novel medical image segmentation backbone named
BMIS. Specifically, unlike traditional U-Nets, BMIS adopts a shallower yet wider structure. This design not only
enables effective learning of high-level semantic features but also markedly mitigates the loss of fine-grained
details caused by frequent downsampling. Consequently, the decoder in BMIS can focus primarily on spatial
reconstruction rather than compensating for lost details, which greatly reduces its workload. Leveraging these
benefits, the decoder of BMIS does not need to be as complex as its encoder, and skip connections between
the encoder and decoder are no longer necessary. These two factors collectively reduce model complexity
without compromising segmentation performance. Extensive segmentation experiments across five medical
imaging modalities demonstrate that BMIS achieves a better balance between segmentation performance and
computational costs compared to twenty-nine competing methods. In short, compared with the best-performing
comparative method DSU-Net, BMIS improves IoU by вҲј0.32%, while substantially reducing model parameters
by 97.54%, FLOPs by 68.49%, GPU memory usage by 54.48%, training time by 58.82%, and inference time
by 80.00%. These impressive results highlight BMISвҖҷs great potential for deployment in medical devices with
limited computational resources.

1. Introduction
Medical image segmentation holds paramount importance in computer vision, affording clinicians vital insights into complex anatomical
regions. These insights are invaluable in pinpointing injuries, monitoring disease progression, and assessing treatment requirements. Consequently, with the escalating demand for intelligent medical image analysis, the significance of precise and automated segmentation techniques
is escalating exponentially.
In response to this demand, many deep learning methods based on
an encoderвҖ“decoder architecture have emerged. The encoderвҖҷs primary
function is to generate feature maps containing important semantic information through progressive downsampling, while the decoder
compensates for the loss of spatial details, restores resolution, and

predicts pixel-wise labels. As a representative encoderвҖ“decoder architecture, U-Net [1] has excelled in various medical segmentation tasks
and has become the foundation for many state-of-the-art medical image
segmentation methods. Inspired by U-NetвҖҷs success, researchers have
incorporated advanced techniques such as attention mechanisms [2],
Transformer [3], and Mamba [4] into its architecture, resulting in a
series of improved networks, including Attention U-Net [5], DenseASPP [6], MSCA-Net [7], TransFuse [8], and VM-UNet [9]. Although
these networks exhibit strong segmentation performance, their impressive results often come at a high computational costs, such as large
parameter counts, complex calculations, substantial memory usage,
and prolonged runtime, making them unsuitable for deployment on
resource-constrained medical devices.

To successfully deploy on resource-constrained medical devices,
researchers developed efficient segmentation networks like ConvUNeXt
[10], MobileNetV2+ [11], TinyU-Net [12], MMViT-Seg [13], and
MambaU-Lite [14]. While these methods offer lighter weight or faster
operations compared to traditional U-Nets, they still face two key
challenges. First, these models only partially optimize computational
costs. For example, ConvUNeXt boasts a low parameter count but
high FLOPs, while MambaU-Lite has both fewer parameters and lower
FLOPs, yet suffers from slower inference speed. This is mainly because
some operations do not increase the number of parameters but will
frequently access memory, resulting in slow running speed. Secondly,
their segmentation performance lags behind that of more complex
methods. This is primarily due to the relevant single information flow in
the lightweight models, limiting their effectiveness in handling complex
and variable medical images, resulting in suboptimal segmentation
results.
Given the above discussion, we suggest that an ideal model that
can meet the needs of resource-constrained medical devices should
possess four key attributes: (i) Practicality, with a straightforward
design that facilitates seamless customization for specific applications.
(ii) Consistency, achieved through minimal core modules to simplify
model complexity. (iii) Effectiveness, demonstrated by consistently
high performance across diverse segmentation tasks, regardless of data
modality. (iv) Efficiency, characterized by reduced parameters, FLOPs,
and memory usage, along with accelerated training and inference
speeds.
Inspired by the four attributes enumerated above, we strategically
integrate several established modules, such as asymmetric convolution, dilated convolution, and attention modules, to propose a novel

medical image segmentation backbone named BMIS. The proposed
BMIS can better balance segmentation performance and computational
costs through its minimalistic network design. Specifically, unlike conventional U-Nets with deep and narrow architectures, BMIS adopts
a shallower yet wider structure. This design not only facilitates effective learning of high-level semantic features but also substantially
reduces the loss of fine-grained details caused by frequent downsampling. Consequently, the decoder of BMIS primarily focuses on spatial
reconstruction rather than compensating for lost details, significantly
reducing its computational burden. By leveraging these advantages, the
decoder of the proposed BMIS no longer needs to be as complex as its
encoder. Moreover, the encoder and decoder of BMIS no longer require
the skip connection operation. These factors collectively reduce model
complexity while maintaining high performance. As demonstrated in
Table 1 and Fig. 1, BMIS significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
methods in five segmentation tasks with competitive computational
costs. These merits make BMIS a promising backbone for medical image
segmentation in resource-constrained settings, where both segmentation precision and computational efficiency are critical. The code of
BMIS is available at https://github.com/duweidai/BMIS.

2.2. Low-cost segmentation methods

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 briefly
summarizes the related work. Section 3 provides detailed information
about the proposed BMIS. Section 4 presents a comprehensive comparison between BMIS and twenty-nine other segmentation methods.
Section 5 is dedicated to discussion, and Section 6 concludes the paper.

To enable successful deployment on resource-constrained devices,
various methods have been developed to enhance the efficiency of deep
neural models.
Inceptionv3 [22] was one of the pioneering efforts in crafting
efficient neural networks. It employed asymmetric convolutions to decompose standard convolutions into smaller and more computationally
efficient operations. Subsequently, SqueezeNet [23] reduced model
parameters by using 1 Г— 1 filters instead of 3 Г— 3 convolutions, effectively compressing channel dimensions. Xception [24] advanced this
paradigm through depth-wise separable convolutions, decoupling spatial filtering (depth-wise) from channel mixing (point-wise) to minimize
redundancy. Similarly, MobileNets [25] adopted depth-wise separable convolutions as their core building block, achieving parameter
efficiency while preserving accuracy. MobileNetV2 [26] further innovated with inverted residual blocks, combining depth-wise convolutions with linear bottleneck layers to enhance feature propagation in
low-dimensional spaces.
AlexNet [27] introduced grouped convolutions, a strategy that splits
filters into distinct groups to reduce computational costs. Although
computationally efficient, traditional grouped convolutions suffer from
limited inter-group feature interaction, restricting their representational capacity. To address this, ShuffleNet [28] introduced a channel
shuffle unit that rearranges output channels to enhance cross-group
feature interactions. Building on this, ShuffleNetV2 [29] fused three
sequential operations (concatenation, channel shuffling, and splitting)
into a unified operation, enhancing feature reuse while minimizing
redundancy.
Beyond the classic lightweight methods mentioned above, recent advancements in artificial intelligence have given rise to numerous more
efficient segmentation methods. For instance, EGE-UNet [30] proposed
a group multi-axis Hadamard product attention and group aggregation bridge modules to significantly enhance UNet efficiency. TinyUNet [12] utilized a cascade multi-receptive fields module to boost feature representation while maintaining low parameters. MALUNet [31]
combined multiple attention mechanisms with a streamlined UNet
architecture, achieving competitive performance with reduced computational overhead. MMViT-Seg [13] fused MobileViT blocks with
a multi-query attention module for lightweight segmentation. BATFormer [32] proposed a boundary-aware lightweight transformer with
cross-scale global and boundary-aware local modules for efficient medical segmentation. MambaU-Lite [14] synergized Mamba and CNN
architectures via a novel P-Mamba block and integrated channel-spatial
attention, notably reducing parameter count. Lastly, ULVM-UNet [33]
drastically reduced parameters and FLOPs by incorporating a parallel
vision Mamba layer, enhancing scalability for edge devices. For more
lightweight segmentation methods, please refer to [34вҖ“36].

2. Related works
This section categorizes related works into two groups: highperformance segmentation methods and low-cost segmentation methods. The former usually seeks higher segmentation performance with
minimal consideration for computational costs, while the latter hopes
to minimize the computational costs without sacrificing segmentation
performance.
2.1. High-performance segmentation methods
To enhance medical image segmentation performance, Ronneberger
et al. introduced skip connections into a fully connected network, resulting in the well-known U-Net [1]. However, despite aiding encoderвҖ“
decoder information fusion, skip connections struggle with the significant semantic gap between low- and high-resolution features, often
leading to blurred predictions. To address this, Zhou et al. proposed
U-Net++ with nested skip connections [15], and Huang et al. developed U-Net3+ with full-scale skip connections to integrate contextual information across different resolutions [16]. Additionally, Feng
et al. proposed CPFNet, which dynamically incorporates global and
multi-scale context information via two pyramidal modules [17]. Sun
et al. developed MSCA-Net, which fully utilizes multi-scale contextual
information in images to improve segmentation performance [7].
By combining spatial pyramid pooling (SPP) and atrous convolution
techniques, Chen et al. developed the atrous spatial pyramid pooling
(ASPP) module and the DeepLab series of networks [18], which exhibit
strong capability in recognizing objects of varying scales. However, the
original DeepLab struggled with precise boundary delineation due to its
reliance on basic bilinear interpolation for restoring feature resolution
after ASPP. In response, Yang et al. developed the densely connected
ASPP structure (DenseASPP) [6]. This structureвҖҷs decoder integrates
low-level ResNet features with their respective predictions, thereby
enhancing the accuracy in detecting objects of varying shapes and
refining boundaries.
With the continuous advancement of artificial intelligence,
researchers have integrated advanced techniques such as
Transformer [3] and Mamba [4] into the U-shaped architecture, giving
rise to a series of improved networks. For example, Swin-Unet [19]
introduced a pure Transformer-based U-shaped architecture using Swin

3. Proposed method
3.1. Overall
The central objective of this study is to achieve an optimal balance
between segmentation performance and the associated computational
costs with a minimalistic network architecture. To accomplish this,
we strategically integrate several well-established modules, including
asymmetric convolution, dilated convolution, and attention modules,
to propose a novel 2D medical image segmentation backbone named
BMIS (see Fig. 2).
In contrast to conventional U-Nets, which are characterized by deep
and narrow architectures, the proposed BMIS adopts a shallower yet
wider structure. This design enables effective learning of high-level
semantic features in the image while substantially reduces the loss of
detailed information caused by frequent downsampling. Consequently,
the decoder of BMIS primarily focuses on spatial reconstruction rather
than compensating for lost details. This significantly reduces the decoderвҖҷs workload, making it easier to generate accurate segmentation
results. Thanks to this advantage, the proposed BMIS introduces three
structural innovations: (i) It enables asymmetric decoders, meaning the
decoder of BMIS can be much simpler than the encoder. (ii) It can eliminate the skip connections, a standard configuration in traditional codec
networks, between the encoder and decoder without segmentation
performance sacrifice. (iii) It can save the network from doubling the
number of feature maps after each down-sampling operation, allowing
it to achieve powerful feature learning ability with fewer feature maps.
These three innovative designs collectively contribute to a substantial
reduction in network complexity.

3.4. Channel & spatial attention module
To further expand the networkвҖҷs receptive field, we introduce the
widely used CSAM module [37], which can efficiently capture longrange dependencies and multi-channel representations (see Fig. 5). The
input to CSAM is processed through two parallel attention blocks: the
spatial attention block and the channel attention block. These two
blocks work together to enhance featuresвҖҷ sensitivity to both spatial and
channel attention.
Notably, in this work, the implementation of CSAM differs slightly
from its original version to better align with our lightweight backbone
design. Specifically, we reduce the number of channels for вҖҳвҖҳQвҖҷвҖҷ and вҖҳвҖҳKвҖҷвҖҷ
from рқҗ¶ to рқҗ¶вҲ•2. For more implementation details on the CSAM module,
please refer to Refs. [37,40,41].

3.3. Enhanced asymmetric convolution module
We propose an enhanced asymmetric convolution module (EACM,
see Fig. 4) to improve the feature learning ability of traditional convolution while reducing the computational costs. The proposed EACM
mainly consists of asymmetric convolution, efficient channel attention [38], and residual connections.
Previous studies have demonstrated that a рқ‘ҳ Г— рқ‘ҳ convolution
can be efficiently decomposed into a sequence of a рқ‘ҳ Г— 1 filter
followed by a 1 Г— рқ‘ҳ filter. Drawing from these insights, Kahatapitiya
et al. revealed that convolutional layers could be reformulated using
1D filters, incorporating non-linearities and activation functions for
enhanced functionality [39]. This decomposition not only simplifies the
architecture but also cuts computational costs. Moreover, introducing
nonlinearity between these 1D filters effectively enhances learning
capacity. Therefore, in this paper, we consistently replace the 3 Г— 3
convolution with two asymmetric convolutions: 3 Г— 1 and 1 Г— 3.

attention modules to boost segmentation performance, but this comes

3.6. Evaluation criteria
To evaluate the performance of various segmentation models, we
employ three widely adopted metrics: Intersection over Union (IoU)
for quantifying spatial overlap, Dice Similarity Coefficient (Dice) for
measuring region similarity, and Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) for
assessing threshold-dependent robustness.
In addition, to assess statistical significance, we apply a paired ttest algorithm [42] to calculate p-values comparing BMIS with each
comparison method across three performance metrics: IoU, Dice, and
AUC. Following commonly accepted statistical practice, we adopt a
significance threshold of рқ‘қ-value < 0.01 for all comparisons [43,44].
3.7. Network configuration and training procedure
The proposed network is implemented using PyTorch on an NVIDIA
GeForce RTX 2080Ti GPU. The initial learning rate is set to 0.001, and
the learning rate schedule follows the CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts
policy with parameters рқ‘Ү0 = 10 and рқ‘Үmult = 2. In addition, the adaptive
moment estimation (Adam) optimizer with a weight decay of 0.00005
is used throughout training. All comparison methods are trained for
250 epochs with a batch size of 4, and the best-performing model on
the validation set is selected for testing. Importantly, to ensure fair
comparisons, all input data is resized to 320 Г— 320 pixels and no data
augmentation is applied.

4.7. Summary of computational costs and segmentation performance
Through extensive experiments on five segmentation tasks, we observed that among the comparative methods (excluding BMIS), DSUNet is the best-performing model when computational costs are not
considered, MedT is the top-performing lightweight method, and CANet achieves the best balance between segmentation performance and
computational costs (see Tables 3 to 6). However, our proposed BMIS
surpasses DSU-Net, MedT, and CA-Net in both segmentation performance and computational efficiency (see Table 7). Specifically, compared to DSU-Net, BMIS achieves an average IoU improvement of
0.32% across the five segmentation tasks, while requiring significantly
lower computational costs. When compared to MedT, BMIS boosts the
average IoU by 0.88% and significantly improves training and inference
speeds. Moreover, BMIS outperforms CA-Net with a 1.51% increase
in average IoU, while demanding substantially fewer computational
resources, particularly in reference time.
These findings highlight that the proposed BMIS attains a better
balance between segmentation performance and computational costs
compared to other state-of-the-art methods, making it well-suited for
integration into medical devices with limited computational resources.

5.2. Study of asymmetric structure
The classical U-Net [1] has a symmetric encoderвҖ“decoder structure,
in which the decoder is often used to compensate for the loss of
spatial details, restore resolution, and predict pixel-wise labels. Subsequent segmentation networks have widely adopted this symmetric
codec design. This study explores the impact of an asymmetric codec
structure on both U-Net and BMIS. The experimental results are shown
in Table 9, where an asymmetric structure indicates that the decoder is
considerably less complex than its encoder. The data in Table 9 reveals
two key insights: (i) Adopting an asymmetric decoder dramatically cuts
computational costs for both U-Net and BMIS; (ii) While the asymmetric
decoder significantly impacts U-NetвҖҷs segmentation performance, our
proposed BMIS remains largely unchanged.

5.3. Study of skip connection
The classical U-Net [1] introduces the skip connection mechanism
between the encoder and decoder to mitigate the information loss
caused by frequent down-sampling. Since then, the skip connection
has almost become a standard configuration in modern segmentation
networks. This study explores the impact of skip connections on both
the classical U-Net and our proposed BMIS, and present pertinent
experimental results in Table 10. Analyzing the data in Table 10, we
observe that: (i) Removing the skip connections results in lower computational costs for both U-Net and BMIS; (ii) While U-NetвҖҷs segmentation
performance notably decreases without skip connections, our BMIS
remains largely unaffected.
Fig. 12 depicts the heat maps generated during BMISвҖҷs decoding

design, and in its original form. Upon careful examination, it becomes

BMIS is capable of maintaining segmentation performance while
using a decoder that is significantly simpler than the encoder, primarily due to its shallow yet wide network structure. This design
not only enables effective learning of high-level semantic features but
also markedly mitigates the loss of fine-grained details caused by frequent downsampling. Therefore, the decoder of BMIS primarily focuses
on spatial reconstruction rather than compensating for lost details,
significantly reducing its workload.

5.4. Study of the number of feature maps
Existing segmentation networks typically double the number of feature maps after each down-sampling, which enhances feature capacity
and learning capability, but also leads to increased computational costs.
This study explores the impact of varying feature map counts on the
proposed BMIS and presents the experimental results in Table 11.
The comparison between Setting I and Setting II underscores that
increasing the number of feature maps leads to enhanced segmentation
performance. Compared with Setting III, Setting IV incurs a higher
computational costs but does not improve segmentation performance.
This indicates that doubling the number of feature maps after each
downsampling step is not the most efficient approach. A further comparison between Setting III and Setting V reveals that once the number
of feature maps reaches a certain threshold, further increments do not
enhance segmentation performance but rather significantly increase
computational costs. This is mainly because redundant feature maps
can only capture approximate and overlapping representations, which
have limited impact on enhancing the modelвҖҷs learning capabilities.
Therefore, to strike a better balance between computational costs and
segmentation performance, we recommend adopting Setting 3 as the
preferred configuration.

5.6. Study of different input scales
This experiment examines the impact of input scale on both computational costs and segmentation performance, with results presented in
Table 13. As shown in the table, as the input scale gradually increases,
the computational cost of the models rises, accompanied by an improvement in segmentation performance. In addition, we observe that
input scale has a significant effect on the inference speed of U-Net and
CA-Net, while its influence on BMIS remains minimal. Notably, even
at an input scale of 512 Г— 512, BMIS maintains a low parameter count
(0.87M), FLOPs (8.85G), memory footprint (4060 MB), and inference
time (15 ms). This advantage of BMIS is particularly valuable for processing high-resolution medical images, which require both abundant
spatial information and extensive receptive fields.

By strategically integrating severe well-established modules, such as
asymmetric convolution, dilated convolution, and attention modules,
this paper proposed a novel 2D medical image segmentation backbone named BMIS. In contrast to conventional U-Nets, BMIS adopts
a shallower yet wider asymmetric structure and eliminates the need
for encoder-to-decoder skip connections. To verify the effectiveness of
the proposed BMIS, we compared it with twenty-nine state-of-the-art
methods across segmentation tasks involving five different data modalities. Extensive experimental results consistently demonstrate that the
proposed BMIS achieves a better balance between segmentation performance and computational costs through a minimalistic network
design.
Beyond its technical strengths, BMIS holds significant potential for
clinical applications by enabling real-time, on-device medical image
analysis across diverse healthcare settings. For example, when deployed
on handheld dermatoscopes such as the DermLite IV and FotoFinder
Vexia, BMIS could enable instant segmentation of skin lesions, reducing
reliance on cloud-based diagnostics while preserving patient privacy
and accelerating early melanoma detection. Similarly, when integrated
into portable ultrasound systems like Butterfly iQ+ and GE Logiq E10,
BMIS could automate breast lesion segmentation, assisting radiologists
in the early diagnosis of breast cancer with enhanced precision. Furthermore, BMIS could operate efficiently on mobile X-ray platforms like
Carestream DRX-Revolution Mobile and Nanox.ARC, delivering rapid
imaging analysis for lung abnormalities (e.g., pneumonia, tuberculosis,
and lung cancer) in emergency and primary care environments.
In summary, the proposed BMIS represents both a technical advancement in medical image segmentation and a valuable benchmark
model for clinical applications, particularly in resource-constrained
devices. Its compatibility with existing and emerging medical technologies positions it as a promising tool for improving global diagnostic
accuracy, accessibility, and healthcare equity.
