GSFANet: Global Spatial–Frequency Attention
Network for Infrared Small Target Detection
Chuiyi Deng , Zhuoyi Zhao , Xiang Xu , Member, IEEE, Yixin Xia, Junwei Li ,
and Antonio Plaza , Fellow, IEEE

Abstract—Infrared small target detection (IRSTD) has progressed significantly in spatial-domain learning. However, singleframe images’ limited spatial semantics impair discrimination
between targets and similar noise while complicating integrity
detection of large-scale target. To address these, we propose
global spatial–frequency attention network (GSFANet), which
enhances the distribution difference between targets and noise
from a frequency-domain perspective while preserving spatial
information integrity. The core innovations consist of three modules: 1) parametric wavelet downsampling (PWD), preserving
small target details during frequency refinement to prevent
feature fragmentation; 2) hierarchical gated kernel attention
(HGKA), capturing cross-level frequency relationships through
cross-channel kernel attention (C2 K) and maintaining spatial
coherence via cross-spatial gate attention (CSG), effectively
bridging semantic gaps across layers; and 3) adaptive frequencydecoupled fusion (AdaFD), dynamically fusing target-associated
frequency components while suppressing noise. We further
develop AdaFL Loss to balance multiscale target gradients and
stabilize training. Experiments on three benchmark datasets
demonstrate GSFANet’s superior detection performance and
enhanced segmentation robustness in complex scenarios compared with state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods. Our code will be
made public at https://github.com/dengfa02/GSFANet IRSTD
Index Terms—Cross-attention, infrared small target detection (IRSTD), spatial–frequency, transformer, wavelet transform
(WT).

single-frame images contain limited semantic information,
making this distinction challenging [19]. While deeper
network architectures with multiscale encoder–decoder frameworks [3], [23] offer one solution, they introduce excessive
branching that compromises inference speed.

In essence, the wavelet transform (WT) can be considered
as storing the spatial-domain contrast at multiple scales and
orientations. With these contrasts, IRSTD in the frequency
domain is statistically equivalent to searching for templates
that best capture the differences between target and background. Such contrast information can be expressed more
directly and efficiently in the frequency domain. From the
perspective of sparse signal, target information is more concentrated and thus more easily detectable in the frequency domain.
Furthermore, frequency-domain analysis naturally provides a
global view, capturing both global statistical properties and
local abrupt variations in the signal, which is theoretically
advantageous for detecting sparsely distributed small targets.

target T 1 , and even high-frequency combined approaches

In contrast, our approach incorporates WT for progressive
frequency refinement with adaptive high/low-frequency decoupling. By leveraging learnable frequency-domain features,
most background interference and noise can be effectively
suppressed. We chose WT over DFT because it preserves
crucial spatial–local structures during frequency decomposition. Unlike DFT, which eliminates local spatial relationships
and remains dependent on input dimensions, WT enables
precise multilevel operations and effective spatial–frequency
information fusion capabilities essential for capturing small
target features in complex scenes.
2) Kernel Function: Kernel functions in machine learning
transform nonlinear problems into linear ones by computing
dot products in high-dimensional feature spaces with reduced
computational cost. Building on this idea, traditional crossattention mechanisms essentially extract similarity coefficients
from mapped features for weighted aggregation. Recognizing
similarities between kernel functions and traditional crossattention mechanisms, we substitute attention with kernel
functions to avoid explicit feature mapping while enhancing
high-dimensional nonlinear relationship capture (detailed in
Section III-C).
3) Feature Integrity: Conventional downsampling methods
present significant limitations: Maxpooling [10], [23] often
confuses nonmaximum target edges with background, while
strided convolution (SC) [21], though capable of learning
some small-target features, struggles with extremely small
targets, resulting in fragmented representations. Alternative
approaches use lossless information transformation techniques
through Haar WTs [33], [34] or pixel slicing methods in the
spatial domain [19], [35], but single-domain downsampling
inadequately addresses the dual challenge of enhancing crucial
target structures while suppressing background noise. Our
approach overcomes these limitations through complementary
spatial–frequency-domain processing.
B. Contributions
To address the challenges mentioned above, we propose global spatial–frequency attention network (GSFANet),
which decomposes spatial information into multilevel frequency components during encoding, progressively enhancing
frequency resolution while reducing spatial resolution to construct a semantic pyramid. Global frequency attention and
unified spatial attention mitigate encoder–decoder semantic
gaps across all the levels, while an adaptive frequency decoder
integrates adjacent levels for segmentation. Our main contributions are as follows.
1) We introduce a novel spatial–frequency joint learning framework that amplifies distributional differences
between small targets and noise, enabling more accurate
target recognition.
2) We propose a parametric wavelet downsampling (PWD)
module that preserves critical spatial details of small
targets through approximate spatial slicing, seamlessly
integrating into the existing backbone architectures.
3) We design a global spatial–frequency attention mechanism based on kernel functions and gating mechanisms,

II. R ELATED W ORKS
A. Attention-Based IRSTD Methods
IRSTD models have evolved beyond CNN-based networks
[10], [36], [37] by incorporating attention mechanisms that
establish global patch associations, broadly categorized as narrow and generalized approaches. Narrow attention mechanisms
primarily use transformer-based architectures such as Swin
Transformer [38], TCI-Former [28], and FTC-Net [39]. These
approaches generally rely on CNNs to capture local features
while incorporating transformers to model global contextual
information. Advanced hierarchical structures in IRSTFormer
[40], RKFormer [41], SCTransNet [23], and MTU-Net [3]
leverage multiscale feature encoding and top–down feature aggregation to enhance detection performance through
improved multilevel interactions. Generalized attention mechanisms use convolutional or gating techniques to emphasize
spatial or channelwise information. For instance, ISNet [20]
extracts precise target shape information through Taylor finite
difference (TFD)-inspired edge blocks and two-orientation
attention aggregation (TOAA), incorporating high-frequency
joint learning. Similarly, IAANet [42] and MLP-Net [26]
implement coarse-to-fine internal attention, modeling crosslayer associations between high-level semantics and low-level
details. RDIAN [43] emphasizes spatial gradients in various
directions using a multidirectional difference approach, which
essentially acts as a form of spatial attention.
Unlike previous works, we improve conventional attention
mechanisms through the use of kernel functions, establishing
frequency associations between all the levels of encoders
and decoders with low computational complexity. Meanwhile,
we apply a gating mechanism to modulate global spatial
representations, enabling efficient construction of global crosschannel and cross-layer semantic representations. This ensures
the correct propagation of deep semantic features to low-level
decoders, effectively reducing false alarms while maintaining
low computational costs.
B. Deep Learning in Frequency Domain
While the existing approaches predominantly use spatialdomain features, frequency-domain analysis offers more

5007017

discriminative perspectives for IRSTD. WT facilitates multiscale frequency feature extraction by decomposing signals
into different frequency components. Recent networks have
integrated WT into neural architectures: Liu et al. [34] and
Alaba and Ball [44] implement WT for downsampling in
modified U-Net structures with inverse WT for upsampling,
while Finder et al. [45] embed multilevel WT into convolutional layers to expand receptive fields without increasing
parameters. In IRSTD, Zhu et al. [31] enhance discrimination
between targets and background by applying fast Fourier
transform to feature map patches, dividing and enhancing
high/low-frequency features. However, their nonoverlapping
patch approach compromises global frequency correlation
modeling and target representation continuity. FreqODEs [46]
use frequency-domain neural ordinary differential equations
with frequency interaction attention to improve target-noise
discrimination, introducing high-precision ODE solvers to
suppress interference. However, this network lacks adaptive frequency selection capabilities for IRSTD. Both the
approaches lack official implementation code and redefine test
sets, preventing fair comparative evaluation.
Current frequency-based IRSTD methods remain underdeveloped, lacking sufficient hierarchical frequency feature
interaction and adaptive frequency selection. Our method
addresses these limitations by preserving spatial details during
frequency-based downsampling and adaptively selecting relevant frequencies at high spatial resolution during decoding,
significantly enhancing detection performance.

As discussed in Section I-A.1, both Fourier and WTs
naturally serve as explicit regional contrast operators, projecting local image content onto a set of basis functions that
emphasize spatial differences (i.e., templates). This property
is advantageous for IRSTD, where targets are typically manifested as subtle, sparse regional contrasts within complex,
noisy backgrounds. Conventional Haar WTs, while powerful,
are limited in that their basis functions and decompositions
are fixed thus lacking adaptivity to the diverse frequency

C. Hierarchical Gated Kernel Attention
To effectively fuse frequency and semantic information
across all the network levels, spatial transformers (STs) [3],
[48] and channelwise transformers (CTs) [49], [50] serve
as natural global mixers. However, using either approach in
isolation results in insufficient interaction. While advanced
architectures such as SCTransNet [23] and MetaFormer
[51] incorporate joint spatial–channel interactions, they rely
exclusively on spatial interactions between adjacent levels, potentially compromising small targets’ global semantic
consistency. Furthermore, traditional attention mechanisms’
computational demands present significant challenges for
IRSTD applications. To address these issues the following are
put forth.
1) Based on the natural similarity between kernel functions
and attention mechanisms, we integrate Gaussian kernel functions into the attention framework, enhancing nonlinear feature
capture while reducing computation.
2) Recognizing that STs fundamentally establish interpatch relationships, we transform these into patch-based gated
attention mechanisms that maintain small target semantic
consistency across all the levels while dramatically reducing
computational costs.

The resulting HGKA effectively bridges the gap between
STs and CTs with lower computational complexity, while
ensuring comprehensive global fusion of frequency and spatial
information throughout the network.

the dataset splits defined by [10] for SIRST and NUDTSIRST (50% for training) and by [20] for IRSTD-1k (80%
for training), respecting the split standards established by the
dataset creators. In Table I, “Tr” denotes the ratio of the

2) Settings: We used a U-Net architecture with four-level
skip connections as the detection backbone [47]. Configuration parameters included: three downsampling levels, encoder
channel numbers of 16, 32, 64, and 128, a basic patch size
of 32, two HGKA blocks, and an FFN channel expansion
factor of 2.66 [23]. To preserve the original image sizes, inputs
for the SIRST and NUDT-SIRST datasets were cropped to
256 × 256, and to 512 × 512 for IRSTD-1k. To mitigate
overfitting and enhance robustness to camera tilt, random
flipping and small-angle rotation (±3◦ ) augmentations were
applied during training. The Adam optimizer was used with
an initial learning rate of 0.001, a batch size of 8, and 1500
training epochs. Learning rate was decayed to 10−5 using
cosine annealing. Following [10] and [23], the prediction
threshold for evaluation metrics was fixed at 0.5. GSFANet
was trained in PyTorch on a single Nvidia RTX 4090 GPU,
an Intel Xeon Platinum 8352S CPU, and a Linux server with
512-GB RAM. For comparison experiments, traditional methods are implemented following the settings in [16], [22], and
[47], while learning-based methods are trained using their original parameter configurations. Most of the adopted methods are

V. C ONCLUSION
This article introduces the GSFANet to address two fundamental challenges in IRSTD: distinguishing targets from
complex noise patterns and preventing fragmentation of
large-scale target features. Our frequency-domain analysis
reveals that this perspective significantly amplifies distributional differences between genuine targets and visually
similar noise. The PWD achieves precise frequency decomposition while preserving detail features critical for detection.
The lightweight HGKA module captures intrinsic frequency
correlations and globally consistent semantic relationships,
enabling effective cross-level feature interaction without the
computational burden of traditional transformer architectures. Finally, the AdaFD accurately integrates target-related
frequency components through dynamic filter generation,
effectively suppressing background interference. Coupled with
the gradient adaptive balancing mechanism of AdaFL Loss,
GSFANet learns multiscale target features more stably during
training, greatly improving detection accuracy and adaptability
in complex scenarios.
GSFANet still has limitations: recognition errors persist
in some complex single-frame scenarios, and computational
complexity requires further reduction for practical deployment.
Future work will explore temporal–spatial–frequency fusion
mechanisms and implement optimization techniques including
pruning and distillation to enhance deployment efficiency.
