ABSTRACT
Reconstructing images from under-sampled Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) signals significantly reduces
scan time and improves clinical practice. However, Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based methods,
while demonstrating great performance in MRI reconstruction, may face limitations due to their restricted
receptive field (RF), hindering the capture of global features. This is particularly crucial for reconstruction,
as aliasing artifacts are distributed globally. Recent advancements in Vision Transformers have further
emphasized the significance of a large RF. In this study, we proposed a novel global Fourier Convolution Block
(FCB) with whole image RF and low computational complexity by transforming the regular spatial domain
convolutions into frequency domain. Visualizations of the effective RF and trained kernels demonstrated that
FCB improves the RF of reconstruction models in practice. The proposed FCB was evaluated on four popular
CNN architectures using brain and knee MRI datasets. Models with FCB achieved superior PSNR and SSIM
than baseline models and exhibited more details and texture recovery. The code is publicly available at
https://github.com/Haozhoong/FCB.

1. Introduction
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a widely employed imaging
modality for clinical diagnosis. However, the clinical application of
MRI is impeded by its slow acquisition speed caused prolonged scan
time and possible motion artifacts. Acquiring under-sampled data in
the Fourier acquisition space, known as k-space, is a common approach to shorten the MRI scan time (Ravishankar and Bresler, 2010).
However, acquiring fewer data points results in a violation of the
Nyquist sampling criterion, leading to inevitable aliasing artifacts in
the reconstructed images (Shannon, 1948).
Removing aliasing artifacts in under-sampled MRI scans presents a
challenging inverse problem that requires incorporation of additional
prior information. Compressed sensing (CS) technique (Lustig et al.,
2007) utilizes the sparsity prior in predefined transforms and iterative optimization algorithms to address aliasing. However, CS requires
manual selection of sparsity types and other parameters, and it often
exhibits poor performance at higher under-sampling rates (Zbontar
et al., 2018).
In recent years, deep learning (DL) based models have shown remarkable performance in MRI reconstruction. These models take advantages of capabilities of deep neural networks to recover images
and exploit the prior knowledge obtained from the learning process

of data, surpassing traditional CS algorithms in terms of reconstruction quality and speed. There are two main approaches to utilizing
DL models in MRI reconstruction. The first approach utilizes end-toend models (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2018; Han et al.,
2019; El-Rewaidy et al., 2020), which are trained in a paired manner
using under-sampled images and corresponding fully-sampled images
without relying on other physical priors of MRI. The second approach
involves unrolling existing iterative algorithms for reconstruction and
replacing part of the iterations with neural networks (Sun et al., 2016;
Zhang and Ghanem, 2018; Xiang et al., 2021; Hammernik et al., 2018;
Schlemper et al., 2017; Sandino et al., 2020; Aggarwal et al., 2018).
In this way, complex parameter selection of iterative algorithms can
be optimized by learning, and the neural network serves as a more
powerful regularization term.
In the realm of CS and DL based MRI reconstruction, undersampling
patterns are typically random or uniform (Sriram et al., 2020b), rather
than truncated as in super-resolution tasks (Chen et al., 2018). The
resulting aliasing artifacts exhibit a long-range distribution in the image
domain, requiring reconstruction models with a large receptive field
(RF) to effectively remove these artifacts. However, most DL based MRI
reconstruction models are based on Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), which have limited RF because of the local spatial convolutions

Recently, Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as a powerful alternative (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021),
and outperform traditional CNNs by with stacked 3 × 3 convolutions
utilizing multi-head self-attention to capture global information. Other
approaches have explored CNNs with larger kernels (Liu et al., 2022b;
Ding et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Deng et al., 2023), achieving

is considered a key factor in the success of these methods. However,
both large kernel CNNs and ViTs face challenges such as extensive
computational requirements and training difficulties (Liu et al., 2022a).
In this study, we propose the Fourier Convolution Block (FCB), a
Plug-and-Play global operator that can be used in CNNs for MRI reconstruction. The term ‘Plug-and-Play’ used here is for indicating that FCB
is a standalone convolution block, distinct from the pretrained modelbased PnP iteration algorithm (Ahmad et al., 2020). FCB transforms
standard spatial domain convolutions into the frequency domain to
provide an extensive RF, leveraging the equivalence of spatial discrete
domain convolution to element-wise multiplication in the frequency
domain. FCB achieves a global RF, which matches the image size
and can be adaptively learned, while needing less computational requirement than large kernel convolutions. To reduce the parameters
of FCB, the regular convolutions are modified to depth separable convolutions (Howard et al., 2017), inspired by the design of Sun et al.
(2023). This modification enables the Fourier Convolution’s application
in the modern CNN with deep architecture. To further overcome the
challenge in train networks with large RF (Liu et al., 2022a), a novel
re-parameterization strategy for training networks with FCB was also
proposed for better capturing both local and global information.
Thus, the main contributions of this work are:
1. Focusing on scaling up the RF of CNNs for MRI reconstruction,
a Plug-and-Play Fourier operator FCB was proposed to replace local
spatial convolutions. The results demonstrated that the proposed FCB
could enlarge the ERF of CNNs and provide better reconstruction
quality than ViTs and large-kernel CNNs with large RFs while costing
less computing time.
2. We integrated FCB into a depth separable structure and proposed
a novel re-parameterization strategy for training, enabling the network
better fuse the local and global information. The two improvements
pave the way for the efficient training of Fourier Convolution within a
deep architecture.

2. Related work

2.3. Fourier convolution

2.1. DL-based MRI reconstruction

Fourier convolution was initially proposed for accelerating convolutional operations due to its runtime efficiency (Mathieu et al.,
2013). Spectral representation of CNNs using Fourier convolution was
introduced in Rippel et al. (2015) and Ayat et al. (2019), where Fourier
convolution serves as the spectral parametrization of convolutional
layers. However, due to the large number of parameters in the Fourier
convolution kernel, networks employing this approach lacked the depth
seen in modern CNN architectures. To address this limitation, Wood
and Larson (2021) parameterized the Fourier convolution kernel using
given functions, allowing for a smaller number of trainable parameters
to represent a Fourier convolution layer. However, this parametric approach still incurred high computational memory costs during forward
and backward computations. We initially employ Fourier convolution
within MRI reconstruction networks to capture large-scale correlations in aliased MR images. In our proposed approach, we integrate
FCB with depth-wise convolution (Howard et al., 2017), significantly
minimizing parameters and memory requirements. Furthermore, our
re-parameterization method helps improve the convergence of Fourier
convolution. As we know, our work represents the first instance of
utilizing Fourier convolution in deep CNNs rather than in toy models.

2.2. ViT and large-kernel CNN
Recently, Vision Transformers have been shown to outperform
CNNs in various visual tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
2021; Liu et al., 2021). ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) split the input image into patches and embeds them into vectors with fixed
scale. Multi-head self-attention was then applied to establish longrange dependencies within the image. Swin Transformer (Liu et al.,
2021) proposed a hierarchical structure similar to CNNs with shifted
window-based self-attention, and has shown significant performance
gains over previous state-of-the-art CNNs in various vision tasks. Recent
studies (Lin and Heckel, 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023;
Guo et al., 2023) have also utilized ViTs for MRI reconstruction, leveraging the long-range dependence of ViTs to improve the reconstruction
performance.
Inspired by the long-range dependence in ViTs, CNN designs with
large kernel sizes have been explored, showing promising results in
achieving comparable performance with ViTs (Liu et al., 2022b; Ding
et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Deng et al., 2023). For instance, the
kernel size in ConvNext (Liu et al., 2022b) was increased from 3 × 3
to 7 × 7 to match the size of the local window in Swin Transformer
and Yan et al. (2024) proposed a ConvNeXt-based network for selfsupervised MC MRI reconstruction. Further, Ding et al. (2022) scaled
the normal convolution to 31 × 31 using large depth-wise convolution,
achieving comparable or better performance than Swin Transformer.
Multi-branch convolutions with different kernel sizes (Liu et al., 2022a)
were introduced and fused using softmax attention to adaptively adjust
the receptive field size. Furthermore, Deng et al. (2023) shifted the sampling locations of convolutions in different channels to capture a global
RF, and demonstrated improved results in semantic segmentation tasks.

MRI reconstruction is a specific image recovery task, and DL-based
models have been widely used for this purpose. End-to-End Model
learns a direct mapping from aliased images to fully sampled images. UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), with its multi-scale structure
and skipping connections, is one of the most popular architectures
for MRI reconstruction and has been shown to outperform CS methods (Zbontar et al., 2018) at different acceleration ratios. Some studies
have used separate networks for magnitude and phase reconstruction,
with the dual-frame U-Net designed to avoid high-frequency signal
loss (Lee et al., 2018). Complex-valued Networks have also been explored based on the complex nature of MRI signal (El-Rewaidy et al.,
2020; Cole et al., 2021; Duan and Ren, 2024). Besides, some works
have incorporated perceptual loss or generative adversarial network
(GAN) framework to improve the image quality of the reconstructed
MRI (Yang et al., 2017; Mardani et al., 2018; Quan et al., 2018).

model-based network is designed as an unrolled form of the various

4.2. Baselines and evaluation strategies
The performance of UNet with the proposed FCB (F-UNet), E2EVar
with FCB (F-E2EVar), MoDL with FCB (F-MoDL) and VSNet with
FCB (F-VSNet), as well as baseline UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015),
E2EVar (Sriram et al., 2020a), MoDL (Aggarwal et al., 2018) and
VSNet (Duan et al., 2019) models, were evaluated and compared.
Additionally, three ViT models for MRI reconstruction were compared
including ViT (Lin and Heckel, 2022), ReconFormer (Guo et al., 2023)
and SwinMR (Huang et al., 2022). Methods utilizing k-space data
including Ksp (Han et al., 2019) and MDRecon (Ran et al., 2020) for
global dependency were also considered. Furthermore, the Fast Fourier
Convolution (FFC) method (Chi et al., 2020) employed 1 × 1 convolution and nonlinear layer in the Fourier domain to achieve a global
receptive field. We compared the FasterFC block (Liu et al., 2024b,a),
adapted from FFC for MRI reconstruction in the UNet architecture
(FasterFC-UNet), with the FCB.
The performance of FCB was evaluated on the previously described
brain and knee datasets at 8× and 12× acceleration rate, using a
variable density Poisson under-sampling pattern (Lustig et al., 2008).
The size of the Autocalibration Signal (ACS) was set to 20 × 20, and
the polynomial order of variable density was set to 4. Additionally,
the experiments were extended to include Cartesian and radial undersampling patterns. Models’ performance under extra noise was also
assessed. Gaussian noise with a standard deviation equivalent to 10%
and 20% of the signal intensity was introduced into the under-sampled
k-space data. Peak Signal to Noise Ratio (PSNR) and Structural Similarity (SSIM) on the validation set were used for performance evaluation,
computed on the magnitude image of the reconstruction results compared to the ‘‘ground truth’’ images reconstructed with fully-sampled
k-space. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test was utilized to evaluate the
improvements in the two metrics. To ensure a fair comparison between
End-to-End and model-based methods, the ground truth images were
combined using the sensitive maps from ESPIRiT (Roy and Kailath,
1989).

To ensure a strict equivalence between FCB and local convolution in the
first training phase, circular convolution was used, which also agrees
with the naturally wrapped MRI signal (Sandino et al., 2020).
4. Experiments
4.1. Data preparation
In this study, brain and knee data of FastMRI dataset (Zbontar et al.,
2018) were used to test the performance of the proposed FCB. For
brain experiment, 250 volumes of T1, T2, T2 fluid-attenuated inversion
recovery (FLAIR) and post-contrast T1 brain data (40, 50, 135, 25
respectively) were included. Among them, 200 volumes (3154 slices)
were used for training and 50 (794 slices) for validation with the same
composition rate of contrasts. For knee experiment, 118 volumes of PD
weighted knee data were involved, and 100 (2963 slices) were used
for training, 18 (524 slices) for validation. All the images were centercropped to 320 × 320, and the maximum magnitude of each volume
was normalized to 1. Because the number of coils of brain dataset was
not identical, coil compression was performed by keeping the first 𝑛𝑐
columns singular vectors of singular value decomposition (SVD) (Klema
and Laub, 1980). In this study, 𝑛𝑐 was set to 8. The sensitive maps were
estimated from the center k-space region by using ESPIRiT (Roy and
Kailath, 1989).

To explore the layer-wise RF of the proposed FCB, the convolution
kernels of UNet and F-UNet were visualized (Fig. 8) by showing the
spectrum of the kernel with the maximum norm in each layer. For the
baseline UNet, the spectral visualization map appeared very smooth,
and the distribution varies across different layers. In contrast, for FUNet (FCB was deployed in the last 6 layers), the spectral map of
FCB kernel exhibited more sharp features and sparsely focused on the
zero frequency, which was consistent with the distribution of real kspace data. Furthermore, to illustrate the size in spatial domain of
trained FCB kernel, the rank of the trained kernels’ spectrum in both
models were calculated and showed in Fig. 8 (the white numbers).
Due to the orthogonality of Fourier Transform, the rank is consistent
between convolution kernel and its spectrum (Proakis, 2007), so the
rank of spectrum is a lower bound of kernel’s size in spatial domain, as
described in Eq. (6). The rank of FCB kernels could reach high values,
such as 143, while the ranks of normal convolutions are typically
lower than 3. In terms of layer-wise dimension, it is evident that FCB
significantly scaled up the size of convolution and, namely, the RF.

4.6. Analysis of FCB
As a Plug-and-Play block, FCB can be utilized in any convolutional
layer of CNN models. Here, the impact of implementing FCB in different
layers was further explored. Fig. 7 shows the PSNR results of F-UNet,
F-MoDL and F-VSNet in the knee dataset. The 𝑥-axis represents the
number of convolution layers that were replaced by FCB from the last,
with 0 indicating the baseline model without FCB and 1 indicating only
the last layer being replaced by FCB. The results for different FCB layers
were very close, and using just one FCB layer significantly improved
the reconstruction performance. As a single FCB also has a global RF,
as referred in Section 3.1, this phenomenon suggests that only replacing
the last layer with FCB is capable of incorporating global information
into the reconstruction process, aligning with the findings in Rao et al.
(2022).

5. Conclusion
This paper introduced a novel convolution block design with a
global receptive field for MRI reconstruction CNNs. The experimental results showed that the proposed FCB effectively improved the
reconstruction performance of the baseline CNN models. At different
undersampling rates, models enhanced with FCB achieved better quantitative metrics in various datasets, even with additional noise added.
Furthermore, these models exhibited superior capability in recovering
intricate details, including texture and edges. Notably, the models with
FCB outperformed Vision Transformers, which are considered mainstream models with large receptive fields. Additionally, FCB models
also surpassed methods that embed k-space data to enhance long-range
connections. FCB also demonstrated low computational complexity,
with experiments showing that its runtime is significantly less than Vision Transformers and comparable to traditional CNNs with an 11 × 11
convolution kernel.
Through visualization, it is demonstrated that the proposed FCB
effectively scales up the RF of CNNs. Unlike other approaches that
focus on the architecture design of CNNs, our approach pays attention
to the basic convolution layer in CNNs. Some other works use cascaded or pyramidal architectures (Schlemper et al., 2017; Chen et al.,
2022; Sriram et al., 2020a) to capture long-distance correlation. Our
proposed FCB serves as a Plug-and-Play block that has the potential to
be incorporated into these various CNN architectures. Moreover, FCB
processes data in the hidden layer as real values and utilizes conjugate
symmetry in the frequency domain to save memory and time. For
methods building complex CNNs (Wang et al., 2020; Cole et al., 2021),
FCB could also be smoothly integrated in complex mode.
The proposed FCB approach still has some limitations. Although
its usage memory is comparable to spatial convolution, the number
of parameters in FCB is still significantly higher, leading to increased
storage memory costs. This presents a tradeoff between the memory
