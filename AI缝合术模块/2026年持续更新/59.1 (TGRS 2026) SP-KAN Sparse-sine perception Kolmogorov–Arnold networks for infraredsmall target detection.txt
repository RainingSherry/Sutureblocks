ABSTRACT
Infrared small target detection (IRSTD) plays a critical role in diverse complex remote sensing scenarios.
However, existing IRSTD methods struggle to discriminate dim targets that are heavily entangled with complex
interference due to their fixed activation representations. To tackle this issue, we reformulate IRSTD as a
global context modulation problem driven by sparse nonlinear modules and propose a Sparse-sine Perception
Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (SP-KAN). It marks a novel attempt to leverage the superior nonlinear capability
of the Kolmogorov–Arnold theory for robust IRSTD. Specifically, a compressed vision transformer encoder is
first employed to capture long-range spatial dependencies, while the proposed pattern complementarity module
(PCM) constructs their essential nonlinear interactions. The PCM unifies channel-wise mappings of tokenized
representations with local spatial saliency of structured features, enhancing target–background discrimination
via multi-dimensional and multi-intensity nonlinear embedding. Within the PCM, a sparse-sine perception
Kolmogorov–Arnold layer (SPKAL) is introduced to perceive the original nonlinear space and a sparse gridbased high-dimensional sinusoidal latent space at the pixel level, enabling fine-grained interactions among
neurons and aligning with the inherent sparsity of small targets. Extensive experiments across four datasets
demonstrate that SP-KAN consistently surpasses state-of-the-art IRSTD methods in accuracy, robustness, and
generalization, verifying its superior capability in sparse nonlinear modeling. Code will be available at the
author’s homepage https://github.com/xdFai.

1. Introduction
Single-frame infrared small target detection (IRSTD) has been widely
applied in high-dynamic scenarios such as low-altitude surveillance
(Zhang et al., 2025a), early warning systems (Li et al., 2023a), and
high-precision navigation (Sun et al., 2023). However, owing to the
thermal radiation imaging mechanism of infrared systems and the
requirement for remote sensing, infrared small targets typically exhibit
low contrast, sparse pixels, and blurred contours (Zhu et al., 2024;
Yang et al., 2025a; Ding et al., 2025). In addition, various interferences,
e.g., sea glare, cloud edges, and urban structures, often generate targetlike artifacts, further complicating target separation. Therefore, reliably
extracting faint targets from strongly cluttered backgrounds remains a
long-standing and fundamental challenge (Kou et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
2025; Yuan et al., 2025a).
Conventional IRSTD methods primarily include image filtering (Bai
and Zhou, 2010; Zhu et al., 2020a), human visual system-inspired

contrast mechanism (Chen et al., 2014; Han et al., 2019), and lowrank representation (Cao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2025; Wu et al.,
2026a), which are built upon prior scene analyses to construct handcrafted processing pipelines. Nonetheless, relying on empirical tuning and specific priors limits their robustness in real-world environments (Xiong et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025a). Deep learning technologies have opened new avenues for addressing these limitations,
especially convolutional neural networks (CNNs)-based segmentation
paradigms have garnered significant attention for their ability to capture contours (Wang et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2021b;
Yang et al., 2025b). These U-shaped structures have been continuously
refined through asymmetric attention-guided feature fusion (Dai et al.,
2021a), enhancement of encoders and decoders (Wu et al., 2023a),
dense nested skip-based progressive interaction (Li et al., 2023b), and
shape-prior edge modeling (Zhang et al., 2022). Despite achieving

While CNN-, transformer-, and Mamba-based IRSTD methods have
achieved steady progress, as shown in Fig. 1(a), most of them still
rely on the learnable-linear and fixed-activation multilayer perceptron (MLP) paradigm for the computation within neural units (Ning
et al., 2025). In IRSTD, the image-to-mask transformation involves
highly nonlinear and nonstationary interactions among intensity, spatial structure, and contextual cues, which are difficult to adequately
capture using conventional MLPs with limited functional expressiveness. To address this limitation, Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs)
have recently emerged as a promising alternative for representation
learning (Liu et al., 2024b). Rooted in the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem (KART), KAN models multivariate functions as
superpositions of learnable univariate nonlinear functions (Azam and
Akhtar, 2024). By parameterizing these functions with spline-based
basis representations, KAN explicitly models nonlinear mapping curves,
as illustrated in Fig. 1(b). This flexible function modeling mechanism is
well suited to the localized and nonlinear saliency patterns of infrared
small targets, motivating us to incorporate KAN to enhance nonlinear
representation capacity in IRSTD.
The distinctive properties of infrared targets and backgrounds pose
three challenges for introducing KANs: (1) Form-unknown nonlinear embedding: Existing image processing studies have integrated
KANs into a convolution layer (Bodner et al., 2024), transformer key–
matrix mappings (Wu et al., 2024a), and mixed convolution KANs (Li
et al., 2025a) for nonlinear representation. However, these strategies
lack specific modulation of target saliency and background continuity,
making it hard to achieve a compromise between missed detections
and false alarms. (2) Imbalanced pattern perception: Unlike general
visual targets with abundant structural information, infrared small
targets possess extremely limited features but exhibit strong local contrast (Han et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2013). KAN primarily emphasizes

channel-wise nonlinear learning in unstructured dimensions, compromising spatial perception and target shape prediction. (3) Positionslocked function summation: In deep models, pooling reduces spatial
resolution, making clues of sparse small targets less perceptible in
high-level features (Li et al., 2023b). As highlighted by red in Fig.
1(b), KANs perform node summations along spline curves with fixed
positions, causing limited sensitivity to fine-grained inter- and intranode nonlinear mappings. This drawback leads to inaccurate target
localization.
To address these issues, this study proposes a sparse-sine perception Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (SP-KAN) for infrared small target
detection. To fully exploit the nonlinear potential, our method adopts
a U-shaped transformer–KAN hybrid architecture, leveraging KAN for
fine-grained modulation of the global context built by the transformer
to suppress both false alarms and missed detections. Following this
premise, a compressed vision transformer (CViT) encoder is first employed to capture long-range spatial dependencies and establish clear
target saliency and background continuity. Subsequently, the pattern
complementarity module (PCM) facilitates essential nonlinear interactions in the bottleneck, ensuring that the learnable spline functions can
respond rapidly to the global context. To balance pattern perception,
the PCM unifies element-wise mappings with local spatial saliency
captured by depth-wise convolutions on structured features. This multidimensional and multi-intensity nonlinear stimulation promotes more
coherent structural representations and clearer contour delineation for
IR small targets. In PCM, a sparse-sine perception Kolmogorov–Arnold
layer (SPKAL) is further designed to unlock the positional summation
constraint inherent in vanilla KAN. As illustrated in Fig. 1(c), SPKAL
replaces the fixed position-wise summation of independent univariate
mappings with a sparse grid-based sinusoidal latent space, enabling
adaptive and position-aware nonlinear interactions at the pixel level.
By relaxing the rigid positional correspondence of the original KAN,
this design facilitates fine-grained neuron interactions and naturally
aligns with the Gaussian-like distribution and sparsity characteristics of
infrared small targets (Zhu et al., 2020b), thereby improving detection
accuracy.

2.2. Hybrid-architecture IRSTD methods
To overcome the locality bias of CNNs, vision transformers and
Mamba have been introduced to IRSTD. Liu et al. (2023) first integrate
a multi-head self-attention mechanism and a feature enhancement
module into a compound encoder to efficiently highlight discriminative target features. Meng et al. (2023) utilize central difference
convolution to capture local gradient features and apply criss-cross
multi-attention to aggregate broader contextual information. MTUNet employs multi-level encoded transformers to aggregate contextual
information across-scale spatial patches (Wu et al., 2023b). Considering
the continuity of background semantics, Yuan et al. (2024a) present
the channel-wise cross transformer on the long-range skip connections
to establish channel-wise semantic patterns across all levels with an
acceptable computational overhead. To enhance long sequence modeling and avoid quadratic computational complexity, Mamba has also
been applied to IRSTD tasks. For example, MiM-ISTD (Chen et al.,
2024) proposes a nested Mamba model that effectively establishes
both global and local information through visual vocabulary features.
IRMamba (Zhang et al., 2025b) integrates the intensity and directional
information of pixel differences to enhance target detail representation and suppress background interference. To structure heterogeneous
backgrounds via perspective transformation, Li et al. (2025c) adopt
the spatial–spectral mamba interactive learning to enrich the semantic representations of IR small targets. Based on the deep unfolding
framework, LSNet (Li et al., 2026) reformulates the decomposition
process as an implicit prior regularization model, in which generalized sparse transformation and singular value thresholding are jointly
learned through a State Space Model.
Unlike previous approaches that focus on either local or global
dependence for spatial or channel with fixed activations, this study centers on nonlinear modeling, introducing Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks
into IRSTD to advance nonlinear feature representation.

2. Related work
2.1. CNN-based IRSTD methods
Unlike conventional handcrafted feature extraction schemes, CNNs
exploit locally learnable kernels to flexibly adjust channel responses,
thereby enriching the semantic expression for target detection. A widely
adopted strategy is cross-layer fusion, which effectively preserves the
weak target cues during the aggregation of encoder and decoder (Dai
et al., 2021a; Zhang et al., 2023). Furthermore, densely nested fusion
has been designed to progressively interact and refine multi-level
features, alleviating target detail loss caused by pooling operations (Li
et al., 2023b; He et al., 2022). To further cope with the scale diversity
inherent in small targets, multiscale aggregation strategies have also
been explored to strengthen the descriptive capacity of low-resolution
feature maps (Dai et al., 2021b). Beyond customized feature integration
structures, embedding target-specific prior knowledge into CNNs has
also proven effective (Zhang et al., 2025d). Zhang et al. (2022) apply
the Taylor finite difference scheme to capture intricate edge characteristics, aiming to amplify the grayscale contrast. Lin et al. (2024) utilize
central difference convolutions to form a contrast-shape encoder and
introduce an edge-inspired, shape-reconstructable decoder to handle
dim textures and diverse target shapes. More recently, Xiong et al.
(2025) propose a deep unfolding framework that integrates robust
principal component analysis (RPCA) (Wu et al., 2024b) priors with
a dynamic parameter generation mechanism, significantly enhancing
model interpretability.
Despite the strong performance of CNN-based approaches, their
inherent inductive biases constrain the explicit modeling of longrange contextual dependencies. In contrast, our architecture integrates
transformer-based long-range dependencies and performs nonlinear
modulation tailored for IRSTD.

2.3. Kolmogorov–Arnold networks in image processing
To implement nonlinear representation learning, Liu et al. (2024b)
introduce Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs), a novel architecture
composed of stacked Kolmogorov–Arnold layers equipped with learnable one-dimensional activation functions, offering remarkable adaptability and interpretability (Zhang et al., 2025c). Since their introduction, KANs have rapidly gained traction in diverse vision tasks,
including image segmentation (Wu et al., 2024a), reconstruction (Ge
et al., 2025), and enhancement (Ning et al., 2025). For instance, Azam

4.1. Overall pipeline
As shown in Fig. 3(a), SP-KAN adopts a U-shaped architecture
consisting of three parts: compressed ViT-based encoders, a KAN-based
neck, and CNN-based decoders. The compressed ViT-based encoder
consists of four encoding stages with progressively decreasing spatial
resolutions and increasing channel-wise dimensions, naturally enabling
hierarchical multi-scale feature extraction. Each encoding stage is composed of two consecutive convolution-batch normalization-ReLU (CBR)
units, followed by one compressed vision transformer (CViT) layer and
a max-pooling layer. By employing CViT layers, the encoder achieves a
lightweight design that minimizes memory overhead while retaining
sufficient global contextual information for bottleneck modulation.
The pattern complementarity module (PCM) is deployed at the bottleneck of the network and serves as the core component for sparse
nonlinear modulation. To maintain computational efficiency, PCM is
applied only at the two deepest stages. To alleviate pattern forgetting, the encoder features processed by the CViT layers are directly
concatenated with the corresponding decoder features along the channel dimension via skip connections, without introducing any complex
cross-layer feature fusion strategies. Symmetric to the encoder scales,
the CNN-based decoder consists of four decoding stages with progressively increasing spatial resolutions and decreasing channel-wise
dimensions. Each decoder stage comprises two standard CBR units
and one nearest-neighbor interpolation layer for upsampling. The first
CBR is used to compress the concatenated encoder–decoder features
along the channel dimension, thereby reducing semantic redundancy.

5.1.4. Implementation details
The base channel width of SP-KAN is set to 16, the number of SPKAL
in PCM to 3, the grid size of SPKAL to 12, and both the height and
width of the compressed keys and values to 16. To avoid losing target
information due to downsampling during the preprocessing stage, all
training images are uniformly resized to 512 × 512. The specific image
normalization, cropping, and enhancement follow the preprocessing
strategy in Zhang et al. (2024). Model parameters are initialized using
the Kaiming scheme. The network is optimized with Adam, starting
from an initial learning rate of 0.001, which is gradually reduced to
1 × 10−5 via a cosine annealing schedule. The batch size and number of
epochs are set to 8 and 1000, respectively. Following (Wu et al., 2023a;
Li et al., 2023b), the segmentation threshold for the salient map is fixed
at 0.5. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and conducted on
a workstation equipped with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU, an
Intel Core i7-12700KF CPU, and 32 GB of memory.

5.6. Generalization of SP-KAN
Despite the proposed method demonstrating strong robustness
across various noise types, real-world applications inevitably expose the
model to diverse and unpredictable conditions. To assess its generalization capability, we further evaluate the detection performance of the
proposed method under previously unseen scenarios. Specifically, six
representative high-performance IRSTD methods including DNANet (Li

on the unseen SIRST V1 dataset. The quantitative results are presented
in Table 8, which show that SP-KAN achieves the best performance
in both contour prediction and target localization metrics in unseen
scenarios, demonstrating its strong scene generalization and practical
applicability.

5.9. Limitations
Although the proposed method effectively handles challenging scenarios where targets exhibit characteristics similar to background clutter via nonlinear embedding, it still has certain limitations. Case One
in Fig. 21 illustrates a performance degradation in terms of IoU when
the target size becomes excessively large. For example, the target in
Fig. 21(a) contains 1065 pixels, which no longer conforms to the definition of an infrared small target. In such cases, the proposed method
tends to respond preferentially to locally bright sub-regions within the
oversized target (see the blue pixel regions in Fig. 21(b) and (c)),
resulting in incomplete contour prediction. Future work may alleviate
this issue by enhancing contour modeling through the incorporation
of more explicit edge representations. Case Two in Fig. 21 presents
representative false-alarm scenarios. As shown in Fig. 21(d) and (e),
blind-pixel clusters are mistakenly detected as small targets, whereas
in Fig. 21(f), the false alarm arises from incomplete segmentation of
the true target. To mitigate such errors, future research will investigate
noise-injection strategies to further improve robustness under these
challenging conditions.

5.8. Core hyperparameter analysis
We utilize the number of SPKALs in PCM, the number of grids of
SPKAL, and the basic width of the model to validate the core hyperparameters of SP-KAN. As illustrated in Fig. 20(a), increasing the number
of SPKALs yields a steady improvement in detection performance, while
the model size remains almost unchanged. The performance saturates
when the number of layers reaches three, reaffirming the effectiveness
of SPKAL. In Fig. 20(b), when the number of grids is set within the
range of 12 to 16, the model achieves consistently strong performance,
indicating that maintaining an appropriate number of control points
is essential for preserving the capability of KAN. Additionally, we

6. Conclusion

Acknowledgments

In this study, we reformulated infrared small target detection as a
global context modulation problem driven by sparse nonlinear modules and presented a novel Sparse-sine Perception Kolmogorov–Arnold
Network (SP-KAN), tailoring the nonlinear modeling capability of KAN
to the characteristics of IRSTD. By integrating the Kolmogorov–Arnold
theory with a U-shaped transformer-based architecture, SP-KAN effectively enhanced nonlinear feature interactions and improved the
separation between weak targets and complex backgrounds. The proposed PCM established element-wise unstructured dependencies and
local saliency relations through multi-dimensional and multi-intensity
nonlinear embedding, while SPKAL unlocked position-summation constraints to enable fine-grained pixel-level modulation. Extensive experiments on four public datasets demonstrated that SP-KAN achieved
state-of-the-art performance, highlighting its strong sparse nonlinear
modeling potential for infrared small target detection. Further studies can explore the interpretability of KAN-based models to better
understand how nonlinear mappings contribute to target–background
discrimination. Additionally, designing noise-aware structures and integrating them with the nonlinear modeling capability of KAN is expected
to further enhance IRSTD performance under strong noise interference.
