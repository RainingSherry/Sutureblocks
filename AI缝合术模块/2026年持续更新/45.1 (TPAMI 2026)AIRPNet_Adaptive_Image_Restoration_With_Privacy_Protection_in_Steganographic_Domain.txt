AIRPNet: Adaptive Image Restoration with
Privacy Protection in Steganographic Domain
Fangyuan Gao, Student Member, IEEE, Chao Gao, Student Member, IEEE, Xin Deng, Member, IEEE,
Chenxiao Zhang, Student Member, IEEE, Junjie Huang, Member, IEEE,
and Mai Xu, Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—Cloud-based third-party multimedia services have become increasingly popular in last decade, however, they pose serious
threats to users’ privacy. To address this issue, in this paper, we propose a novel Adaptive Image Restoration network with Privacy
protection, namely AIRPNet, which first attempts to perform image restoration in steganographic domain. Compared with existing
methods, our method has significant advantages in invisibility, security and flexibility. Specifically, we first propose a wavelet
lifting-based Adaptive Invertible Hiding (AIH) module to conceal the low-quality (LQ) secret image into a stego image. Then, instead of
performing single type of restoration on the secret image, an adaptive secure restoration (ASR) module is developed to deal with
multiple image degradations on the stego image. Finally, a high-quality (HQ) secret image can be extracted from the restored stego
image. Here, since the secret image remains hidden throughout the whole image restoration process, the privacy of users can be
greatly protected. The framework can be flexibly extended to multiple image restoration, which can restore multiple secret images from
the same stego image. Experimental results on various datasets demonstrate that our AIRPNet outperforms existing methods in terms
of restoration accuracy, invisibility and security on different image restoration tasks. The source code is available at
https://github.com/gclonghorn/AIRPNet.git
Index Terms—Privacy protection, image restoration, wavelet lifting.

2.1

Privacy-Preserving Image Processing

In recent years, with the increasingly serious privacy disclosure in cloud environment, many methods have been
proposed for privacy-preserving image processing. To protect privacy, most of these methods focus on the operation
in encrypted domain [2], [3], [4], [7], [8], [9]. Shamir Secret
Sharing (SSS) [10] is a common method for encrypted data
processing. Specifically, the authors in [2] introduced an
SSS-based image denoising method over the cloud, but it
only supported integer subtraction and addition operations.
Another study in [7] presented a pre-processing method
that allowed real number addition/scalar multiplication in
the encrypted domain, but it was limited to multiplication
operations with terminating decimals. A solution to support
arithmetic division operations for nonterminating decimals
on image shares was proposed in [8], but it required
non-colluding servers and with weak security. To address
these limitations, Ziad et al. [3] introduced a library that
enabled various operations, such as edge sharpening and
spatial filtering, on encrypted images using homomorphic
encryption. Moreover, Zheng et al. [9] proposed a novel
privacy-preserving image denoising method from external
cloud databases. With the secure computation protocol for
similar patch searching, it enabled the encrypted databases
to provide secure image denoising services.

Wavelet Lifting In Computer Vision Tasks

The lifting scheme was originally introduced in [11] as
a technique to construct biorthogonal wavelets. Recently,
the wavelet lifting scheme has been combined with deep
networks, with well applications in various computer vision
tasks [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. For example, Li et al. [12]
first proposed to reconstruct a reversible block based on
the lifting scheme, and applied it in image compression
and compressive sensing. Due to the perfect reconstruction
property of wavelets, the reversible blocks are demonstrated
to have strong stability and robustness. Based on these
good characteristics, Huang et al. [13] proposed a waveletinspired invertible network to facilitate blind noise removal
in images. Since the lifting scheme is information lossless,
Ma et al. [14] combined wavelet lifting with deep compression network to perform lossless image compression. Li et al.
[15] proposed a novel approach for reference-based image
super-resolution, which leverages wavelet transformation
for efficient texture matching. Rodriguez et al. [16] combined
the lifting scheme and CNNs to learn features for texture
classification, with limited number of trainable parameters.
Inspired by wavelet lifting, the invertible neural networks (INNs) were proposed for memory saving and lossless transformation. With good reversibility, the INNs have
made significant progress in many low-level computer vision tasks [17], [18], [19], [20]. For example, Xiao et al. [17]
explored the use of INN for image rescaling by learning
an invertible mapping between low and high-resolution
images. Lugmayr et al. [18] introduced a normalizing flowbased approach using INN for image super-resolution, aiming to address the ill-posed nature of super-resolution and
generate photo-realistic high-resolution images. For image
compression, Xie et al. [19] proposed an invertible encoding
network with INN to largely mitigate the information loss
during the compression process. Liu et al. [21] presented
a method for real image denoising using invertible networks. It converts the noisy image into a low-resolution
clean image and a latent representation with noise. In the
backward process, the noisy representation is replaced with
a new prior distribution to produce clean images. In this
paper, our proposed method also benefits from the favorable
characteristics of wavelet lifting, such as the multi-scale representations and reversibility, to achieve privacy-preserving
image restoration in steganographic domain.

M OTIVATION

To achieve privacy-preserving image restoration, it is of
significant importance to find a suitable steganographic
domain to operate on. Typically, the ideal steganographic
domain should meet two requirements: 1) the manipulation
in the steganographic domain should be able to sensitively
affect the results in the target restoration domain, and 2)
the influence on the target restoration domain should be
consistent and stable. Regarding this issue, we have an important finding that the stego image generated by invertible
network can satisfy the above two requirements and acts as
a good steganographic domain. More specifically, when image concealing and revealing are reversibly modelled, the quality
change of stego image can sensitively and stably affect the quality
of recovered secret image. Therefore, by manipulating the stego
image reasonably, we can achieve the restoration of the
secret image while maintaining its privacy. This interesting
finding lays as a great foundation for our network design.
Analysis: In order to explore how the quality change
of stego image influences the recovered secret image, we
design the two experiments as shown in Fig. 2. The experiments are carried out on DIV2K [42] testing dataset with
100 images at resolution 1024 × 1024, and we randomly
split the dataset into 50 secret and 50 cover images. The
Gaussian noise with standard deviation as 25 was added to
the secret images. Then, we conceal the whole noisy secret
image into cover image using the invertible image hiding
network HiNet [32]. Note that HiNet was a trained model
which is fixed during the whole experiment. As expected,
the generated stego images are also with noise.
In the first experiment, as denoted by the blue line in
Fig. 2, we adopt three image denoising methods including
DnCNN [22], CBDNet [23] and RIDNet [24] to remove the
noise in the stego images, resulting in denoised stego images
with different quality levels. The Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio
(PSNR) and Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) [43] are used
to measure the quality of the denoised stego images. As
shown in Fig. 3 (a) and (b), when the denoised stego image
has higher quality, the quality of the recovered secret image
also increases. Specifically, when the quality of stego image
is improved from 28.18 dB to 28.56 dB by DnCNN, the quality of the recovered secret image is improved from 21.54 dB
to 25.18 dB. The 0.38 dB improvement in stego image leads
to 3.64 dB improvement in the recovered secret image. This
indicates that the recovered secret image is very sensitive

on the quality of stego image. For more comprehensive
demonstration, we carried out the second experiment as
denoted by the red dotted line in Fig. 2. We degrade the
quality of stego images by adding different levels of Gaussian noise, i.e., σ = 5, 10 and 15. The results are shown in
Fig. 3 (c) and (d). As can be seen, when the quality of stego
images decreases, the quality of recovered secret images gets
worse correspondingly. These results demonstrate that the
quality change of stego image has consistent influences on
the quality of recovered secret image. This finding holds
for different cover images and secret images of varying
complexities, as shown in the supplementary materials.
From the above two experiments, we can conclude that
the stego image is indeed a good steganographic domain
to perform privacy-preserving image restoration. Here, the
invertible network plays an important role in closely relating steganographic domain and restoration domain. Due to
the reversibility of invertible network, the steganographic
domain can have a stable and consistent influence on the
restoration domain, which brings significant benefits to
privacy-preserving image restoration.

4.1

Framework

In this section, we introduce a novel Adaptive Image
Restoration network with Privacy Protection, namely AIRPNet, towards confidential image restoration in the steganographic domain. As shown in Fig. 4, the overall framework
of AIRPNet consists of two key components: the wavelet
lifting-based Adaptive Invertible Hiding (AIH) module and
the Adaptive Secure Restoration (ASR) module. To ensure privacy protection, the AIH module conceals the lowquality (LQ) secret image xsecret within a cover image
xcover , generating a stego image xstego . This stego image,
which contains the information of the LQ secret image, is
visually indistinguishable from the original cover image,
ensuring the invisibility and security of the hidden content.
Importantly, the AIH module is highly versatile, capable of
hiding LQ images suffering from various degradations, such
as noise, blur, low resolution, or their combinations.
The stego image xstego is then passed to the ASR module
for secure restoration. The ASR module consists of K scales
of wavelet lifting-based secure restoration sub-networks,
which performs restoration on the stego image xstego in
a coarse-to-fine manner. In the ASR module, we design a
Task Adaptive Restoration (TAR) block to effectively address various degradations and their combinations. After
processing through the ASR module, the restored stego
image x′stego is obtained, which contains the information
of the restored high-quality (HQ) secret image. Importantly,
x′stego is designed to be visually indistinguishable from the
original cover image xcover to ensure security. Finally, the
restored stego image x′stego is sent back to the AIH module,
where the HQ secret image x′secret can be revealed. Table
2 presents the main notations used in this paper, where the
lowercase letters represent the original image and uppercase letters represent the wavelet sub-bands after discrete
wavelet transform (DWT).
4.2

The Adaptive Invertible Hiding (AIH) Module

As demonstrated by our finding in Section 3, the invertible
network is able to closely relate the steganographic domain

and restoration domain, which is important for the privacypreserving image restoration. Thus, we design a wavelet
lifting based Adaptive Invertible Hiding (AIH) module to
hide different types of degraded images into the cover
image. As can be seen in Fig. 4, the AIH module has a
reversible architecture. In the forward process, it performs
image concealing to hide the secret image into the stego
image, while in the backward process, it performs image
revealing to recover the secret image from the stego image.
These two processes share the same network architecture
and the same network parameters, only with different information flow directions. The details of the AIH module
are introduced as follows.
In the forward concealing process, the inputs to the AIH
module are the degraded secret image xsecret and the cover
image xcover . They are firstly decomposed into wavelet subbands Xsecret and Xcover through DWT. After that, the
wavelet sub-bands are fed into a sequence of wavelet lifting
blocks. These blocks generate the stego sub-bands Xstego ,
together with the side information R. The R contains the
lost information in the forward concealing process, which
is important to keep the reversibility of the AIH module.
The resulting Xstego and R then undergo inverse wavelet
transform (IWT) to generate the stego image xstego and the
corresponding r , respectively. The overall forward process
of AIH can be formulated as follows,

Why Wavelet Lifting is Adopted in Our Network? In
this paper, we propose a wavelet lifting-based invertible
network for image concealing and revealing. There are
three reasons behind our choice. (1) As demonstrated in
[32], the wavelet domain is more appropriate for image
concealing than pixel domain, especially the high-frequency
sub-bands. The wavelet lifting scheme is originally designed for wavelet construction, which can naturally provide the wavelet manipulation domain. (2) The wavelet
lifting scheme has a perfect reconstruction property [44],
which can ensure the reversibility of the hiding process.
This is very important for the stability in confidential
image restoration, as demonstrated in Section 3. (3) Our
work focuses on the restoration of degraded images, and
the key of it is to restore the lost high-frequency details.
The wavelet lifting can naturally split the low and highfrequency details, which brings great convenience to the
image restoration task.

Task Adaptive Restoration (TAR) block. The TAR block
plays an important role in the ASR module, which is
designed to be adaptive to different image degradations,
such as noise, blur and low-resolution, together with their
mixed combinations. Fig. 5 shows the structure of the TAR
block, which is built upon a U-Net style encoder-decoder
architecture. The encoder first extracts features from the
input and passes them through a series of task adaptive
(TA) blocks. Then, the decoder reconstructs the fine details
for image restoration. The TA block is designed with the
attention mechanism. Specifically, each degradation has its
own processing unit, such as denoising unit, deblurring
unit and super-resolution unit. For simplicity, these units all
adopt the NAFBlock [45]. To deal the mixed distortion, we
design a degradation aware (DA) unit to predict the weight
for each degradation, with the structure shown in Fig. 5.
The weighted outputs are then concatenated and processed
by a pointwise convolution layer, mixing the channels for
further refinement. This adaptive mechanism enables the
TAR block to dynamically adjust to different types of image
degradation, effectively managing various restoration tasks
through a unified structure.
Low-frequency Preservation (LP) block . The LP block
is specifically designed to restore the low-frequency information. While the multi-level wavelet lifting scheme enables the recursive recovery of rich details, some structure
information remains concealed in low-frequency sub-band.
Extracting and restoring this valuable structure information
is essential for preserving the image fidelity and improving
restoration accuracy. To achieve this, we develop the LP
block which is composed of an attention unit and a trunk
unit. As shown in Fig. 6, the attention unit is adopted to
generate an attention map, which indicates the concealed
secret information in the low-frequency domain. The attention map is then concatenated with the features from the
trunk unit to guide the restoration of the low-frequency
information of secret image. With the LP block, we can have
better preservation of the secret image’s structural features,
which contributes to the overall restoration performance.

E XPERIMENT

5.1

Experimental Settings

Datasets and settings. We adopt the DIV2K [42] training
dataset to train our AIRPNet. The testing datasets include
DIV2K [42] with 100 images at resolution 1024 × 1024,
and COCO [48] with 5,000 images at resolution 256 ×
256. Note that we directly use the model trained by the
DIV2K dataset to test on COCO dataset without re-training
and fine-tuning. The image restoration tasks involve image
denoising, image deblurring, image super-resolution and
mixed distortion removal. During the training process, we
use Gaussian noise with standard deviation from 0 and 55 to
generate noisy secret images, and Gaussian blurring kernel
with size ranging from 3 to 25 to generate blurred secret
images. To produce low-resolution images, we apply bicubic
downsampling to high-resolution images with 2× and 4×
scaling factors. In the case of mixed distortion removal,
the setup involves a combination of Gaussian noise with
a standard deviation of σ = 10, Gaussian blur with a kernel
size of 9, and a 2× downsampling operation.
The number of wavelet lifting blocks in the AIH module
and ASR module is 12 and 4, respectively. The number of
scales in the ASR module is set as K = 3. For the loss
function, the λC , λR and λF in Eq. (13) are set to 1.0, 1.0 and
0.25, respectively. The λS and λP in Eq. (18) are set to 1.0 and
0.005, respectively. The λM in Eq. (21) and Eq. (22) is set to
7.0. The network was trained on a single NVIDIA GeForce
RTX 4090 GPU for 500K iterations, requiring approximately
56 hours. We used the Adam optimizer with an initial
learning rate of 1 × 10−4 and a batch size of 16.
Comparison methods. To the best of our knowledge, we
are the first to explore privacy-preserving image restoration
in steganographic domain. There exists no method that can
be directly compared with ours. Thus, for comparison, we
create some new benchmarks by combining the state-of-theart (SOTA) image hiding methods with image restoration
methods to form a pipeline similar to our AIRPNet. Specifically, for image hiding, we adopt HiDDeN [49], Baluja [51],
DeepMIH [20] and EUIN-Net [52] as the baseline methods.
Here, the original HiDDeN method can only hide messages.
We slightly modified its input and output dimensions and
re-trained the network, to make it able to hide images.
Regarding the image restoration methods, we adopt the
NAFNet [45] and MIRNetv2 [50] methods for denoising,
NAFNet [45] and XYDeblur [54] methods for deblurring,
and RLFN [55] and FMEN [56] methods for image superresolution. In addition, we include the latest DeepSN-Net
[53] method for all these restoration tasks. Given the lack
of methods to address mixed distortion, following [57], we

9

construct a pipeline by combining NAFNet for denoising
and deblurring, and RLFN/FMEN for super-resolution. For
fair comparison, we train these pipelines using the same
dataset as ours, and the training process is consistent with
our multi-stage training strategy. The conference version
PIRNet is also compared in the experiments to show the
improvements of our AIRPNet.
Evaluation metrics. To evaluate the image restoration
performance of our method, we adopt three metrics to
measure the quality of the restored secret images. The metrics include PSNR, SSIM [43] and the Learned Perceptual
Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) [58]. The PSNR and SSIM are
widely used objective metrics for evaluating image quality.
The larger value of PSNR and SSIM indicates higher image
quality. LPIPS is a perceptual metric that quantifies the
similarity between two images based on human perceptual
judgment. A lower LPIPS value indicates a higher perceptual similarity between the original and restored images.
Lightweight version. To effectively utilize the computing resource, it is crucial to develop a lightweight model
for privacy-preserving image restoration in conditions with
limited computing resource. Towards this goal, we introduce AIRPNet-Light, a lightweight variant of our AIRPNet
designed for high-efficient image processing. In AIRPNetLight, the number of wavelet lifting blocks is set to 2 both
in the AIH module and ASR module. The number of scales
in the ASR module is K = 1. Additionally, the LP block is
not included. We evaluate its performance using the same
metrics as AIRPNet on the DIV2K and COCO datasets.

Ablation Study

Effectiveness of the reversibility of AIH module. In the
AIH module, we model image revealing and concealing
as two invertible processes which share the same network
parameters. To verify the effectiveness of this reversibility
scheme on the privacy-preserving restoration, we trained
another network in which the concealing and revealing processes are independent from each other, i.e., they have two
different sets of network parameters. The results are shown
in Table 5. As can be seen, without the reversibility constraint, the restoration performance significantly drops for
all the three tasks. The reason behind is that the reversibility plays important roles to keep the stability between
steganographic domain and restoration domain. Once the
reversibility is broken, the relationship between the two
domains becomes volatile and inconsistent, which leads to
the decrease in restoration accuracy.
Effectiveness of LP block. In our AIRPNet, we introduce
a low-frequency preservation (LP) block to preserve the lowfrequency structure of secret images. To evaluate its effectiveness, we trained an alternative network in which the LP
block is removed. Table 6 presents the restoration results
with and without the LP block. As can be seen, there exists
a decline in restoration performance for all three restoration
tasks when LP block is removed. Specifically, the PSNR
value is decreased by 1.0 dB, 2.33 dB, 0.35 dB and 0.86 dB for
image denoising, deblurring, super-resolution and mixed
distortion removal tasks, respectively. Similar trend can be
seen in SSIM and LPIPS metrics. These results demonstrate
the essential role of LP block in image restoration tasks.
Effectiveness of DA unit. In our task adaptive retoration
(TAR) block, we employ a degradation aware (DA) unit for
analyzing complicated distortion. To prove the effectiveness

of the DA unit, we trained an additional network with only
one operation unit in each TAR block with no DA unit. Table
7 illustrates the restoration results with and without DA
unit. As can be seen, the restoration performance falls across
all tasks when the DA unit is removed. The PSNR value has
a deterioration of 0.47 dB, 0.89 dB, 0.08 dB and 0.38 dB for
the aforementioned tasks respectively. Same situation occurs
in SSIM and LPIPS metrics. These results indicate that our
DA unit can adaptively rearrange the weight among three
distinct restoration operations to accommodate multiple
complex degradation scenarios.
5.4

Complexity Analysis

We measure the model complexity of our AIRPNet and
other comparison methods based on the number of network
parameters, FLOPs, and inference time. A larger value of
these indicators indicates higher model complexity. Table
8 presents the complexity results of our and comparison
methods. Here, the FLOPs were calculated using a standard
input size of 3 × 256 × 256, while inference time was
measured on high-resolution images of 3×1024×1024 (consistent with the DIV2K testing protocol) on a single NVIDIA
GeForce RTX 4090 GPU. As shown, our AIRPNet-Light has
the smallest number of network parameters at just 1.77M,
saving over 55% compared to the second-best method,
HiDDeN+MIRNetV2. Furthermore, it has the lowest FLOPs
value at only 22.84G, achieving a reduction of more than
45% compared to the second-best method, PIRNet. The time
consumption for our AIRPNet-Light and AIRPNet is 79
ms and 208 ms, respectively, for a high-resolution image
at resolution 3 × 1024 × 1024, which can be acceptable for
practical applications.

5.5

Security Analysis

Security against statistical steganalysis. The statistical analysis is performed on StegExpose [62] which is a combination
of statistical attacks like sample pairs analysis [65], RS analysis [66], chi squared attack [67], and primary sets [68]. Fig.
11 shows the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves
via StegExpose of our method and the comparison methods.
The black dashed line indicates the ideal scenario with
the area under the ROC curve (AUC) as 0.5, i.e., random
guessing. Thus, the AUC value closer to 0.5 indicates higher
security. The AUC value of our AIRPNet is 0.577 in Fig. 11,
which is closer to 0.5 than comparison methods, indicating
that our stego images are more challenging to be detected.
Security against machine learning-based steganalysis. To evaluate the resistance of AIRPNet against mod-

Fig. 12. ROC curves: true positive rate vs. false positive rate using SRM
[63] on stego images for denoising task on DIV2K dataset.

ern feature-based steganalysis, we conducted experiments
using the Spatial Rich Model (SRM) [63] to discriminate
between cover and stego images. The SRM employs a highdimensional feature extractor designed to capture subtle
embedding artifacts, which aids in effective classification.
The corresponding ROC curves are shown in Fig. 12. As
can be seen, our AIRPNet achieves the lowest AUC value
of 0.773 among all comparison methods, indicating its high
security against such attacks.
Security against CNN-based steganalysis. We adopt
three representative steganalysers including YeNet [59], SRNet [60] and SiaStegNet [61], to distinguish cover and stego
images. These steganalysers are retrained from scratch as
binary classifiers. The training set is formed by generating
400 cover–stego pairs from DIV2K training set for eight
methods (including AIRPNet). The test set consists of 100
images (50 cover and 50 stego) from DIV2K validation set.
This configuration ensures a 1:1 class ratio in both training
and testing phases to prevent classification bias. All models
are trained with Adam (lr = 1 × 10−4 ) for about 100 epochs
until convergence. Table 9 presents the detection accuracy
results using the three CNN-based steganalysis methods. As
can be seen, our method yields detection accuracy closest
to 50% (random guess) among all comparison methods,
indicating a higher level of security.
Security against adversarial leakage analysis. To evaluate the resistance of AIRPNet against secret leakage, we
conducted an adversarial leakage experiment. Specifically,
we formulated the attacker as an image-to-image translation
task, aiming to reconstruct the original secret image xsecret
given the denoised stego image x′stego . The Pix2Pix [64] is
adopted as the adversary restoration method, which was
trained for 200 epochs on DIV2K dataset. Fig. 13 shows the
reconstructed images by Pix2Pix from the stego images by
different methods. The more similar with the original secret
image indicates the larger leakage of secret information. As
can be seen, the competing methods leak the information
of the secret image, while our AIRPNet yields a visually
unrecognizable result with very little information leakage.

5.7

Robustness Evaluation over Noise Attack

To comprehensively evaluate the performance of our
method, we test the robustness of our AIRNet against typical noise attacks. Specifically, we added Gaussian noise to
the stego images at noise levels of σ = {5, 10, 15}. Then,
the restoration quality of secret images is evaluated with
two models. In the default model, the quality of secret
images is tested with the original model without any noisy
contamination, while in the fine-tuned model, the quality is
tested with the model finetuned with the noisy samples. As
shown in Table 11, the default model exhibits sensitivity to
transmission noise. Even at a low noise level of σ = 5, the

PSNR drops from 30.21dB to 14.25dB, and further degrades
to 8.19 dB at σ = 15. In contrast, the fine-tuned AIRPNet can
overcome this limitation. By incorporating noise-augmented
training, our AIRPNet achieves remarkable stability, with
a high PSNR of 28.07 dB under the noise attack σ = 15.
This indicates that with targeted fine-tuning, AIRPNet can
be well adapted to the noise attacks.
