ABSTRACT

ARTICLE HISTORY

Accurate welding defect detection is vital for industrial safety, but
traditional visual methods struggle with internal defect complexity.
While Phased Array Ultrasonic Testing (PAUT) can capture internal
defect features, deep learning-based detection faces issues like
noise interference, weak low-light features, and overlapping spec­
tra of grain boundary scattering and defects. This paper proposes
the ELTDF-Net intelligent detection model, which enhances defect
features via a triple innovation module: the Enhanced Channel
Calibration and Refinement Module (ECCR): Fusion of multi-scale
cavity convolution and dynamic channel calibration to enhance
local gradient response of millimetre-scale defects and solve the
problem of attention bias; the Luminance Control Residual Block
(LCRB): Constructing a luminance-noise decoupling framework,
suppressing frequency-domain aliasing noise through gated fea­
ture fusion and enhancing feature correlation in low-light areas;
and the Translation-Variant Convolutional Cross Attention (TVconvCA): Combines frequency domain analysis with spatial adaptive
convolution to enhance low-frequency morphological features of
defects and suppress high-frequency scattering noise. Top-1 accu­
racy: 86.36% on PAUT-Welds, 97.44% on NEU-CLS-64, 99.72% on
NEU-CLS and 84.97% on MT. Furthermore, a dedicated intelligent
classification software system was developed to validate the mod­
el’s engineering practicability and detection capability in industrial
scenarios. Our code is available at https://github.com/storm9967/
ELTDF-Net.

optical images, which leads to subjective bias of the traditional manual judgements [5].
In recent years, machine vision defect detection methods have been applied in many

promoted the development of defect detection but still face the following challenges:
Recognition bias due to model attention bias: The model is easily distracted by nontarget noise during feature extraction, resulting in the suppression of key defect features.

2. Related works
2.1. Detection of internal defects in welds
At present, there are two main methods for detecting internal defects in weld seams,
namely radiographic inspection and ultrasonic inspection [1]. Among them, radio­
graphic inspection requires special protective measures, is greatly affected by the sur­
rounding environmental conditions of the inspection site, involves higher inspection
costs and has a longer inspection cycle. Compared with radiographic inspection, ultra­
sonic inspection offers advantages such as flexibility and convenience in use, high safety,
low sensitivity to environmental conditions, high sensitivity to crack defects, and the
ability to accurately locate defect depth. Compared with conventional ultrasound, Phased

Array Ultrasonic Testing (PAUT) has achieved significant improvements in flexibility,
detection efficiency and detection sensitivity. Notably, it has made remarkable progress
in image-based, quantitative and intelligent ultrasonic detection [1]. Therefore, phased
array ultrasound has been rapidly developed and widely applied in the field of weld nondestructive testing. However, existing PAUT data analysis methods still face challenges:
first, PAUT generates large volumes of data, leading to inefficiency in traditional manual
interpretation and increased human errors [11]; second, acoustic scattering artefacts in
coarse-grained materials have a high degree of overlap with the time-frequency domain
features of real defects, which is difficult to effectively distinguish using existing con­
volutional neural networks (CNNs) [10]. Moreover, the detection of near-surface defects
remains a critical challenge in ultrasonic testing due to the interference of interface
echoes and the blind zone effect [12].
2.2. Deep learning based defect detection
CNNs have been widely used in visual defect detection tasks due to their advantages in
high-dimensional feature extraction. Li et al. [13] proposed LHA-Net, a lightweight highprecision network for pavement defect detection. Compared with YOLOv8n, LHA-Net
achieves superior performance through modular innovations, while having fewer para­
meters and lower computational cost, thus facilitating road structural health monitoring
at a reduced cost. Saeed et al. [14] proposed a novel hybrid framework that exploits the
local feature extraction capability of CNNs and the complex weight sharing feature of
CNNs, as well as the transformer’s ability to understand the larger context of spatial
relationships in large-scale patterns, making it applicable to dataset of different sizes. To
improve defect recognition, LightGBM [15] utilises the properties of the coefficient stack
autoencoder to demonstrate the unique qualities associated with BRB, an integration that
provides a more accurate and concise representation of inputs. Li et al. [16] proposed an
ODCL model that combines salient region decoupling (SRD) and supervised contrast
learning (SCL) by decoupling weld defects from background features in X-ray images
and using contrast learning to alleviate category confusion, innovatively adopting a twostage fine-tuning and data enhancement strategy, the decoupling idea is introduced into
small-sample industrial defect detection for the first time, which significantly improves
the recognition accuracy of weld defects in sample-less scenarios. Additionally, Cheng
et al. [17] developed a multiscale detection method incorporating an image adaptive
adjustment (IAA) module and a global-local semantic fusion mechanism, which adap­
tively enhances image quality and effectively captures features of variable-sized defects in
radiographic images. However, most CNN-based methods rely on standard convolu­
tions, which may struggle to capture weak gradient features in low-light or low-contrast
regions of PAUT images, leading to feature loss. Similarly, to address the difficulty of
distinguishing true defects from geometric indications in PAUT, Kim and Lee [18]
developed an automated evaluation algorithm. By combining A-scan signal learning
with domain-specific features, they effectively reduced false positives caused by geometry
signals. Recently, Han et al. [19] proposed DFW-YOLO for PAUT weld defect recogni­
tion, which integrates FasterNet and a weighted generalised feature pyramid network to
handle redundant features and small targets. Despite these advancements, three key
challenges remain in PAUT defect detection: (1) The model’s attention is easily distracted

2.3. Attention mechanisms
Guo et al. [20] integrated collaborative attention and Efficient Channel Attention
Network (ECA-Net) to make multiple modules collaborate with each other, thus increas­
ing the important features and improving the segmentation accuracy. Xiang et al. [21]
proposed frequency channel attention (FCA) enhanced visual transformer, which
replaces the traditional global average pooling by the frequency domain channel atten­
tion module (based on the DCT multiband spectral analysis) through; combined with
shifted linear layer (Shift Linear Layer) to reduce the computation volume by 33% and
achieve lightweight and high-precision fault diagnosis of bearing vibration signals. Liu
et al. [22] proposed a global attention module (GAM) enhanced steel defect detection
framework and through the hierarchical attention mechanism, the shallow layer adopts
the sparse axial attention AA to extract the local details and the deep layer adopts the
multi-head self-attention AA to extract local details and the deep layer adopts multi-head
self-attention MSA to model long-range dependencies, which is combined with cascade
fusion network (CFN) to optimise multi-scale feature fusion and significantly improve
the detection accuracy of unstructured defects. Inspired by these, we propose novel
attention modules that enhance feature representation while maintaining model
efficiency.

3. Proposed method
3.1. ELTDF-Net
The overall architecture of the ELTDF-Net method proposed in this paper is shown in
Figure 5, which mainly includes the Enhanced Channel Calibration and Refinement
Module, the Luminance Control Residual Block and the Translation-Variant
Convolutional Cross Attention. Specifically, firstly, the input phased array defect image
is passed through the ResNet50 backbone network for basic feature extraction to generate
a multi-scale feature map (X1-X4) and this hierarchical structure can effectively capture
the multilevel information of the image; at the same time, the Enhanced Channel
Calibration and Refinement Module is used in the process to enhance the model’s ability
to capture and characterise local key features. Then, the extracted feature map enters into
Feature Refinement Neck: firstly, it passes into the Luminance Control Residual Block to
adaptively filter the redundant features and strengthen the useful feature flow through
the gate control unit, and then the two features are summed up, and then the spatial
information is compressed through Global Average Pooling for spatial information
compression and integration; subsequently, it is passed to the Translation-Variant
Convolutional Cross Attention to adaptively fuse important features from different

frequency domains. After that, the refined features are fed into Classification Head,
which has multiple parallel classifiers (Classifier1, Classifier2, . . ., Classifierj), the output
of each classifier is processed by a corresponding Sigmoid function to obtain the final
prediction.

3.2. Enhanced Channel Calibration and Refinement module ECCR
Small targets are prone to lose key information in multiple downsampling, using
a parallel multibranching approach, where the task of each branch extracts features of
various scales and levels through a series of efficient channel attentions [23], this multi­
branching strategy helps to capture multiscale features of an object, thus improving the
accuracy of small target detection. Inspired by the above ideas, combined with the
problem that imbalanced attention tends to lose critical information, we propose an
Enhanced Channel Calibration and Refinement Module, which introduces multiple
depth-separable convolutional layers to capture a series of spatial features of sensory
field sizes, followed by adaptive feature augmentation using the attention mechanism.

3.4. Translation-Variant Convolutional Cross Attention TVconv-CA
In the field of image recognition, the two-dimensional discrete cosine transform con­
structs a multi-scale frequency domain representation by decomposing the image into
weighted combinations of different frequency cosine basis functions. Based on this
feature, researchers integrate multi-frequency analysis with attention mechanisms:
multi-level frequency modules regulate cross-band information flow, while dynamic
attention weights synchronise feature extraction from high-frequency details to lowfrequency contours [26]. Inspired by the above ideas, combined with the problem of
grain boundary scattering and the spectrum of defects [10], we designed a TranslationVariant Convolutional Cross Attention.

In image classification tasks, Top-1 Accuracy is a key metric for evaluating the
performance of a model, especially widely used in multi-class classification (e.g. 1000
classes in ImageNet), where Top-1 Accuracy is the accuracy with which the top-ranked
class matches the actual result.

Precision measures the model’s accuracy when it predicts the positive class. It’s
calculated as the proportion of predicted positive samples that are actually correct (TP)
out of all samples predicted as positive (TP + FP). A high precision means the model is
very confident and reliable in its positive predictions.

Recall measures how well the model identifies all actual positive samples. It is the
proportion of actual positive samples correctly predicted (TP) out of all existing positives
(TP + FN). High recall indicates the model is effective at capturing true positives and
minimising missed detections (missed FN).

15

F1-score is the harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. It balances these metrics,
providing a single measure of model performance that is only high when both Precision
(accuracy of positive predictions) and Recall (coverage of actual positives) are high. It
avoids the extremes of focusing solely on one metric.

4.4. Comparison experiments with state-of-the-art methods
To validate the effectiveness of ELTDF-Net, we compared it with the following
methods:
(1) Convmixer (Feature Interactive Convolution with Curriculum Learning for
Small Footprint and Noisy Far-Field Keyword Spotting) [29]: Constructing
a full convolutional layer cascade structure through deeply separable convolu­
tion, realising long-range feature interactions at low computational cost, proving
that pure convolutional networks are still competitive.
(2) Convnext v2 [30]: Introducing Global Response Normalisation (GRN) and
Expansion Attention mechanism to solve the feature collapse problem of
Convnext v1 and improve the small sample learning ability.
(3) Eva-02 (Evolutionary Visual Assistant-02) [31]: Fusion of Visual Transformer
and Masked Auto-Encoder (MAE) for efficient scaling of ultra-large scale visual
pre-training through hierarchical knowledge distillation.
(4) Hivit (Hierarchical Vision Transformer) [32]: Designing a hierarchical visual
Transformer architecture that balances local fine-grained features with global
semantic modelling through a dynamic sparse attention mechanism.
(5) Res2net [33]: Construct a multi-scale residual connection structure to achieve
progressive feature refinement within a single residual block, significantly
improving fine-grained recognition performance.
(6) Riformer [34]: Proposes a recursive iterative attention mechanism to optimise
the efficiency of Transformer’s long sequence modelling through parameter
sharing and gradient routing.
(7) Vig (Vision gnn) [35]: Modelling images as graph-structured data, aggregating
pixel-node relationships through graph neural networks, breaking through the
limitations of traditional grid structures for visual representation.
(8) Mff (Multi-level Feature Fusion) [36]: A multi-frequency band feature fusion
module was developed to decouple high-frequency details from low-frequency
semantic information in the frequency domain space to enhance the robustness
of the model to illumination changes.
(9) TransNeXt [37]: It is a biomimetic vision backbone network that integrates
aggregated attention and convolutional GLU, which can avoid deep degradation
and achieve optimal performance and robustness at the same scale in multi
vision tasks.
(10) SeaFormer (Squeeze Enhanced Axial Transformer) [38]: It is an efficient
Transformer model for mobile devices, which achieves a balance between high
precision and low latency in multi vision tasks through SEA modules, dual
branch architecture and multi-resolution distillation technology.

6. Conclusion
In this paper, we proposed the ELTDF-Net model to address the high misdetection rate
of manual judgement and the limited feature characterisation capability of traditional
machine learning models in industrial ultrasonic phased array welding defect detection.
Through a modular co-design incorporating the Enhanced Channel Calibration and
Refinement Module (ECCR), the Luminance Control Residual Block (LCRB), and the
Translation-Variant Convolutional Cross Attention (TVconv-CA), ELTDF-Net achieves
synergistic optimisation of multi-scale feature fusion and dynamic deformation sensing.
Experimental results on both public datasets and site phased array data demonstrate the
model’s significant advantages, achieving a Top-1 accuracy of 86.36% on a dataset
containing five types of typical welding defects. Furthermore, to bridge the gap between
theoretical research and engineering application, we have encapsulated the proposed
model into a user-friendly software system. This study not only provides an innovative
algorithmic solution but also offers a high-accuracy, high-efficiency and high-reliability
tool for the field of ultrasonic non-destructive testing.
