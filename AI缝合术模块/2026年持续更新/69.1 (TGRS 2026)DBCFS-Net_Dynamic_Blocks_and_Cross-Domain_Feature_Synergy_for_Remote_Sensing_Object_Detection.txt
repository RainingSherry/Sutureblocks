DBCFS-Net: Dynamic Blocks and Cross-Domain
Feature Synergy for Remote Sensing
Object Detection
Qingyao Lin , Yuhui Zheng , Member, IEEE, Le Sun , Senior Member, IEEE, Shihao Dong , Rugang Wang ,
and Feng Zhou
Abstract—The detection of objects in remote sensing images
represents a significant subtask in the domain of computer vision.
However, it poses some challenges: mitigating high-frequency
fine-grained feature loss, capturing efficient and discriminative object features, and improving separability between
complex backgrounds and objects. Therefore, we propose a
dynamic blocks and cross-domain feature synergy (DBCFS-Net)
for remote sensing object detection. First, we propose a dynamic
block downsampling module (DBDM). Its block partitioning
strategy and dynamic offsets overcome the limitations of rigid
downsampling that cause blurred features, while adaptively
focusing on critical target regions to preserve high-frequency
fine-grained details. Second, a cross-domain aware feature
enhancement module (CDAFEM) is introduced to enhance
the model’s adaptability to scale variations and complex
backgrounds by establishing consistency constraints through
cross-domain joint modeling, thereby preserving discriminative
feature information. Concurrently, it refines object boundaries
and edge structures through texture information optimization,
reinforcing edge continuity to improve the model’s generalization capability and robustness. Finally, a mamba-based
local–global feature co-promotion module (MB-LGFCPM) is
designed to guide the model to couple local details with global
contextual information, constructing a bidirectional ‘detailsemantic’ enhancement mechanism for feature representation.
This achieves the dynamic interaction and synergistic complementarity optimization between features. Extensive experimental
results validate the effectiveness of the proposed method, achieving mAP scores of 87.8%, 96.9%, and 96.6% on DIOR,
RSOD, and NWPU VHR-10 datasets—surpassing state-of-the-art
detectors.

I. I NTRODUCTION

W

ITH the advancement of optical remote sensing technology, the object detection in remote sensing imagery
has emerged as an active research area. This task aims to
identify both the categories and spatial distributions of specific
objects within remote sensing images. As a critical component
of remote sensing image analysis, it plays a crucial role in
domains including military reconnaissance [1], environmental conservation [2], agricultural monitoring [3], and traffic
management [4].
Nevertheless, compared with conventional scenarios, remote
sensing object detection remains challenging due to complex
scene environments, diverse object scales, and feature interference among densely distributed objects, as illustrated in
Fig. 1. Remote sensing images are typically acquired from
large field-of-view (FOV) observations, where objects occupy
only a minimal proportion of pixels in comparison with the
expansive and cluttered backgrounds [5], [6]. Also, persistent
challenges in remote sensing object detection arise from
insufficient feature representation due to low-resolution objects
and poor object–background contrast. Concurrently, the distinctive attributes inherent to remote sensing imagery remain
underexplored in optimizing feature extraction performance
[7]. In terms of downsampling, the currently widely used fixeddomain downsampling method cannot adapt to the geometric
shape of the target and is accompanied by irreversible loss of
spatial details. Fixed windows rigidly cut off the boundaries
of the target or destroy the structured information of irregular
targets, resulting in blurred boundaries and shape distortion,
which further exacerbates the loss of features of small targets
in remote sensing images.
Researchers have undertaken efforts from multiple perspectives to address these challenges. Data augmentation
techniques have been employed to enhance the feature robustness against scale-induced feature mismatch [8],
[9] [10]. Also, advances in deep learning methodologies
offer promising directions for tackling these challenges.
Liu et al. [11] introduced ABNet, which adaptively integrates channelwise and spatial-location information to acquire
more diverse discriminative features. This approach addresses
scale variation challenges by enhancing semantic richness.

Zhang et al. [12] introduced CoF-Net, a progressive network that incrementally refines primitive features toward
discriminative representations characterized by rich spectral
details and semantic dependencies. Ma et al. [13] proposed
AFSNet, which decomposes object features into distinct
channels to effectively circumvent interfeature interference
and enhance detection efficacy. Gao et al. [14] developed
AGMF-Net, which reinforces multiscale feature representations by enhancing high-level semantic information and
capturing salient global features while mitigating background
interference.Tong et al. enhanced small object detection performance by expanding receptive field ranges and integrating
spatial detail features with contextual semantic information
[15]. Li et al. [16] proposed TBNet, which enhances the
discriminability of textural features by exploring interpixel
relationships to accentuate discrepancies between objects and
backgrounds in remote sensing imagery. Although the aforementioned methods have achieved notable progress from
diverse perspectives, they overlook the significance of establishing long-range dependencies through feature affinity and
coupling local–global characteristics. These mechanisms play
crucial roles in distinguishing objects of interest from image
backgrounds. Furthermore, how to efficiently retain discriminative favorable features and mitigate high-frequency
irreversible information loss are also issues worthy of further
in-depth investigation.
In this article, a method is proposed to mitigate feature
loss and enhance feature perception through dynamic block
downsampling and cross-domain feature synergy, addressing
the challenges of complex background interference and suboptimal feature extraction in remote sensing imagery. The
principal contributions are summarized as follows.
1) A novel method called dynamic blocks and crossdomain feature synergy (DBCFS-Net) is proposed for
the object detection in remote sensing images. The
method forms a step-by-step, functionally coupled
feature optimization link by constructing a synergistic mechanism of “feature retention-cross-domain
enhancement-complementary fusion,” and provides an
effective solution for remote sensing target detection in
complex scenarios by utilizing this end-to-end synergistic design. Comprehensive evaluations were conducted
on multiple benchmark datasets (DIOR, RSOD, and

NWPU VHR-10). Experimental results demonstrate
that the proposed DBCFS-Net method achieves robust
detection performance across all three datasets, attaining
SOTA results.
2) The DBDM is introduced, which not only effectively mitigates feature loss during downsampling but
also addresses the inherent limitations of rigid modeling in conventional fixed-grid approaches, specifically
their neglect of spatial heterogeneity and geometric
transformations in objects.
3) The CDAFEM is proposed, in which a tri-stage
optimization mechanism for cross-domain synergy is
constructed through channel recalibration, spatial focusing, and frequency-domain enhancement. This approach
preserves discriminative information while suppressing
redundant noise. Concurrently, an edge-aware structure is designed to perform explicit edge modeling
by leveraging textural features of objects, thereby
mitigating indistinct object boundaries and enhancing
foreground–background separability.
4) The MB-LGFCPM is proposed to enhance longdistance positional interactions for global modeling
while preserving local detail features, thereby achieving
complementary information fusion.
The remaining structure of this article is organized as
follows. Section II provides a concise review of related
works. Section III presents the architecture of the proposed
DBCFS-Net methodology, followed by detailed elaborations on the structures of the DBDM, CDAFEM, and
MB-LGFCPM. Experimental results and corresponding analyses are given in Section IV. Concluding remarks are provided
in Section V.
II. R ELATE W ORKS
A. Deep Learning-Based Remote Sensing Image Detection
Unlike traditional methods reliant on handcrafted features
such as shape, texture, and fixed scales [17], [18], [19], the
advent of deep learning has enabled convolutional neural network (CNN)-based object detectors to adaptively extract image
features. These approaches preserve richer semantic characteristics while exhibiting enhanced robustness. Currently, deep
learning-based object detectors are mainly divided into twostage detectors [20], [21], single-stage detectors [22], [23], and
Transformer-based detectors [24], [25]. These methods have
driven the development and advancement of object detection
technology.
The application of these object detectors to remote sensing
imagery has led to significant progress, primarily through
architectural adaptations that address domain-specific characteristics. For instance, Li et al. [26] developed an adaptive
points assessment and assignment model for rotated object
detection, which demonstrated superior performance but was
limited by feature-instance misalignment when processing
oriented bounding boxes. Shao et al. [27] reduced interclass
feature interference by leveraging interclass similarity through
a cross-entropy loss, though the method incurred high computational costs due to extensive concatenation operations.

et al. [33]. Alternatively, Zhang et al. [34] expanded dilated
convolutions to expand the receptive field and guided feature
dependency by constructing global contextual relationships.

B. Dynamic Block Downsampling Module
Fixed-kernel downsampling in remote sensing object detection often causes irreversible loss of spatial details and blurred
object boundaries, which severely limits the detection performance. To mitigate this issue, we propose a DBDM, as
illustrated in Fig. 3, which effectively preserves structural
information while remaining computationally efficient. The
module first partitions the input feature map into four regions
along the horizontal and vertical axes for separate processing.
This strategy helps retain fine-grained details within local
blocks. A dynamic sampling grid, derived from normalized
coordinates and content-adaptive offsets, is then applied to
enable geometry-aware feature extraction that adapts to object
shapes. The independently processed regional features are
subsequently concatenated and fused. In addition, a residual connection is introduced to facilitate gradient flow and
strengthen feature representation. The operational workflow

multiscale context. The resultant features are then additively
merged with the original input through a residual connection.

D. Mamba-Based Local–Global Feature Co-Promotion
Module (MB-LGFCPM)
For RSOD, precise localization and discrimination of
objects across diverse scales and spatial contexts are
paramount. To address this, we design MB-LGFCPM, with
its architectural implementation depicted in Fig. 5. This module establishes a complementary interplay between localized
details and global contextual semantics, ensuring integritypreserving feature propagation to subsequent processing
stages. Within the MB-LGFCPM, the primary goals are
to enhance local–global feature fusion, improve contextual
modeling, and preserve fine-grained details alongside global
semantics. This integration achieves bidirectional complementarity, enriching representations and mitigating contextual
ambiguities, thereby improving detection robustness in complex remote sensing scenarios.
For local detail extraction, convolution and pooling capture
object-level features, which are mapped to a 1-D space to
model nonlinear relationships. A gating mechanism adaptively
emphasizes relevant channels and recalibrates the attention
map, enabling second-order feature interactions with low computational cost and improving geometric detail representation.
Position encoding further introduces local bias and spatial
awareness, enhancing sensitivity to input features. In addition,

During the experiment, the experimental equipment used
was an NVIDIA GeForce RTX3090 24-GB GPU. Python
3.8.19 was used as the compiler, and the code was written
under the PyTorch 2.4.1 framework. During model training,
stochastic gradient descent (SGD) was used as the optimizer.
To ensure fairness and comparability of the experiments, we
strictly adhered to the widely adopted dataset partitioning
method in the field, with a ratio of 7:2:1 for the training,
validation, and test sets. To mitigate the category imbalance,
we used a correlation data augmentation method, as shown in
Table I. The method and parameters are as follows: translate
set to 0.13, scale set to 0.08, fliplr set to 0.09, and mosaic set to
0.43. Other parameters are kept as the default. When training
the model, we set the experimental epoch to 500 to allow the
model to fully converge, and we set an early-stopping strategy
to stop training if there is no improvement after waiting for

C. Analysis of Experimental Datasets
Public datasets not only provide researchers with a comprehensive object detection benchmark but also offer rich
data support for practical applications. In this article, the
performance of the proposed method is tested and evaluated
using three datasets: RSOD, NWPU VHR-10, and DIOR. The
number of instances and size distribution in the three datasets
are shown in Fig. 6.
1) DIOR: The detection in optical remote sensing images
(DIOR) dataset [42] is a large-scale, multiclass, multiscale,
high-resolution benchmark dataset for object detection in optical remote sensing imagery. It represents the largest publicly
available benchmark of its kind. The dataset comprises 23 453
images containing 192 472 annotated instances, spanning 20
distinct object categories. These categories encompass vehicles, buildings, infrastructure, and natural objects. Each image
has a fixed size of 800 × 800 pixels, with spatial resolutions
ranging from 0.5 to 30 m/pixel. This diversity in scale and

content makes the DIOR dataset well-suited for evaluating
object detection tasks across diverse environments, including
urban areas, rural landscapes, and industrial zones.
2) RSOD: The remote sensing object detection (RSOD)
dataset [43] is a publicly available dataset specifically designed
for object detection tasks in remote sensing imagery. It is
widely adopted as an evaluation standard for benchmarking
and comparing the performance of various remote sensing
object detection algorithms, particularly in scenarios involving
multiscale objects, cluttered backgrounds, and diverse object
categories. The dataset comprises 976 images with 6950 annotated instances, organized into four distinct object categories.
3) NWPU VHR-10: The NWPU VHR-10 dataset [44] is a
publicly available benchmark specifically designed for object
detection tasks in remote sensing imagery. It comprises 800
high-resolution optical remote sensing images featuring ten
distinct geo-spatial object categories, with 3651 meticulously
annotated instances. The imagery exhibits spatial resolutions
ranging from 0.5 to 2 m, providing high-quality visual data
coupled with precise annotations. Characterized by its diverse
object categories and varied scene complexity, this dataset
serves as a significant benchmark for evaluating and advancing
algorithms in the field of remote sensing object detection.

V. C ONCLUSION
In this article, we propose a novel method called
DBCFS-Net to improve the detection capability of remote
sensing images. This method consists of three modules:
DBDM, CDAFEM, and MB-LGFCPM. It not only effectively
alleviates the loss of high-frequency fine-grained features
during sampling, but also captures efficient and discriminative object features through multiscale modeling. In addition,

establishing long-range dependencies based on feature similarity to form a local–global feature synergy mechanism
plays a crucial role in improving the separability between the
background and the objects. Extensive experiments conducted
on the DIOR, RSOD, and NWPU VHR-10 benchmark datasets
demonstrate that DBCFS-Net achieves superior detection
performance, outperforming current state-of-the-art detectors.
Specifically, on these three datasets, DBCFS-Net achieved
mAP scores of 87.8%, 96.9%, and 96.6%, respectively. However, our comprehensive experimental analysis shows that the
continuous pursuit of higher detection accuracy inevitably
leads to an increase in the number of parameters, which results
in a lack of overall lightweight design of the network architecture and an increase in the training cost. As an important
part of our future work, we will carry out research on model
lightweighting techniques based on branch reduction, quantization, and knowledge distillation, with a view to significantly
reducing the model size and improving the inference speed,
while maintaining its high accuracy advantage as much as
possible. We will also explore the possibilities of its deployment in edge devices, such as utilizing inference engines like
TensorRT and ONNX Runtime, to achieve extreme acceleration on platforms like NVIDIA Jetson and FPGAs. At the
same time, we have shortcomings when dealing with the
task of detecting objects with extreme aspect ratios, large
geometries, and small sizes. The task of directional object
detection also faces limitations. Future research will improve
the adaptability to linear and small objects from the perspective
of network detection head, bounding box representation, and
optimization of loss function and matching strategy, as well
as expand the capability of directional object detection. Also,
this study has not yet explored the potential advantages of
integrating multisource data to enhance detection performance;
effective utilization of multimodal information will also be a
key direction for subsequent research.
