A Cross-Modality Feature Adaptive Interaction
Approach for RGB-Infrared Object Detection
in Aerial Imagery
Chushi Yu , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, and Yoan Shin , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Object detection in aerial imagery, particularly from
unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and remote sensing platforms,
is crucial but faces significant challenges such as modality
misalignment, feature fusion degradation, and high computational complexity. To address these issues, this article introduces
cross-modality feature adaptive detection (CMFADet), a novel
framework for robust RGB-infrared (IR) object detection across
diverse aerial scenarios. CMFADet improves feature learning
through its innovative spatial–frequency feature enhancement
module (SFEM) and IR adaptive feature aggregation block
(IR-AFAB). It also integrates a channel interaction fusion
(CIF) module for dynamic weight allocation, ensuring truly
complementary information integration and avoiding mutual
interference. This allocation is governed by the specific characteristics of the target and the inherent strengths of each
modality. Detection accuracy is further refined via an adaptive
task-aware alignment head (ATAH) that learns the joint features.
Extensive experiments on the DroneVehicle, vehicle detection
in aerial imagery (VEDAI), and OGSOD-1.0 datasets demonstrate CMFADet’s superior performance, consistently surpassing
state-of-the-art algorithms, and effectively addressing the aforementioned challenges. The source code for this work is publicly
available at https://github.com/Yooyoo95/CMFADet
Index Terms—Channel interaction fusion (CIF), crossmodality, feature extraction, RGB-infrared (IR) object detection.

However, aerial object detection, whether from low-altitude

more comprehensive solutions.

different sensors like visible light, IR, and synthetic aperture
radar (SAR), multimodal methods leverage the complementary information between modalities and improve detection
accuracy and robustness in complex aerial environments.

Despite promising advancements in multimodality fusion,
several challenges persist. Fig. 1 illustrates these challenges
using examples from the DroneVehicle dataset. First, due to
differences in perspective, distance, and sensor characteristics,
a weak misalignment challenge often exists between RGB and
IR images, particularly for small targets. This misalignment
requires careful consideration during fusion. Second, conventional multimodal fusion methods, primarily operating in the
spatial domain, often miss valuable frequency-domain cues
that could provide more robust representations, neglecting the
distinct frequency characteristics of RGB and IR modalities.
Additionally, existing object detector heads commonly employ
independent classification and localization branches, which
can lead to a lack of interaction and suboptimal joint feature
learning between these two crucial tasks. Finally, the pursuit
of performance often leads to computationally heavy models,
hindering real-time deployment on aerial platforms.
To address the aforementioned challenges and significantly
boost detection accuracy across diverse aerial scenarios, this
article proposes an innovative object detection framework for
RGB-IR aerial images, named cross-modality feature adaptive detection (CMFADet). At its core, CMFADet leverages
spatial- and frequency-domain decomposition through a dedicated spatial–frequency feature enhancement module (SFEM)
to isolate global structural information from fine-grained
local details, while an IR adaptive feature aggregation block
(IR-AFAB) is specifically designed to enhance feature extraction for IR imagery. Instead of relying on simple feature-level
summation, CMFADet employs more sophisticated strategies
for dynamic weight allocation, expertly managed by a channel interaction fusion (CIF) module. Furthermore, it tackles
the limitations of independent classification and localization
branches by proposing an adaptive task-aware alignment head
(ATAH), which learns to integrate task-specific features from
various convolutional layers. The main contributions are summarized as follows.

1) We propose the CMFADet framework for multimodality
object detection in aerial images with a two-stream
backbone, which incorporates an SFEM to enhance RGB
feature fidelity against variance and an IR-AFAB to
prevent thermal feature degradation and reinforce sparse
IR features.
2) We introduce a CIF module to overcome static feature
merging limitations. CIF facilitates adaptive aggregation
by dynamically allocating fusion weights and promotes
deep residual cross-modality feature exchange to mitigate mutual interference.
3) We design the ATAH as a unique detection head that
performs adaptive task-aware alignment on task-specific
features by implicitly modeling inter-task coupling to
maximize predictive performance.
4) We conduct experiments on three representative multimodality object detection datasets, DroneVehicle,
VEDAI, and OGSOD-1.0, demonstrating state-of-theart performance with favorable computational efficiency,
validating its suitability for aerial deployment.

II. R ELATED W ORK
A. Generic Object Detection
Object detection is a core computer vision task focused
on classifying and precisely localizing objects with bounding boxes. DL-based methods promote the development
of this task, which can be broadly categorized into twoand single-stage detectors. Two-stage detectors such as the
R-CNN family [6], [12], [13] first generate candidate regions
and then classify and refine them, which achieves high accuracy but incurs substantial computational cost. In contrast,
single-stage detectors, including single-shot multibox detector
(SSD) [14], RetinaNet [15], and the YOLO series [16], [17],
[18], directly predict object categories and locations in a single
pass, providing superior speed. Improvements in backbone
networks (e.g., residual neural network (ResNet) [19]), feature aggregation mechanisms in neck networks (e.g., feature
pyramid network (FPN) [20] and path aggregation network
(PANet) [21]), refined detection heads (e.g., dynamic head
[22]), and optimized loss functions further enhance performance. Beyond these refinements, methods like deformable
convolutional network (DCNv2) [23] enhance adaptability to
object scale and shape variations, while task-aligned detectors
like the task-aligned one-stage object detection (TOOD) [24]
improve consistency between classification and localization
by designing a task-aligned head and task alignment learning strategy. Building upon the real-time DETR (RT-DETR)
[25], the MCG-RTDETR architecture enhances precise object
detection in UAV imagery by integrating dual and deformable
convolution modules, a cascaded group attention module,

B. Oriented Object Detection
Unlike generic object detection using horizontal bounding
boxes (HBBs), oriented object detection employs oriented
bounding boxes (OBBs) or quadrilaterals for precise localization of targets with arbitrary orientations. OBB overcomes
limitations of HBB-based methods in dense scenes, complex
backgrounds, or with high-aspect-ratio targets, especially in
aerial and remote sensing imagery. The field has evolved from
two-stage to single-stage architectures. RoI Transformer [27]
pioneers spatial transformations and consists of the rotated
region of interest (RRoI) Learner and RRoI Warping. Oriented
R-CNN [28] later proposes an oriented region proposal network to generate high-quality oriented proposals. However,
the computational cost of two-stage methods motivated the
development of single-stage detectors. A single-shot alignment
network (S2 A-Net) tackles feature misalignment with a feature
alignment module (FAM) and an oriented detection module
(ODM) [29]. ReDet [30] incorporates rotation-equivariant networks into the detector to extract rotation-equivariant features.
Song et al. [31] introduce a rotation-invariant transformer plus
model (RIFormer+) by designing a rotation-invariant crosspatch embedding (RICPE) module and a response-enhanced
attention (REA) to enhance local and global feature learning.
These developments highlight the importance of rotation modeling for oriented targets and provide valuable insights into
how orientation-aware representations can facilitate effective
detection under complex imaging conditions.
C. Multimodal Object Detection
Multimodal object detection integrates complementary cues
from heterogeneous sensors (e.g., visible, IR, and depth) to
improve robustness where single modalities are insufficient.
The key challenge lies in effectively fusing diverse data
and mitigating cross-modal discrepancies. Fusion strategies
are categorized by processing stages. Some fusion methods combine raw data channels directly by concatenating
multimodality images as a preprocessing process. In decision level, each modality is detected independently, and
predictions are combined (e.g., weighted non-maximum suppression). Feature-level fusion is the most common approach,
balancing interaction and modality-specific processing by integrating extracted features (e.g., via concatenation or attention
mechanism [32]). In feature-level fusion, detectors prioritize
alignment and conflict resolution. UA-CMDet [9] introduces
an uncertainty-aware mechanism to evaluate the uncertainty
weights of every target for RGB and IR modalities by calculating the cross-modal intersection over union (IoU) and the
illumination value. The two-stream feature alignment detector
(TSFADet) addresses misalignment challenge by calibrating
feature maps via a translation-scale-rotation alignment (TSRA)

module [33]. In [34], a transformer-based calibrated and
complementary method named C2 Former can simultaneously
alleviate the modality miscalibration and fusion inaccuracy.
The Dual-YOLO [35] is an IR object detector based on
YOLOv7, which introduces the inception and SE modules
into the attention fusion method to strengthen the characteristic complementarity. The cross-modal conflict-aware
learning network (CALNet) [36] is a multispectral oriented
object detector designed to tackle the semantic conflict problem. For illumination changes, Shang et al. [37] propose a
cross-modality and cross-domain low-light detector (CCLDet)
consisting of an illumination-aware module (OIAM), a visibility loss and cross-domain feature fusion module (CDFFM).
Nie et al. [38] present a robust CNN–Transformer-based
framework for cross-modal feature fusion in visual and IR
imagery, focusing on the effective interaction between local
and long-range features. Overall, existing multimodal object
detectors typically rely on explicitly designed fusion or alignment modules to handle heterogeneous discrepancy, with
cross-modal interactions governed by the predefined model
structure.
D. Related Multimodal Applications and Insights
Related multimodal applications in computer vision and
remote sensing offer complementary perspectives on handling
heterogeneous modalities, with recent advances in tasks such
as salient object detection (SOD) providing relevant references
for multimodal detection. The CONTRINET [39] utilizes
a divide-and-conquer strategy with a triple-flow network to
handle modality-specific cues and enhance resilience to defective data. The transformer-based CATNet [40] and CPNet
[41] tackle multiscale aggregation challenges. Specifically,
CATNet uses a cascaded and aggregated decoder to mitigate
information loss, while CPNet employs a progressive decoding
strategy to refine predictions. Beyond SOD, GCViTDet [42]
demonstrates that generalized vision transformers with masked
token modeling can enhance semantic fusion.
General multimodal remote sensing research focuses on
robust low-level feature handling. The content-adaptive nonlocal convolution (CANConv) [43] shows the utility of spatially
adaptive convolution and nonlocal self-similarity for effective
image fusion. The co-occurrence filter-based matching work
[44] confirms the importance of building feature representations robust to nonlinear radiation differences (NRDs) for
accurate multimodal image registration. The anchor-guided
fuzzy clustering study [45] shows the effectiveness of learning
highly discriminative, low-dimensional multimodal features
to enable robust high-level interpretation. In hyperspectral
scene classification, the HRSS-C [46] employs a three-branch
spectral–spatial fusion network to leverage diverse feature
representations. These findings further motivate considering
adaptive alignment and modality-specific feature modeling.
From an application perspective, aerial person detection (APD)
represents another relevant application domain. A recent
survey [47] categorizes APD methods into object-aware,
sample-oriented, fusion-based, and lightweight designs. For
instance, a lightweight distillation network (APDNet) [48]
validates an effective approach to balance detection speed and

III. P ROPOSED M ETHOD
This section introduces the CMFADet approach engineered
for enhanced RGB-IR object detection within drone and
remote sensing imagery. This is achieved by integrating synergistic components into a cohesive end-to-end framework.
As depicted in Fig. 2, the proposed architecture is composed
of a backbone network, a neck network, and prediction
heads. Initially, the multimodality backbone processes input
RGB-IR paired images, extracting comprehensive information.
This backbone then generates multiscale feature maps from
three distinct stages (P3 –P5 ) through the synergistic operation
of the CIF module and element-wise addition. Subsequently,
these enriched feature maps are propagated to the neck network, where they undergo sophisticated feature fusion. Finally,

the ATAH is employed to perform precise object localization
and classification as the prediction head.
A. Multimodality Backbone
The backbone network comprises two parallel network
structures, specifically designed to process RGB and IR
images, respectively. Paired images are fed into their corresponding network branches. In the initial stage, we design
dedicated modules to enhance feature extraction for the distinct characteristics of each image modality. Specifically, an
SFEM is utilized in the RGB branch, while an IR-AFAB is
proposed for the IR branch. Then, the method retains the crossstage partial with kernel size 2 (C3K2) block consisting of
two cross stage partial (CSP) bottlenecks, the spatial pyramid
pooling-fast (SPPF) block, and the convolutional block with
parallel spatial attention (C2PSA) block from the original
YOLOv11 version. Detailed explanations of these modules are
provided in the following.
1) Spatial–Frequency Feature Enhancement Module:
The SFEM is designed to improve feature representations
by synergistically integrating spatial- and frequency-domain
information through two parallel branches as shown in Fig. 3.
This decomposition mechanism is critical for RGB modality, as the frequency branch introduces frequency-domain

2) IR Adaptive Feature Aggregation Block: We propose the
IR-AFAB to enhance the feature extraction for IR imagery.
As shown in Fig. 4, the IR-AFAB operates by splitting the
input into two pathways: a deep branch and a skip connection.
The deep branch is processed by a sequence of cascaded
adaptive mixer blocks (AMBs), while the skip connection
is implemented to prevent thermal feature degradation in

the deep path. These pathways are fused via concatenation
followed by a 1 × 1 convolution. This design effectively
integrates features from both shallow and deep processing
streams to preserve sparse, low-level thermal features.

residual connections. To stabilize the training and regularize

convolutional gated linear unit (ConvGLU), enhances channel
discriminability. The output of each stage is added to its
input through a residual connection. This sequential structure
allows the AMB to first aggregate spatial information and then
enhance channel discriminability, forming a powerful feature
representation. The process can be formulated as follows:

feature representations and task-specific requirements, we
propose the ATAH. This head module enhances detection
performance by combining implicit inter-task feature coupling
with dynamic, task-specific feature alignment. The ATAH’s

IV. E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
A. Datasets
The DroneVehicle dataset [9] is a large-scale drone-based
vehicle detection dataset, comprising 28 439 paired RGB-IR
images across five categories: car, truck, bus, van, and freight
car. In accordance with the original publication’s protocol, we
partition the dataset into a training set with 17 990 pairs, a
validation set with 1469 pairs, and a test set with 8980 pairs.
We remove the white border with a width of 100 pixels in the
original dataset image through preprocessing.
The VEDAI dataset [10] is another RGB-IR aerial data set
for small vehicle detection, consisting of 1246 paired images.
The data are split into training and testing sets following the
officially provided methodology. While VEDAI is available in
both 1024 × 1024 and 512 × 512 resolutions, we utilize the
512 × 512 images for our experiments. The dataset encompasses nine categories: car, truck, tractor, camping car, van,
pickup, boat, plane, and others.
The OGSOD-1.0 [11] is a recently released SAR-optical
paired image dataset for multi-object detection in remote
sensing. It contains 14 665 training pairs and 3666 test pairs,
with an original resolution of 256 × 256 pixels. The dataset
includes over 48 000 annotated instances across three static
categories: bridges, harbors, and oil tanks.
B. Implementation Details
All experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA 4080 graphics processing unit (GPU), and the proposed model is
implemented in PyTorch 2.1.0. For the DroneVehicle and
VEDAI datasets, all input images are resized to a resolution of 640 × 640 pixels, and the model is trained for 300
epochs with batch sizes of 8 and 4. For the OGSOD-1.0,
the images are resized to 512 × 512 with a batch size of 8
and trained for 100 epochs. The model is optimized using
stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with an initial learning rate
of 0.01, a momentum of 0.937, and a weight decay of 0.0005,
following the default training configuration of YOLOv11 [18].
The training retains the default data augmentation protocol,
including Mosaic with 1.0 probability and random horizontal
flip with 0.5 probability. This identical protocol is consistently
applied to all models within our ablation study to ensure a fair
internal comparative analysis.

F. Discussion
The superior performance of CMFADet stems from a
systematic framework that effectively exploits multimodal
information for vehicle detection. The method enhances
modality-specific features prior to fusion and employs a
dynamic fusion strategy. As quantitative and qualitative results
confirm, CMFADet achieves a substantial performance advantage over state-of-the-art and baseline methods. Specifically,
CMFADet records the highest mAP@0.5 of 83.42% on
the DroneVehicle dataset, surpassing the second-best method
by 4.02 percentage points. This superiority is confirmed
across different challenge profiles, including VEDAI and
the OGSOD-1.0 datasets, validating the architecture’s strong
cross-modality generalization capability. The effectiveness of
each component (SFEM, IR-AFAB, CIF, and ATAH) is confirmed through the ablation study presented in Table V.
From a modeling perspective, SFEM is designed as a unified
spatial–frequency feature enhancement module that complements conventional spatial feature learning. The ablation
results demonstrate that incorporating frequency-domain feature transformations alongside spatial edge-aware information
consistently improves detection performance across datasets,
validating the effectiveness of the spatial–frequency design in
complex aerial scenarios.
Despite its strong performance in general scenarios,
CMFADet still has room for improvement. For instance, while
the model excels at detecting well-defined classes such as
car and bus on the DroneVehicle dataset, its performance
is relatively lower for fine-grained categories like truck and
freight car. This confusion is mainly caused by the top-down
aerial viewpoint, where discriminative structural differences
are weakened by scale variation, limited spatial resolution,
and partial occlusion. Our computational analysis (Table IV)
confirms the model achieves a superior accuracy–efficiency

V. C ONCLUSION
In this article, we present CMFADet, a novel cross-modality
detection framework tailored for aerial RGB-IR imagery
under challenging conditions. To address modality imbalance and misalignment, we propose a multimodal backbone
incorporating an SFEM and an IR-AFAB for dedicated feature extraction, a CIF module for adaptive cross-modality
interaction, and an ATAH head for task-aware feature alignment. Extensive experiments on DroneVehicle, VEDAI, and
OGSOD-1.0 demonstrate that CMFADet achieves consistent
performance gains over state-of-the-art methods with favorable
computational efficiency, particularly in low-light and smallobject scenarios. Overall, this work highlights the importance
of maintaining modality-specific features and task-specific
representations in multimodal detection. Future research may
explore extending CMFADet to real-time or sequential aerial
perception tasks.

Electrical and Computer Engineering, The University of British Columbia,
