MSCK-Net: Multiscale Chinese Knot Convolutional
Network for Dim and Small Infrared Ship Detection
Yuhao Lin , Dongliang Peng , Liang Wang , Lingjie Jiang , and Haewoon Nam , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Remote sensing infrared ship detection is crucial
for maritime safety and traffic management in civil-military
integrated applications. However, the detection accuracy and
robustness of current methods are still limited by dataset
constraints, including small scale, narrow target size distribution, sparse targets, and high scenario specificity. To
address these problems, we integrate publicly available datasets
to construct infrared ship detection dataset (IRShip)—a
relatively large-scale infrared ship detection dataset comprising 27 138 images, significantly improving data scale
and diversity. We further design a copy-Poisson blend
(CP-PB) offline data augmentation approach and introduce a
dense one-to-one (Dense-O2O) online augmentation strategy,
which improves adaptability to complex backgrounds and scale
variations, mitigates problems from sparse targets, and improves
dataset utility and model robustness. We propose MSCK-Net,
a multiscale Chinese knot convolutional network tailored for
detecting dim and small infrared ship targets (DSIRSTs). Specifically, we propose a novel Chinese knot convolution (CKConv)
that better aligns with both the local features of small targets
and the morphological characteristics of ship targets, thereby
significantly enhancing low-level feature representation. Built
with CKConv, the multiscale knot block (MSK-Block) and
Stem-ck modules enhance deep feature transmission efficiency
and global modeling of DSIRSTs, leading to notable gains in
detection performance. Extensive experiments on IRShip, NUDTSIRST-Sea, ISDD, IRSDSS, and Maritime-sirst demonstrate that
MSCK-Net-M achieves state-of-the-art performance, with AP50 ,
AP75 , and AP of 82.5%, 53.1%, and 50.9% on IRShip, significantly outperforming the existing 20 general object detectors
and 6 infrared target detectors. Generalization experiments on
four subdatasets further verify the effectiveness and robustness
of our proposed method. The code and dataset are available at:
https://github.com/sjxxrh/MSCK-Net
Index Terms—Chinese knot convolution (CKConv), dim and
small infrared ship targets (DSIRSTs), infrared ship detection
dataset (IRShip), multiscale network.

I. I NTRODUCTION

I

N MODERN ocean monitoring and national defense systems [1], ship detection in remote sensing infrared imagery
has become a critical technology. With increasing marine
resource development and a more complex maritime security environment, timely and accurate vessel detection is
essential for safeguarding maritime sovereignty, ensuring navigation safety, and supporting reconnaissance missions [2].
Infrared imaging offers all-weather, all-day operation, inherent
stealth, strong anti-interference capabilities, and high thermal sensitivity, making it a core technology for maritime
surveillance. However, satellite-based infrared imaging over
the open ocean involves more complex and variable backgrounds than land [3] or airborne [4] imaging. Maritime targets
are typically smaller, exhibit large-scale variations, and are
often obscured by background clutter such as sea waves,
clouds, and coastal structures, leading to false alarms and
reduced detection accuracy. Moreover, atmospheric scattering
and refraction, along with sensor noise, result in low signalto-noise ratios, loss of texture, and weak target-background
contrast, further challenging infrared ship detection. Infrared
ship detection is a critical research area within the field of
infrared small target detection (IRSTD). Traditional methods
rely on predefined priors such as background suppression
[5], [6], and low-rank sparse decomposition [7]. However,
in complex environments, these approaches often fail due to
over-reliance on scene-specific priors and insufficient semantic
understanding, resulting in limited detection performance and
poor generalization. With the advancement of deep learning,
convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [8] and Transformerbased [9] models have become the dominant approaches
for both infrared and general small-target detection. These
methods are typically classified into one-stage, two-stage, and
end-to-end detection frameworks. Two-stage detectors such as
Faster R-CNN [10] achieve high detection accuracy through a
multistep process involving region proposal generation, feature
extraction, and classification with regression. However, their
high computational cost and large parameter count hinder
real-time deployment. In contrast, one-stage detectors like
YOLO [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], and SSD [17] are
widely adopted for their efficiency and simplicity. For instance,
Li et al. [18] proposed CYSDM, a method based on YOLOv5
for thermal remote sensing infrared images, which incorporates an expanded convolution module to enlarge the receptive
field and preserve more semantic features. Gao et al. [19]

End-to-end detectors like RT-DETR [9] enable direct

super-resolution, the discrimination ability of small targets has
been improved.

in model complexity and training cost. Peng et al. [25]

improves localization accuracy. The model is also lightweight,
reducing parameters and computation while balancing accuracy and speed. To address the sparse supervision issue in
DFine’s one-to-one (O2O) matching, Huang et al. [26] proposed DEIM, which introduces a dense O2O (Dense-O2O)
strategy and a matching-aware loss (MAL), accelerating
convergence and improving training efficiency and detection performance. Overall, these improvements have made
DETR-based models highly competitive in real-time detection,
outperforming YOLO-based approaches in both accuracy and
efficiency in academic and practical settings.

heavy interference. Moreover, they lack distinct color and
texture features found in optical images, making them hard to
detect against complex marine backgrounds. Current infrared
ship datasets [27], [28], [29], [30] are typically limited in

CKConv across multiple scales to construct the MSKNet, our method enables robust deep feature extraction
for DSIRSTs, alleviates weak-feature attenuation during

II. R ELATED W ORKS
First, we reviewed the related work on IRSTD and remote
sensing infrared ship target detection (RSIRSD).
A. Infrared Small Target Detection
Deep neural networks, due to their powerful automatic
feature-learning capabilities, can accurately extract critical
information from large-scale data in complex scenarios. As
a result, data-driven deep learning has become the dominant
approach in IRSTD [31]. Dai et al. [3] released the first
public IRSTD dataset and proposed an asymmetric contextual modulation mechanism for adaptive cross-layer feature
fusion, thereby enhancing the semantic representation of small
targets. Li et al. [32] designed the dense nested interaction
module (DNIM), which improves contextual perception and
feature reconstruction through hierarchical connections and
feature reuse. Considering the scale diversity of infrared
targets, subsequent research has gradually shifted toward
multiscale feature fusion and modeling [33]. Li et al. [34]
introduced the MFPF-LTA framework, which progressively
fuses multiscale features and applies local target amplification
to enhance small-target saliency. Xiao et al. [35] constructed
the DenseSIRST dataset and proposed BAFE-Net, a multitask learning framework that balances detection precision and
false-alarm suppression under complex maritime backgrounds.
Guoy et al. [4] developed a dynamic large-kernel attention
(DLC-Attention) mechanism that leverages adaptive local
context learning to expand the receptive field and improve
adaptability to targets of different scales. Meanwhile, the
inherently dim and small characteristics of infrared targets
have motivated studies focusing on local feature modeling and
edge structure preservation [36]. Yang et al. [37] proposed
PsConv, a pinwheel-shaped convolution designed to simulate
the Gaussian response of infrared targets, thereby improving
low-level feature extraction. Li et al. [38] further introduced
the multibranch mutual learning network (MMLNet), which
jointly models edge, localization, and detection tasks to enable
comprehensive information interaction and improve the structural consistency and texture representation of small targets.
Most existing methods focus on multiscale fusion or large
receptive fields, with little attention given to feature evolution
in low-level extraction. In the field of RSIRSD, we propose
CKConv, a novel low-level feature extraction structure that
integrates the morphological characteristics of both ships and
small targets. By leveraging a CrissCross-Square structure,
it efficiently utilizes weak pixel information around targets
to enhance central features, enabling the network to achieve
multiscale global modeling of DSIRSTs in its deep layers.
B. Remote Sensing Infrared Ship Target Detection
RSIRSD can be regarded as a subset of IRSTD. Although
many methods developed for IRSTD are applicable to

5602218

RSIRSD, remote sensing infrared ship targets exhibit unique
characteristics, such as sea-land backgrounds, cloud occlusion,
and the elongated morphology of ship targets. In recent
years, several public RSIRSD datasets have advanced this
field. Wu et al. [27] introduced MTU-Net, a multilevel TransUNet with a multiview feature extraction module (MVTM)
to capture long-range dependencies of small targets. They
released NUDT-SIRST-Sea, a large airborne infrared ship
dataset. However, it contains many background-only images
and some incomplete annotations, which may hinder training. Han et al. [28] developed KCPNet, a knowledge-driven
context-aware network, and released the ISDD dataset. Their
method balances multilayer features and expands the receptive
field via BFF-Net. A context attention network (CA-Net)
was also introduced to enhance target information and suppress background clutter, improving detection performance.
Hu et al. [29] proposed SMPISD-MTPNet and introduced
IRSDSS, an infrared ship dataset with scene segmentation.
They also enhanced ISDD by adding scene masks to create
EISDD. These methods reduce false alarms through semantic
segmentation and use a gradient-based module to enhance
dim targets. However, annotation costs are high, and performance depends on segmentation accuracy, limiting end-to-end
deployment. Moreover, the backgrounds and scale variations
in IRSDSS and EISDD are limited, reducing generalization.
Guo et al. [30] released Maritime-sirst, a dataset with complex
maritime backgrounds, along with FCNet, a flexible convolutional network. FCNet combines dilated and deformable
convolutions to adaptively adjust the receptive field, improving
segmentation. However, the dataset is small and shows limited
scale variation, restricting real-world applicability.
So, current datasets still lack in scale, diversity, and
detection difficulty, limiting their ability to reflect real-world
complexity and hindering practical deployment. Our work
builds on these datasets by standardizing image resolution
and label formats, and designing customized offline data
augmentation strategies to better utilize background images.
The goal is to construct a larger scale, more diverse, and more
challenging RSIRSD dataset that bridges the gap between
theory and real-world application.

B. Properties of IRShip
Without significantly changing the original image target
resolution, we perform separate segmentation and fusion of
the training set, validation set, and test set of the above four
public datasets to form a dataset with an image resolution
of 512 × 512. Given the low native resolution of MaritimeSIRST, we mosaic four randomly selected images into a

512 × 512 composite and convert the provided mask annotations to bounding boxes, while keeping the total number of
images unchanged. For NUDT-SIRST-Sea, we corrected a few
missed and incorrect labels through reannotation and likewise
converted mask annotations to bounding boxes. For ISDD and
IRSDSS, we standardized all images to 512 × 512 by resizing.
The final IRShip dataset contains 27 138 images, including
16 283 training, 2720 validation, and 8135 test images. It
covers near-infrared, short-wave infrared, and thermal infrared
ship images captured in diverse maritime environments such as
ports, canals, coastal waters, and open seas. In IRShip, images
from NUDT-SIRST-Sea, ISDD, IRSDSS, and Maritime-sirst
make up 85.61%, 4.73%, 5.49%, and 4.17% of the corpus, respectively. Correspondingly, annotated targets derived
from these datasets account for 51.26%, 8.42%, 11.18%, and

with the four existing public datasets, IRShip demonstrates

the IRShip dataset encompasses a wide range of scenarios,
including various environments such as ports, land, inland
rivers, oceans, and islands, as well as different weather conditions like high and low temperatures, fog, and sea winds.
It also includes diverse types of ship targets, such as sparse,
dense, dim, and small ones. The combination of these factors results in a greater variety of complex scenarios. These
complex scenarios provide a richer and more challenging data

A. MSCK-Net
Fig. 4 shows the overall structure of the MSCK-Net, which
mainly consists of the backbone network, the feature fusion
network, and the decoder network. The backbone network uses
MSK-Net, which is composed of Stem-ck, patch embedding,
and multiscale knot blocks (MSK-Blocks) of different scales,
with the spatial resolution gradually reduced by 32 times,
outputting multiscale feature maps (P1, P2, P3, and P4) from
the last four stages. To effectively enhance the deep feature
extraction capability of DSIRSTs, we propose an innovative
convolutional module-CKConv (see Section IV-B for details).
Multiscale CKConvs are embedded into the Stem-ck to capture
multiscale representations of dim targets in high-resolution
images. Based on this design, we further develop the core
module MSK-Block (see Section IV-C), which leverages the
local feature extraction advantages of multiscale CKConv
to continuously enhance high-level semantic representations.
Notably, MSK-Net employs small kernels (3, 5) in shallow
layers to capture fine spatial details, while deeper layers
utilize larger kernels (e.g., 7) to gather richer contextual
and background information. This multiscale design expands
the effective receptive field and overcomes the limitations
of fixed-kernel convolutions, preserving small-target features
and reducing background interference, thereby improving the
accuracy and robustness of infrared ship detection.
Then, the multiscale feature maps output by the backbone
network are sent to the multiscale feature fusion network
(MSFF-Net), where a series of upsampling and downsampling operations enhance cross-scale feature interaction. The
RepNCSPELAN4 [13], [25] module integrates the RepVGG
reparameterization branch with the CSP-ELAN structure,
enabling efficient multiscale feature aggregation and gradient
optimization in the Neck stage. The decoder module follows
the D-FINE [25] design, decoding multiscale feature maps
with fine-grained distribution refinement (FDR) and global

B. CKConv
In DSIRSTs tasks, targets typically exhibit slender shapes,
low intensity, and weak local contrast, and are often immersed
in cluttered maritime or coastal backgrounds. Standard 3 × 3
convolutions maintain local spatial continuity but have a
limited receptive field, making them ineffective for elongated structures. Although LKCs [39] enlarge the receptive
field, they lack directional sensitivity and may introduce

redundant background responses, leading to over-smoothing
and contrast loss. Strip convolutions [40], though adept at
accentuating directional features, fall short in aggregating
information across orthogonal orientations. To overcome these
limitations, we propose a novel CKConv module inspired
by the intertwined geometry of traditional Chinese knots.
CKConv integrates horizontal, vertical, and square convolutional kernels in a multiscale and cross-oriented fusion scheme,
thereby forming a centrally convergent and four-directionally
interwoven receptive topology. This design employs a hybrid
parallel–serial directional fusion mechanism that enables compact and discriminative feature aggregation for slender and
DSIRSTs. It effectively balances directional feature extraction,
local spatial consistency, and feature fidelity while maintaining
a lightweight parameter footprint. The following presents the
core design of CKConv:

The multiscale strip-convolution branches are designed to
capture the infrared features along orthogonal directions of
ship targets, while the square-kernel branch preserves local
spatial continuity. Their outputs are directly fused via elementwise addition to form a CrissCross-Square structure that is
both centrally focused and locally coherent [37], [41]. This
design enhances directional sensitivity and center aggregation
while maintaining a compact parameter size, enabling the
module to effectively aggregate weak surrounding pixel information and strengthen the feature responses of weak targets,
thereby improving detection stability under low-SNR conditions. It further avoids the parameter redundancy and gradient
attenuation of deep serial stacks, achieving efficient complementary feature integration within a single stage. Notably,
in the proposed backbone, CKConv integrates grouped convolution to maximize the receptive field while minimizing
parameter count.
Local feature extraction is critical in infrared ship detection.
Effectively utilizing edge weak pixel information during downsampling helps preserve and enhance both local and global

C. MSK-Block
Due to limitations in current infrared imaging technologies,
existing infrared ship images typically exhibit low resolution
and large variations in target scale. Effectively extracting
multiscale features from such images remains a significant
challenge. Key features of DSIRSTs are often confined to
shallow network layers and struggle to propagate into deeper
layers. While shallow networks preserve spatial resolution,
they demand higher computational resources and often require
specialized architectural designs (e.g., dedicated design for
the P1 layer [24]). To address these problems, we propose
the MSK-Block based on CKConv. As shown in Fig. 7, the
MSK-Net is built primarily from stacked MSK-Blocks. Each
MSK-Block contains two residual sub-blocks: a CKConvbased sub-block with an inverted bottleneck (IBN) structure
inspired by ResNet [42], and a feed-forward network subblock. The CKConv sub-block expands and then compresses
channel dimensions via 1 × 1 convolutions, reducing parameters and computational cost. Multiscale junction convolutions
are integrated between these stages to enhance multiscale
feature extraction and improve nonlinear mapping. The feedforward sub-block adopts a convolutional gated linear unit
(CGLU) [43], which incorporates depthwise separable convolution (DWConv) into the standard gated linear unit (GLU).
This design improves channel mixing and local feature modeling, avoiding the limitations of global average pooling used in
conventional SE modules. It enables precise spatial modeling
and effective feature refinement.
In summary, the MSK-Block is designed to preserve features of DSIRSTs during deep feature extraction. With the
CrissCross-Square structure, it balances directional feature
extraction and local spatial consistency, thereby strengthening
the representation of infrared ship targets. Initially, we apply
a 1 × 1 convolution to the input features in order to expand

E. Data Augmentation
1) Offline Data Augmentation: As shown in Section III, the
IRShip dataset suffers from a severe foreground–background
imbalance, with over 69% of samples being background.
This leads the model to overfit uninformative background
areas, slowing convergence [27]. Moreover, complex and noisy
backgrounds further hinder detection, especially when targets
are sparse and dim, leading to elevated miss and false alarm
rates in current methods. The copy–paste (CP) technique is a
powerful data augmentation strategy. We propose an enhanced
CP-PB method (in Fig. 8). First, during target selection, the
annotated bounding box is randomly expanded to crop the
target region while retaining a small portion of the surrounding
background, thereby preserving local contextual information.
Next, in the paste region filtering stage, the transformed target
is accurately placed onto real ocean areas with minimal or
no cloud coverage. The selected regions have pixel intensities
ranging from 0 to 35, and the intersection-over-union (IoU)
between pasted targets is constrained to be below 0.1 to
prevent overlap. Finally, during target fusion, Poisson blending
is applied to ensure a natural and smooth transition between
the target and the background, effectively eliminating artificial
boundary artifacts that could mislead the model during training. Overall, CP-PB balances the dataset by increasing the
prevalence of foreground targets while preserving target features and contextual information, thereby improving detection
performance in complex infrared scenes.

2) Online Data Augmentation: In the original dataset,

the DETR framework. DFine uses an O2O matching strategy,
which assigns only one positive sample per target, limiting
positive samples and slowing model convergence. To address
this, a Dense-O2O matching strategy [26] is introduced. It
generates extra positive samples online for each image via a
new scheduler using Mosic and Mixup augmentations. The
scheduler has four stages: disable advanced augmentation in
the first four epochs for simpler attention learning. Use Mosic
and Mixup from epoch 5–48 to boost positive samples during
training. Disable Mixup from epoch 48–148 to enhance real
background learning. Turn off all augmentation from epoch
148–160 to reduce training noise, improve real data fitting,
and stabilize training. Different learning rates (0.0004 for
stages 1–2 and 0.0008 for stages 3–4) help the model adapt
faster, improve efficiency, and prevent overfitting. Combining
offline and online augmentation increases positive samples,
stabilizes training, speeds up convergence, utilizes many
infrared background images, and enhances the model’s generalization and practicality.

3) Evaluation Metrics: We adopt the widely used COCO
metrics—AP50 , AP75 , AP, APS , APM , and APL —as evaluation
criteria. Notably, the targets in the IRShip dataset do not
follow the standard COCO target definition. According to
the definition by the Society of Photo-Optical Instrumentation
Engineers (SPIE), small targets are those with an area smaller
than 80 pixels in a 256 × 256 image [45]. Therefore, we
redefine the size categories and introduce the APT metric to
represent tiny targets. Specifically, ships are classified into four
size ranges: [0, 40] pixels (tiny), [0, 80] pixels (small), [80,
256] pixels (medium), and [256, 1e5 ] pixels (large). These
categories account for 11.5%, 34.1%, 38%, and 16.4% of all
targets, respectively. During testing, only predicted bounding
boxes with a confidence score of 0.5 or higher are considered,
and the IoU threshold for NMS is set to 0.5. In Sections
V and VI, AP50 , AP75 , AP, APT , APS , APM , and APL are
used as shorthand for AP@50, AP@75, AP@50:95, APT @50,
APS @50, APM @50, and APL @50, respectively.

MSCK-Net-S improves AP50 and AP by 4.4% and 3% over
DEIM-M using half the parameters and FLOPs. These results
confirm that MSCK-Net enhances multiscale infrared ship
detection in complex environments, achieving stronger feature
representation and outperforming existing methods.
2) Comparative Evaluation of Infrared Target Detection
Algorithms: To validate the superiority of the MSCK-Net in
infrared ship target detection, this section presents a comprehensive comparison with six state-of-the-art open-source
algorithms from the past four years. These algorithms include
ACM [31], RISTD [64], AGPCNet [65], DNANet [32],
EFLNet [66], and IR-DETR [23]. Since most ISTD methods
rely on pixel-level segmentation, while our approach is based
on an object detection framework, Chen et al. [67] added
detection heads to the first four methods to generate bounding
boxes, enabling a unified comparison across all models.
As shown in Table VIII, MSCK-Net-M achieves the best
detection performance with the lowest parameter count and
computational cost. Compared to EFLNet, the suboptimal
model, MSCK-Net-M reduces parameters and FLOPs by
66.88% and 44.25%, respectively, while improving AP50 ,
AP75 , and AP by 6%, 13.5%, and 9.7%. Notably, APT ,
APS , APM , and APL are enhanced by 10.5%, 13.6%, 9.7%,
and 1.1%, respectively. EFLNet achieves APL performance
comparable to MSCK-Net-S, suggesting its focus on large
targets. However, its performance on medium and small
targets is limited, indicating a weaker multiscale detection
capability. This highlights the adaptability and superiority of
MSCK-Net in multiscale detection. IR-DETR outperforms
EFLNet in multiscale detection and has the lowest model

complexity. However, its APT score remains 7.5% lower than
that of MSCK-Net-S. This may be due to the IRShip dataset
containing smaller targets with more complex backgrounds
than standard ISTD datasets, limiting IR-DETR’s adaptability.
This further supports the strong multiscale detection capability
of the proposed model for DSIRSTs.

VII. C ONCLUSION
In this article, an efficient and lightweight network,
MSCK-Net, is proposed for RSIRSD. To mitigate the limitations of existing datasets, such as small scale, poor
generalization, and limited practical utility, we construct the
large-scale IRShip dataset, facilitating the real-world application of infrared ship detection methods. We further develop
the CP-PB offline augmentation method and introduce a
Dense-O2O online enhancement strategy, which significantly
increases positive sample density per image, alleviates the
positive-negative sample imbalance in DETRs-based models, and accelerates convergence. An innovative CKConv is
designed for low-level feature extraction, integrating the morphological characteristics of ships and local features of small
