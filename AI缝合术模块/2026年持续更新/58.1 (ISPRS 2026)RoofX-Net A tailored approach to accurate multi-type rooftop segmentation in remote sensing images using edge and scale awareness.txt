ABSTRACT
High-resolution remote-sensing images play a vital role in advancing solar photovoltaic (PV) system deployment, which is crucial for renewable energy generation. Urban rooftops are widely recognized as optimal
platforms for PV deployment, and the accurate identification of multi-type rooftops using remote-sensing
images is essential for effective PV capacity planning. However, existing image segmentation models face
challenges in rooftop segmentation, particularly in addressing ambiguous edges and variations in rooftop scales.
To address these challenges, we propose RoofX-Net, a novel decoder within an encoder–decoder framework
designed to enhance the precision of rooftop segmentation. RoofX-Net introduces two key modules: (1) the Edge
Extraction Module, which employs hand-crafted edge-computing kernels for improved edge detection, and (2)
the Scale Awareness Module, which addresses scale variations by generating geometric awareness at different
scales, with a specific focus on small-scale rooftops. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of RoofX-Net
on the WHU dataset and our established Rooftop+ dataset, which is specifically curated to support multi-type
rooftop segmentation. RoofX-Net demonstrated superior performance across both datasets. Notably, on the
Rooftop+ dataset, our model achieves an overall accuracy of 96.39%, a mean Intersection over Union (mIoU)
of 90.75%, and an F1-score of 95.11%, outperforming the compared models. RoofX-Net is versatile and can
be integrated into existing segmentation frameworks, offering enhanced performance with minimal additional
cost. In practical urban planning projects, our method significantly reduces planning time while maintaining
reliability, demonstrating substantial potential for optimizing solar PV deployment in urban environments. The
implementation of the proposed method is publicly available at https://github.com/K-Y-Chen/RoofX-Net.

1. Introduction
Remote-sensing images are essential in advancing solar energy applications, particularly in facilitating photovoltaic planning. Solar energy has been widely recognized as a sustainable alternative to mitigate the environmental impacts associated with fossil fuel consumption (Solangi et al., 2011). Among the various solar energy applications, solar photovoltaic (PV) systems, which convert sunlight directly
into electricity, are particularly noteworthy. In urban environments,
rooftops provide an ideal platform for deploying solar PV systems. The
accurate identification and area estimation of multi-type idle rooftops
using high-resolution remote-sensing images represent a critical and
challenging preliminary task for PV capacity planning in solar energy
development projects.

Image segmentation has been extensively employed across various
domains, including medical imaging (Liu et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
2025), autonomous driving (Lai et al., 2022), industrial applications
(Yang et al., 2022), and remote sensing (Long et al., 2023). Its
widespread adoption can be attributed to its capability of providing
high-precision delineation and enabling automation, both of which
are critical for accurate and efficient analysis in these domains. These
characteristics make image segmentation particularly suitable for solar
photovoltaic (PV) pre-tasks, which involve the automatic extraction
and analysis of building structures from high-resolution remote-sensing
images (Zhang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2020; Qiu et al., 2022; Chen
et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025). By quantifying the total number of
pixels within segmented multi-type rooftop objects, the rooftop area
can be accurately assessed for photovoltaic capacity planning.

2. Related work

To achieve precise segmentation, modern models commonly utilize
an encoder–decoder framework (Zhu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020;
Badrinarayanan et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018). The encoder transforms
the input image into a feature map, capturing its high-level semantics.
Subsequently, the decoder then progressively up-samples this feature
map to reconstruct the spatial details of the original image and generate
per-pixel segmentation predictions. The segmentation model is optimized to ensure high accuracy by minimizing a pixel-wise loss, which
quantifies the discrepancy between the predicted segmentation and the
corresponding ground truth.
However, we observed that current segmentation models exhibit
sub-optimal performance in accurately segmenting multi-type rooftops.
As shown in Fig. 1, our experimental results reveal that these limitations are primarily manifested in two aspects: (1) the tendency
to produce ambiguous edges and (2) the inability to detect smaller
rooftops effectively. We attribute these deficiencies to the decoder’s
limited capability in capturing the distinct geometric features and
scale variations characteristic of rooftops in remote-sensing images.
Consequently, there is a pressing need to develop a more sophisticated
decoder capable of leveraging explicit geometric features across varying
scales, such as edges, to improve the precision of multi-type rooftop
boundary segmentation.
Inspired by the above analysis, we follow the encoder–decoder
structure and re-design a decoder RoofX-Net with multiple RX blocks.
Specifically, the RX block consists of two key designs: (1) the Edge
Extraction Module, equipped with hand-crafted edge-computing kernels (i.e., Sobel and Laplacian) for accurate edge identification; (2) the
Scale Awareness Module, which generates geometric awareness across
different scales to address the scale variations.
We evaluate our method on the public WHU dataset (Ji et al.,
2018) and a new Rooftop+ dataset collected for multi-type rooftop
segmentation regarding solar photovoltaic deployment. The Rooftop+
provides the per-pixel rooftop segmentation ground truths with different rooftop types (i.e., slope, standard, and steel), where only the
standard and steel rooftops are suitable for solar photovoltaic deployment. On the Rooftop+ dataset, the overall accuracy (OA), mean
Intersection over Union (mIoU), and the F1-score achieved by our
method are 96.39%, 90.75%, and 95.11%, respectively. These results
demonstrate that the proposed model delivers precise segmentation
results for remote-sensing images, which are essential for deploying
photovoltaic systems in urban solar energy projects. Furthermore, on
the WHU dataset, our method significantly outperforms the baseline
models, achieving improvements of 2.68% in mIoU and 2.37% in
F1-score.
We summarize our contributions as follows:
∙ We propose a novel decoder named RoofX-Net, tailored for multitype rooftop segmentation from remote-sensing images. We develop
the Edge Extraction Module to generate more distinct edges within
predictions, and the Scale Awareness Module to address scale variations. Consequently, our framework facilitates the precise estimation
and allocation of photovoltaic capacity in solar energy projects.
∙ We establish an extensive dataset of high-resolution remotesensing images, labeled with detailed annotations to identify three
distinct types of rooftops. The dataset comprises 5346 training images
and 2346 testing images, covering 16 urban areas.
∙ We conduct extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of
our proposed method. Our method demonstrates superior performance
in predicting small-scale rooftops, achieving an 8.29% increase in the
F1-score. Furthermore, the proposed decoder RoofX-Net can seamlessly integrate into most existing segmentation methods, enhancing
performance with minimal additional cost.

segmentation methods, with particular emphasis on building segmentation methods.
2.1. Image semantic segmentation
Semantic segmentation has witnessed significant advancements in
recent years, largely driven by the rapid development of deep learning techniques. The introduction of Fully Convolutional Networks
(FCN) (Long et al., 2015) was a major breakthrough, enabling convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for dense pixel-wise predictions. Building on this foundation, encoder–decoder architectures have gained
widespread adoption (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Badrinarayanan et al.,
2017; Lin et al., 2017; Yuan et al., 2020). Further innovations, such
as those in the Deeplab series (Chen et al., 2014, 2017a,b, 2018), have
further enhanced segmentation accuracy through techniques like atrous
convolution, spatial pyramid pooling, and advanced decoder designs.
More recently, transformer-based models (Zheng et al., 2021; Liu
et al., 2021) have emerged, incorporating self-attention mechanisms
to enhance multiscale representation and global context modeling.
2.2. Building segmentation
Image semantic segmentation methods (Liu et al., 2019; Zhang
et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2022)
are widely applied in building segmentation for remote-sensing images.
Inspired by FCN (Long et al., 2015), SRI-Net (Liu et al., 2019) introduced a Spatial Residual Inception (SRI) module, which is designed to
capture multiscale contextual information and accurately detect large
buildings while maintaining computational efficiency. This approach
demonstrated significant improvements over FCNs for building detection in remote-sensing images. DS-Net (Zhang et al., 2020) further
advanced the field by proposing a dual-stream network that effectively
captures both local and long-range contextual information, enabling
accurate mapping of building rooftops. Map-Net (Zhu et al., 2020)
employed parallel pathways to gradually generate high-level semantic
features at a fixed resolution, enhanced by an attention mechanism
and a pyramid spatial pooling module to refine building footprints.
MSL-Net (Qiu et al., 2022) focused on extracting multiscale and multilevel building features, achieving an optimal balance between segmentation accuracy and inference speed. To address the challenges
posed by shadows, occlusions, and complex building materials, BOMSCNet (Zhou et al., 2022) integrated an encoder–decoder architecture
with a multiscale context-aware module and a direction feature optimization module. This design aimed to mitigate discontinuities in
building extraction caused by shadows, occlusions, and complex materials, thereby improving boundary accuracy and multiscale context
integration.
Recent advancements in the field have leveraged additional supervisory signals from remote-sensing imagery, such as boundaries,
backgrounds, and orientations. GCCINet (Feng et al., 2022) addressed
the challenges of large-scale variability and complex backgrounds in
remote-sensing images by employing continuous atrous convolution
feature enhancement, global high-low feature cross-fusion, and crosslayer refined fusion with boundary refinement modules. In the context
of PV deployment, PV-CSN (Chen et al., 2024) tackled the challenges
of photovoltaic diversification and complexity by integrating receptive
field enhancement and multi-scale attention mechanisms. Similarly,
PV Segmenter (Wang et al., 2025) utilized frequency-domain learning,
edge-guided feature discrimination, and cross-layer fusion to improve
the localization of distributed PV systems.
However, the aforementioned additional supervision was extracted
using learnable networks, which introduced variability during training

The significant scale variations of rooftops present challenges for

5. Experiment results
In this section, we demonstrate the efficiencies of the proposed
RoofX-Net on Rooftop+ and WHU datasets.
We implement the encoder in Deeplabv3+ (Chen et al., 2018) as an
encoder in Fig. 2. It is pertinent to mention that Deeplabv3+ has formally integrated ResNet50 (He et al., 2016), Xception (Chollet, 2017),
and MobileNet (Howard et al., 2017) as its backbone networks. In this
work, we refer to these models as baseline models named Deeplabv3+ R, Deeplabv3+ -X, and Deeplabv3+ -M, respectively. Correspondingly,
the variants of our proposed RoofX-Net, labeled as RoofX-NetR, RoofXNetX, and RoofX-NetM, are designed to ensure full compatibility with
and effective extension of the existing encoder frameworks. To provide
a comprehensive comparison, we implement additional models listed
in Table 3 and evaluate their performance on both datasets. Notably,
the methods mentioned above incorporate learnable modules to capture
geometric features from remote-sensing images, whereas our approach
relies solely on handcrafted edge computing kernels. The segmentation
results are summarized in Table 3.

4.2. Implementation details and training
We follow the basic encoder design of Deeplabv3+ (Chen et al.,
2018) as the encoder in our framework. For the decoder, RoofX-Net, the
number of layers 𝐿 is set to 4. In the four successive RX blocks, the numbers of convolution kernels 𝐶 are set to [512, 256, 128, 64] in the Feature
Propagation Module, [256, 256, 128, 64] in the Edge Extraction Module,
and [128, 128, 64, 64] in the Scale Awareness Module, respectively. The
Edge Extraction Module in the RX block employs a combination of
Laplacian and Sobel as handcrafted kernels. For the training, we use
the cross-entropy loss as segmentation loss 𝑠𝑒𝑔 . To achieve optimal
performance, the loss weights in Eq. (14) are set to 𝛼 = 0.7 and 𝛽 =
0.3. The images in both datasets undergo the same data augmentation
strategies, including normalization, random horizontal flipping, and
random Gaussian blur. During training, we employ stochastic gradient
descent (SGD) with Step LR for all models, running them for 300
epochs. The initial learning rate is set to 0.1 for the Rooftop+ dataset
and 0.07 for the WHU dataset. All models are evaluated on the NVIDIA
A100 GPU.

5.5. Cross region generalization
To assess the generalization capability of RoofX-Net across different
regions, we conducted a leave-one-region-out experiment. Specifically,
the Rooftop+ dataset was divided into four regions based on geographic
and urban characteristics: East, West, South, and North, as illustrated
in Fig. 6(d). For each experiment, one region was entirely excluded
and used as the test set, while the model was trained on data from
the remaining three regions. This experimental setup provides a rigorous evaluation of the model’s ability to generalize to new regions
with varying rooftop structures, urban layouts, and environmental
conditions.
The results are presented in Table 12, which presents the performance of RoofX-Net across different regions. Among the regions,
the West experiences the largest drop in mIoU, with a decrease of
4.45% compared to the complete dataset, while the East shows the
smallest drop, with a decrease of 2.03%. For the F1-score, the most
significant reduction is observed in the West (−3.99%), and the smallest
reduction occurs in the South (−2.36%). Despite these variations, the
OA remains above 92% across all regions. The West region records
the lowest OA (92.89%), and the South region achieves the highest
OA (94.21%). Precision and Recall follow similar trends, with the
largest reductions observed in the West region (−4.53% and −3.44%,
respectively) and the smallest reductions in the East region (−3.08%
and −2.92%, respectively).
Fig. 10 highlights the performance drop under different settings.
The performance drop for Steel rooftops is the least noticeable due to
their simpler structure, which allows them to be more clearly identified
across varying data distributions. Standard rooftops exhibit larger performance differences, due to the lack of small-scale rooftop information
in the region. For Slopes rooftops, the main source of performance
degradation lies in less distinct boundary delineation. Nevertheless, the
basic rooftop structures can still be effectively identified.

(4) Handcrafted Kernels vs. Learnable Modules.
Previous works focus on designing dedicated learnable networks
to extract additional supervision from remote-sensing images, such
as boundary and orientation. However, we argue that simple handcrafted kernels can provide sufficient feature extraction capabilities
for this task. In this ablation study, we retain the basic structure of
RoofX-Net and evaluate three variants: RoofX-NetM with handcrafted
kernels (RoofX-NetM(H)), RoofX-NetM with learnable modules (RoofXNetM(L)), and RoofX-NetM with a handcrafted kernel Canny-based
edge detector (RoofX-NetM(C)). To ensure reliable evaluation, each
experiment is repeated three times, and the results are summarized in
Table 11.
The comparison with RoofX-NetM(C), which uses the widely
adopted Canny edge detector for extracting edge features, demonstrates
the effectiveness of our proposed handcrafted kernels. RoofX-NetM(H)
achieves the highest performance on both datasets, while maintaining
a simple design. RoofX-NetM(L), which replaces handcrafted kernels
with learnable modules, shows slightly lower performance. Together
with the results in Table 3, the results highlight the capability of
the handcrafted kernels to extract robust features without requiring
additional learnable parameters.
As presented in Table 11, RoofX-NetM(L) exhibits the standard
deviation. Specifically, RoofX-NetM(L) achieves an OA of 96.12% on
the Rooftop+ dataset and 98.81% on the WHU dataset. Notably, RoofXNetM(H) attains higher F1-scores and mIoU values on both datasets,
further demonstrating the superior performance of handcrafted kernels
in segmentation tasks.

RoofX-Net was applied to a roof planning task in Chongqing, China

including model inference, result analysis, and manual verification, was
completed by one person in 3 days. This is a significant reduction
in time compared to the traditional manual method, which typically
requires 2 weeks for a similar scope of work. This application shows
that the model can effectively streamline the process of roof selection
for PV projects by reducing the reliance on manual inspection while
maintaining reliable accuracy.

6. Conclusion
This paper is supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No: U22B2061, 62576078) and the Sichuan
Provincial Natural Science Foundation (Grant No: 2025ZNSFSC1467,
2026NSFSC0429).

In this work, we proposed a novel decoder designed for multitype rooftop segmentation from remote-sensing images, specifically
aimed at identifying idle rooftop areas suitable for photovoltaic system
installations. The specialized decoder RoofX-Net effectively captures
the distinct geometric characteristics and manages scale variations of
rooftops in remote-sensing images, resulting in more precise rooftop
segmentation. The proposed method is evaluated on the WHU and
the Rooftop+ datasets. The experimental results highlight the potential
of the model for precise rooftop segmentation in solar photovoltaic
projects. This research provides valuable insights and advancements in
rooftop segmentation and its application in solar energy projects.
Limitation: Despite its strong performance on remote-sensing images, we observe that our model suffers from performance degradation
when applied to natural images, where geometric features and scale
variations are less pronounced. In future work, we aim to address these
limitations by integrating handcrafted features to enhance the model’s
performance on natural images.
