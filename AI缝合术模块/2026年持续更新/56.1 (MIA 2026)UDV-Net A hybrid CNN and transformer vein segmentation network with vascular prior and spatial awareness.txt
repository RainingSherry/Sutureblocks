CNNs handling multi-scale variations and Transformers modeling long-range dependencies are crucial for vascular segmentation. The fusion of these two models eﬀectively combines the multi-scale local features extracted by
CNNs with the global information modeled by Transformers, signiﬁcantly enhancing the accuracy of blood vessel
segmentation. However, the powerful model faces challenges when dealing with the gradual formation of extensive collateral vessels in the upper digestive system veins of patients with cirrhotic portal hypertension, leading
to numerous false negative and false positive segmentation results. To this end, the paper proposes UDV-Net, a
fusion network combining CNN and Transformer with vessel prior and spatial awareness for upper digestive system vein vessel segmentation. Initially, a CNN utilizing an encoding-decoding architecture is employed to create
a multi-scale representation of blood vessels from the image. The representation is further reﬁned by the blood
vessel attention module at the corresponding scale to address tubular structures, thereby reducing false positive
results. Secondly, a Transformer bridge with three-dimensional voxel position encoding is proposed to connect
the corresponding encoding-decoding layer, eﬀectively perceiving widely distributed blood vessels with diverse
shapes, improving blood vessel connectivity, and avoiding false negative blood vessel results. We collected and
annotated abdominal contrast-enhanced CT images of 191 patients with liver cirrhosis, constituting the PHCT
dataset. Our method’s validation result on this dataset is state-of-the-art. When evaluated on the publicly available 3D-IRCADb dataset as an unseen external validation set for PHCT, the model demonstrated satisfactory
performance. Additionally, our method also achieves the optimal performance on the public MSD hepatic vessel
dataset.

1. Introduction
Liver diseases result in two million deaths annually, with cirrhosis as a primary cause of death De Franchis et al. (n.d.), Sun et al.
(2023). Conditions like cirrhosis can lead to an abnormal increase in
portal vein blood ﬂow and a sustained rise in portal vein pressure. Portal hypertension further triggers extensive collateral circulation formation De Franchis (2015). The venous vessels of the upper digestive system are closely connected to the portal vein system, and their structure
and appearance are directly aﬀected by portal hypertension. Their clinical manifestations are crucial for clinical diagnosis and treatment. For

example, esophageal and gastric varices are complications caused by
portal hypertension, which can be very harmful and potentially fatal if
ruptured.
Abdominal contrast-enhanced portal venous phase CT images are the
main modality for diagnosing venous lesions in patients with portal hypertension. The venous vascular regions of the upper digestive system
in the study include esophageal and extraluminal varices, gastric fundus varicose veins, hepatic portal vein, splenic portal vein, and superior
mesenteric vein, as well as collateral circulation vessels formed due to
portal hypertension. These regions are collectively referred to as the
upper digestive system vein (UDV), and precise and rapid voxel-level

Currently, deep learning techniques play a signiﬁcant role in vascular segmentation tasks. U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015) based on
convolutional neural network (CNN) is the primary method for vessel
segmentation. Incorporating skip connections in U-Net allows merging
encoder feature maps with the corresponding decoder maps. This fusion
of features at various scales aids in the restoration of more precise image
edges and details. Yu et al. Yu et al. (2019) integrated residual blocks
into 3D U-Net to segment hepatic vein and portal vein. LVSNet Yan et al.
(2021), which includes an attention-guided module (AGC) in U-Net, utilized attention from high-level features to direct low-level features and
capture more vascular details. Gao et al. Gao et al. (2023) introduced a
Laplacian saliency gated ﬁlter to U-Net. Following the fusion of multiscale features, the features were ﬁltered to enhance the vascular area
while suppressing other areas of the liver. However, convolutional networks primarily capture local patterns due to limited receptive ﬁelds
and translation-equivariant weight sharing, which can hinder modeling
of long-range dependencies.
Vision Transformer (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) divides the input
image into a sequence of patches, enhancing the overall perception of
the image by utilizing the powerful long-range dependency modeling capability of self-attention. However, the Transformer’s extensive training
parameters rely on a larger training dataset, which can be challenging
to acquire, particularly in the ﬁeld of medical imaging. Consequently,
many studies are exploring the use of the Transformer to establish longrange connections between the local features extracted by CNN, aiming
to create a network that combines the strengths of both CNN and Transformer. Yu et al. Yu et al. (2022) integrated the channel attention vision
transformer (CAViT) into the skip connection of U-Net to leverage the
interdependence among channel features for enhancing the extraction of
crucial edge structures of fundus blood vessels. Xu et al. Xu et al. (2024)
introduced a dual-stream encoder that merges CNN encoding with Swin
Transformer encoding. The Transformer branch is utilized to establish
the relative position relationship between targets, create connections between patches, and broaden the receptive ﬁeld; the CNN branch captures
local features and enhances sensitivity to small blood vessels, resulting
in improved segmentation of the hepatic vein and portal vein. Li et al. Li
et al. (2023) utilized U-Net as the backbone network for fundus vessel
segmentation. They substituted the bottleneck layer with Transformer
to incorporate long-distance dependencies for comprehensive modeling.
Additionally, they integrated local attention into the jump connection
to improve edge details.
However, existing methods have two deﬁciencies in segmenting UDV
region: 1) False negative predictions for small vascular structures. Some
methods yield fragmented segmentation results for minor vessels that
should be continuous in the Ground Truth, while other models directly
produce negative results, as illustrated in Fig. 1(b). 2) False positive outcomes for metal instruments. As depicted in Fig. 1(c), the CT value of the
metal instrument indicated by the red arrow is similar to the CT value of
the vein on the portal venous phase contrast-enhanced CT image, leading the model to generate a false positive result for this instrument and
incorrectly recognize it as the target vessel.
To tackle the complexity of the UDV region, it is essential to obtain
vessel priors using classical image-processing algorithms. Then, this paper proposes a novel hybrid framework of CNN and Transformer, which
integrates vascular priors and spatial perception for precise segmentation of UDV in patients with portal hypertension. Firstly, the CNN encoder with a pyramid structure extracts multi-scale features from the
input 3D image. Subsequently, the vessel attention module computes
the Hessian and applies the FrangiFrangi et al. (1998) vesselness ﬁlter
to multi-scale feature maps, incorporating prior knowledge about vessels to ﬁlter out non-tubular regions, enhancing the model’s perception
of anisotropic vessels. Lastly, the Vision Transformer bridges the corresponding stages of the CNN encoder and decoder. A voxel position encoding (VPE) module improves the Transformer’s positional awareness

and enhances vessel connectivity. VPE simulates global spatial position
information, aiding in extracting deep semantic features of the network
and eﬀectively addressing the issue of vascular interruption.
The contributions are summarized as follows:
1) We propose a novel UDV segmentation network that eﬀectively
fuses the strengths of traditional image processing techniques (e.g.,
Frangi ﬁltering) with those of CNNs and Transformers. This network
enables end-to-end, fully automatic voxel-level segmentation of upper
digestive system veins on contrast-enhanced CT.
2) We propose a vessel attention module that combines vascular
priors with multi-scale features to enhance the model’s perception of
anisotropic vessels.
3) We propose a spatial-awareness Transformer with VPE that enhances spatial positioning performance and preserves vessel continuity,
leading to a reduction in both false-negative and false-positive detection
outcomes.
4) We collect and construct a novel dataset of abdominal contrastenhanced portal venous phase CT images from patients with portal hypertension (PHCT). We professionally annotate a total of 191 CT images
to delineate upper gastrointestinal venous vessels, thereby supporting
the training and validation of venous segmentation methods.
2. Related works
2.1. Frangi ﬁlter
The Frangi ﬁlter Frangi et al. (1998) is a widely used method for enhancing vascular structures in medical images. It relies on second-order
image derivatives to identify vessel-related intensity variations across
scales. Speciﬁcally, the ﬁlter computes the Hessian matrix at multiple
orientations to capture local structural information. By analyzing the
eigenvalues of the Hessian, the method distinguishes tubular structures
from blob-like or plate-like patterns (e.g., calciﬁcations), thereby selectively enhancing vessel-shaped regions.
In this study, we integrate a Frangi-based vascular attention module
into the channel features at each level of the encoder. Because the encoder progressively downsamples feature maps, this design enables the
attention module to detect vascular structures at multiple resolutions,
facilitating multi-scale representation of vascular morphology.
2.2. CNN
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have long been the dominant
paradigm in medical image analysis. U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015)
is a representative architecture with two major strengths: (1) its Ushaped encoder-decoder design enables eﬀective multi-scale feature extraction, and (2) skip connections enrich high-level semantic features
and reﬁne low-level boundary details by directly propagating encoder
features to the decoder. The 3D U-Net Çiçek et al. (2016) extends this
architecture from 2D to volumetric data, whereas V-Net Milletari et al.
(2016) incorporates residual connections to improve gradient ﬂow and
enhance representation learning. DenseU-Net Wu et al. (2021b) substitutes traditional encoding-decoding modules with densely connected
blocks Huang et al. (2017), enabling high-density 3D feature representation and improving the detection of small structures in holographic
reconstruction. U2 -Net Qin et al. (2020) further deepens contextual
modeling by embedding nested U-shaped modules within each stage.
U-Net++ Zhou et al. (2018) redesigns skip connections with nested
and densely connected pathways to reduce semantic gaps between encoder and decoder. U-Net 3+ Huang et al. (2020) strengthens full-scale
feature aggregation through extensive cross-layer connections, facilitating more accurate segmentation of targets with diverse scales. More recently, nnU-Net Isensee et al. (2021) has automated network conﬁguration by analyzing dataset-speciﬁc “ﬁngerprints,” thereby optimizing architectures, hyperparameters, and preprocessing steps without manual

In this study, we leverage a Transformer to connect corresponding
layers of the U-Net encoder and decoder, modeling spatial voxel representations at multiple resolutions. Given the demonstrated versatility
and eﬀectiveness of CNN-Transformer hybrid models, our approach is
primarily compared against these methods throughout the paper.

2.3. Transformer
The Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017), originally developed for natural language processing, employs a self-attention mechanism to capture long-range dependencies, addressing the limitations of traditional
CNNs and RNNs in modeling global context. The Vision Transformer
(ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) adapts this framework to images by partitioning them into patches and establishing dependencies between any
two positions, thereby eﬃciently capturing global information. ViT relies on absolute positional encoding to retain spatial information, but
this approach limits the model’s ﬂexibility. To address this, Wu et al.
Wu et al. (2021a) incorporated relative positional encoding Shaw et al.
(2018), allowing ViT to adapt more eﬀectively to inputs of varying resolutions, although at the cost of increased complexity. Conditional Positional ViT (CPVT) Chu et al. (2021) further improves ﬂexibility by
dynamically generating conditional positional encodings that adjust to
input size. Beyond positional encoding limitations, standard ViT uses
ﬁxed-size image patches and lacks inherent multi-scale feature processing. Swin Transformer Hatamizadeh et al. (2022a) introduces a sliding window mechanism, enabling hierarchical patch processing and
enhanced multi-scale representation. Similarly, Pyramid Vision Transformer (PVT) Wang et al. (2021) employs a pyramid structure to progressively extract features at diﬀerent scales while capturing both global
and local context.

3. Methods
Our method aims to improve the representation of blood vessel by
connecting a U-shaped network with a Transformer bridge. As shown
in Fig. 2, the backbone network of our UDV-Net consists of an encoderdecoder architecture, with each encoder-decoder level connected by a
spatial-aware Transformer. The output features of each encoder level
will be enriched with vascular priors through the vessel attention module. This enhancement will assist the voxel position encoding module
within the Transformer structure in recognizing the spatial relationships
between voxels. The following are detailed explanations.

The UDV-Net, as proposed in the paper, utilizes nnU-Net as the backbone network for segmenting the UDV. The image X serves as input and
is forwarded to the encoder for feature extraction. Each layer of the
encoder downsamples the extracted features before passing them to the
subsequent layer. Simultaneously, the features are fed into the vessel attention module to enhance the vascular structure and then transmitted
to the Transformer. The Transformer reconstructs the spatial position of
the features, reshapes them, and sends them to the corresponding layer
of the decoder. Subsequently, the features are element-wise summed
with those upsampled from the lower layer of the decoder. Finally, the
prediction results are generated. The loss between the prediction result
Y and the true label G is then calculated, followed by gradient backpropagation.

3.2. Spatial-aware transformer with voxel position encoding
For 3D images, features can be extracted from pixels in a 2D slice
image or from voxels across multiple slices. The beneﬁt of 3D images
compared to 2D images is the inclusion of spatial information. To enhance the utilization of information across slices, we adopt the approach
proposed in CPVT Chu et al. (2021) and develop a Transformer model
with voxel position encoding for 3D images.

4.4. Ablation experiment
The proposed UDV-Net comprises two key modules: a vascular attention module (VAM) and a spatial-awareness Transformer. VAM strengthens the representation of vascular structures, whereas the spatialawareness Transformer improves spatial modeling. To assess their individual contributions, we removed each module in turn while keeping
all other settings unchanged. As shown in Table 3, the model combining
both modules achieved the best performance, with Dice, Jaccard, 95HD,
and ASD of 0.958, 0.936, 1.738, and 0.697, respectively.

To address these issues, we propose a hybrid CNN-Transformer network with spatial awareness and vascular prior. The spatial-awareness
Transformer module eﬀectively captures the wide vessel distribution by
encoding the voxel position, thereby reducing the phenomenon of breakage in the segmentation results (Fig. 1b). To provide structural cue for
the model without interfering with semantic learning, we incorporated
a vessel-enhancement prior derived from the classical Frangi ﬁlter. This
prior information is not directly applied to semantic feature maps, but is
fused through a learnable vascular attention module (VAM). This design
enables the network to selectively utilize structural information while
preserving semantic integrity. In this manner, the Frangi prior, as a soft

complementary cue, not only highlights tubular patterns and suppresses
background noise, but also eﬀectively reduces false positive segmentation caused by metal artifacts (Fig. 1c). Our method achieves state-ofthe-art (SOTA) performance on both the internal PHCT datasets, external 3D-IRCADb dataset and publicly available hepatic vessel datasets
(MSD hepatic vein). In key evaluation indicators such as Dice, Jaccard,
95HD, and ASD, this method comprehensively surpasses previous research results (Tables 1–2), and signiﬁcantly improves the key failure
modes shown in Figs. 3–4.
Despite these advances, our framework’s performance is inherently
linked to the quality of the vascular prior used. The chosen Frangi
ﬁlter, which relies on Hessian eigenvalue analysis, struggles in clinically complex scenarios. Its core assumption of tubular structure fails
at the junctions and overlapping regions of blood vessels; its response
is unstable in high-curvature vascular segments; and its gradient-based
9

B. Shen et al.

nature yields weak and fragmented priors in thrombotic or low-contrast
areas. Fortunately, due to the fact that the Frangi response is used only
as a soft prior through attention, the aforementioned limitations may
constrain the strength of the structural cue but do not distort the core
semantic features. Therefore, we mainly consider Frangi as an eﬃcient
baseline to validate the eﬀectiveness of our prior integration framework,
while acknowledging that its own quality determines its contribution to
the ﬁnal performance.
To address these bottlenecks, we propose to adopt non-Hessian
strategies, especially monogenic-signal-based local phase analysis, being
a promising direction. By decomposing local measurement into phase,
amplitude, and orientation, the monogenic signal oﬀers descriptors that
are robust to complex topologies and intensity inhomogeneity. Future
extension will explore the following directions: (1) using local phase
information to more accurately model vascular overlap and abnormal
collaterals; (2) exploiting phase-amplitude decoupling to enhance robustness to thrombus-induced disruptions; and (3) integrating phase,
amplitude, and orientation features via a multi-feature fusion attention
module to generate richer priors. The contribution of this article also introduces a practical trade-oﬀ: although the proposed spatial-awareness
Transformer and VAM signiﬁcantly improve segmentation accuracy,
they also increase the size and computational cost of the model. This
expense will aﬀect the deployment of the model in resource constrained
environments. Thus, the future work will focus on lightweight design,
including methods such as model pruning, knowledge distillation, and
eﬃcient Transformer modules.
We also note a practical trade-oﬀ introduced by our design. Compared with the baseline U-Net, integrating the spatial-awareness Transformer and VAM consistently improves segmentation performance but
increases the number of parameters and computational cost. In our implementation, these modules raise both the parameter count and FLOPs
during inference. While the overhead remains moderate relative to the
accuracy gains, it may hinder deployment in resource-constrained settings. Future work will therefore investigate lightweight and acceleration techniques, including model pruning, knowledge distillation, and
more eﬃcient Transformer variants.
In summary, our hybrid framework integrates long-range reasoning
with spatially awareness and vascular priors containing structural information, aiming to improve vessel segmentation performance in complex clinical scenarios. While this study focuses on the structure of upper gastrointestinal veins, the architectural principles proposed here are
not task-speciﬁc, and the framework has the potential to be extended to
other vascular systems such as retinal vessels. Looking ahead, a highly
promising research path is to integrate more robust signal-driven priors
(such as the monogenic signal Felsberg and Sommer (n.d.)) and combine
them with model compression strategies (including pruning, knowledge
distillation, and eﬃcient Transformer modules) to further enhance the
robustness and clinical applicability of the model in diverse vascular
segmentation tasks.

Future work will focus on addressing the segmentation challenges
