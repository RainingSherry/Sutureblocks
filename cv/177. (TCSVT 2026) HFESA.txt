Ultra-High-Definition Image Restoration via
High-Frequency Enhanced Transformer
Chen Wu , Ling Wang , Zhuoran Zheng , Weidong Jiang , Yuning Cui , Graduate Student Member, IEEE,
and Jingyuan Xia , Member, IEEE

Abstract—Transformer-based architectures exhibit substantial
promise in the realm of ultra-high-definition (UHD) image
restoration (IR). Nevertheless, they encounter significant challenges in maintaining high-frequency (HF) details, which are
crucial for the reconstruction of texture. Conventional methods tackle computational complexity by significantly reducing
the resolution (by a factor of 4 to 8). Moreover, the majority of high-frequency components are eliminated due to the
inherent characteristics of self-attention mechanisms, as these
mechanisms tend to naturally suppress high-frequency elements
during non-local feature integration. This paper proposes a
dual-branch transformer architecture that synergistically combines native-resolution HF preservation with efficient contextual
modeling, named HiFormer. The high-resolution branch utilizes
a directionally-sensitive large-kernel decomposition to effectively address anisotropic degradations with fewer parameters
and applies depthwise separable convolutions for localized
high-frequency (HF) information extraction. Concurrently, the
low-resolution branch assimilates these localized HF elements
using adaptive channel modulation to offset spectral losses
induced by the inherent smoothing effect of self-attention. Comprehensive experiments across numerous UHD image restoration
tasks reveal that our approach surpasses current leading methods
in both quantitative metrics and qualitative analysis. The code
is available at https://github.com/5chen/HiFormer
Index Terms—Image restoration, UHD image, frequency learning, transformer.

distribute across 4-8× larger spatial regions than equivalents
in common images, yet derive semantic meaning from

(which are common gaming or home GPUs, known for

memory sizes of up to 12 GB).
Recent transformer-based approaches for UHD image
restoration face an inherent resolution-fidelity trade-off rooted
in architectural constraints. Current methodologies primarily
adopt two compromised strategies to handle UHD’s extreme
pixel density: i) Patch-based processing (e.g., LLFormer [22])
divides images into sub-regions for localized restoration, but
introduces boundary artifacts due to inconsistent feature aggregation across adjacent tiles [23]; ii) Aggressive downsampling
(e.g., UHDformer [24] employs 8× resolution reduction)
enables GPU-compatible processing by sacrificing spatial
fidelity, particularly high-frequency components essential for
texture clarity. Both strategies stem from the self-attention
mechanism’s dual limitations - its quadratic computational
complexity prohibits native UHD processing, while its inherent
low-pass filtering characteristic [25], [26], [27] systematically
attenuates high-frequency signals during global feature integration. This creates a paradoxical scenario where maintaining
computational feasibility necessitates information loss, and
preserving full spectral integrity demands prohibitive hardware
resources. The central challenge thus resides in developing
mechanisms that reconcile transformer-based global modeling
with precise high-frequency preservation under strict memory
constraints.

HiFormer, a novel dual-branch transformer architecture that
strategically reconciles high-frequency preservation with computational efficiency in UHD restoration. Our framework

II. R ELATED W ORK
A. Vision Transformer for Image Restoration

operating at complementary resolutions. The high-resolution
branch directly processes native UHD inputs through two
synergistic pathways: i) Direction-sensitive local modeling
using decomposed large-kernel convolutions that capture
anisotropic degradations (e.g., motion blur, rain streaks)
through spatial factorization, and ii) High-frequency mining
via depthwise separable convolutions coupled with channel attention, explicitly preserving texture-critical components
often suppressed by standard operations. These extracted
high-frequency features serve as adaptive supplements to the
parallel low-resolution processing stream. The low-resolution
branch employs conventional self-attention mechanisms but
crucially integrates high-frequency guidance through feature
recalibration modules. This cross-branch interaction compensates for the intrinsic spectral losses caused by both
downsampling operations and attention-based low-pass filtering. A hierarchical fusion mechanism then combines the
globally aware contextual features from the low-resolution
stream with the locally preserved high-frequency details,
enabling continuous information exchange while preventing boundary artifacts common in patch-based approaches.
Extensive validation across five challenging UHD restoration scenarios (low-light enhancement, deraining, dehazing,
deblurring, and desnowing) demonstrates our method’s consistent superiority in preserving fine textures and structural
integrity (See Fig. 1). The results reveal significant qualitative
improvements in edge sharpness and artifact suppression compared to existing transformer-based approaches, particularly in
handling directional degradations while maintaining practical
GPU memory consumption.
Our main contributions are summarized below:
• We introduce HiFormer, a novel dual-branch transformer
architecture that enables full-resolution UHD image
restoration on consumer-grade GPUs. This is achieved
through a systematic spatial-computational decoupling
strategy, representing a significant architectural innovation for native-resolution processing in UHD restoration
tasks.

In the wake of the ViT [28] success, numerous transformerbased strategies for image restoration have emerged. IPT
[29], a trailblazer in applying Transformer-based models
to image restoration, addresses computational challenges by
dividing input images into smaller patches and processing
them in sequence. Uformer [2] and SwinIR [30] utilize
window-based self-attention mechanisms to enhance image
restoration efficiency. With the rising interest in linear attention, Restormer [3] introduces a channel-level computation
of self-attention mechanisms, notably decreasing the model’s
computational burden. GRL [31] incorporates both windowbased and channel-based self-attention methods, offering
versatile solutions for various image restoration tasks. DRSformer [32] presents a top-k sparse attention approach to
limit feature aggregation among unrelated tokens, minimizing
the interaction between mismatched query and key tokens
to efficiently perform image deraining. Similarly, AST [33]
develops an adaptive sparse self-attention mechanism to prevent unnecessary interactions from irrelevant regions. Beyond
spatial-domain research utilizing self-attention, some studies
are exploring transformer capabilities in the frequency domain.
For example, FFTformer [34] takes advantage of the convolution theorem to transform the calculation of attention maps
from spatial-domain matrices to element-wise multiplication
in the frequency domain, resulting in significant computational
savings. In addition to these works, other noteworthy studies
have achieved impressive outcomes in diverse image restoration domains, such as deraining [35], [36], [37], dehazing
[38], [39], [40], and deblurring [41], [42]. Despite these
methods yielding promising results, the massive pixel count
of UHD images demands considerable memory, restricting
their feasibility on consumer-grade GPUs. Nevertheless, the
significant performance advancements offered by transformerbased techniques in restoration tasks, due to their advanced
non-local modeling capabilities, are striking. Consequently,
there is an urgent demand for a specialized and efficient
transformer-based UHD image restoration method, capable of
handling UHD images with reduced computational resource
requirements.

B. UHD Image Restoration
The restoration of UHD images through learning-based
techniques has attracted notable attention recently. Certain
studies [43], [44], [45] integrate bilateral learning within
deep learning frameworks to tackle UHD image restoration challenges. Their central concept involves extracting
local affine coefficients from low-resolution images using
the bilateral grid and applying these to high-resolution
images. Other research efforts have investigated [46], [47]
processing UHD images in the frequency domain because
this approach efficiently discriminates between various types
of degradations. For example, UHDFour [46] reveals that
both high-resolution and low-resolution images share similar amplitudes, with luminance information predominantly
residing in the amplitude. Hence, they enhance the frequency domain of the low-resolution image followed by its
upscaling to high-resolution, addressing the issue of UHD
low-light enhancement effectively. Wave-Mamba [48] is an
innovator in utilizing state space models to capture longrange dependencies in UHD images, yielding promising results
for low-light enhancement tasks. Leveraging the powerful
non-local modeling capabilities afforded by the self-attention
mechanism, several studies have examined transformer-based
architectural applications in diverse UHD image restoration
tasks, exemplified by LLFormer [22] and UHDformer [24].
LLFormer decomposes UHD images into non-overlapping
patches, processes each as a separate low-resolution image,
which may introduce boundary artifacts and affect visual
quality. UHDformer proposes a dual-path correlation matching
transformation module that optimally interpolates features
between high- and low-resolution spaces to exclude redundant

content, thereby enhancing feature representations in lowresolution space for more effective UHD image recovery.
Nonetheless, substantial downsampling results in considerable
information loss. Moreover, transformer-based methods face a
common challenge: the self-attention mechanism functions as
a dynamic low-pass filter, naturally neglecting high-frequency
details. Consequently, developing methods that re-emphasize
high-frequency information is crucial for network enhancement in focusing on textural detail restoration.
III. M ETHODOLOGY
A. Overview
The architecture of our HiFormer is depicted in Fig. 2
(a). Starting with an image I ∈ RH×W×3 , HiFormer initially
utilizes a convolutional layer to generate the low-level feature
maps F0 ∈ RH×W×C , where H, W, and C denote height,
width, and channels, respectively. The low-level features F0
are then processed through a 3-level hierarchical encoderdecoder architecture, similar to UNet [49], to derive deep
features Ire ∈ RH×W×C . During this procedure, the image’s
resolution is initially decreased by a given factor and subsequently restored to its original size through a matching
increase in resolution. Conversely, the feature channels display
the opposite pattern, fluctuating in number. Both the encoder
and decoder incorporate multiple High Frequency Enhanced
Transformer (HFET) modules (see Fig. 2 (b)), which integrate
dual-branch learning to emphasize the reconstruction of highfrequency detail information. Ultimately, HiFormer applies
another convolutional layer to compute the residual image
R ∈ RH×W×3 from Ire . The final restored image is acquired

by summing the degraded image with the residual image,
represented as Î = I + R.
B. High Frequency Enhanced Self-Attention
HFESA constitutes the central element of HFET, with its
architecture illustrated in Fig. 2 (c). It primarily aims to
enhance the network’s focus on high-frequency detail while
simultaneously prioritizing the restoration of background texture information. This system integrates high-resolution and
low-resolution branch learning within its framework. The
synergistic learning of these branches leads to improved image
restoration outcomes.
1) High-Resolution Branch Learning: UHD images are
dominant by their resolution of 3840 × 2160, which results
in a higher pixel count than standard images. This means
that each image contains more content and displays complex local patterns, which poses challenges for traditional
convolutional sizes in capturing the intricate variations of
texture patterns. Specifically, semantically coherent structures
in UHD images can span hundreds or even thousands of pixels,
while conventional 1 × 1 or 3 × 3 convolutions, due to their
limited receptive fields, restrict the network’s ability to model
subtle spatial dependencies and fine-grained texture patterns.
To address this issue, the high-resolution branch employs
convolutions with expanded receptive fields to capture these
local features, denoted as Fh ∈ RH×W×C . However, simply
increasing the receptive field leads to a growth in model
parameters. To mitigate this, large kernels are decomposed into
a linear combination of asymmetric elongated convolutions,
which process local features independently along the H and W
dimensions. This design not only reduces parameter overhead
but also enhances the model’s ability to recognize directional
patterns, making it particularly suitable for addressing various
types of directional degradation, such as rain streaks or motion
blur. For layer-normalized feature maps Fin ∈ RH×W×C , this
can be mathematically expressed as:
Fh = W p1×1 Wd3×3 ([Wd11×1 Fin ; Wd1×11 Fin ]),

Additionally, a dedicated high-frequency extraction pathway
is constructed to explicitly capture and preserve texture-critical
details that are often suppressed in subsequent downsampling and global modeling stages. Inspired by previous work
[50], we utilize 3 × 3 depthwise convolutions to capture
high-frequency information, effectively extracting local highfrequency components such as edges and fine textures with
minimal computational overhead. Before extracting highfrequency information, we employ a 1 × 1 convolution as
a lightweight feature projector, enabling efficient fine-grained
feature interactions. This process can be denoted as:

The low-resolution branch in our HFESA is designed to
address this dual challenge: efficiently performing global modeling in the low-resolution space while actively compensating
for the lost high-frequency information by incorporating the
high-frequency feature maps from the high-resolution branch.

IV. E XPERIMENTS
A. Implementation Details and Datasets
1) Implementation Details: Our experiments are conducted
using PyTorch on a setup of 4 NVIDIA A800 GPUs. The
model is trained for 300k iterations utilizing the Adam
optimizer (as described by Kingma and Ba [55]) with hyperparameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. The learning rate is initially
set to 2 × 10−4 and is adjusted according to a cosine annealing
schedule throughout the training. Specifically, the warmup
period spans 15% of the total epochs, and the maximum
learning rate is reached at 75% of the total epochs. For input,
we randomly crop the original 4K images to a resolution
of 768 × 768 and use a batch size of 8. To enhance the
training dataset, we apply random horizontal and vertical flips
to the images. Our method’s architecture features a 3-level
encoder-decoder with a varying number of transformer Blocks
at each level, specifically [4, 4, 8] from level 1 to level 3. The
downsampling ratio is set to 4. To optimize the weights and
biases of the network, we utilize the L1 loss in the RGB color
space as the basic reconstruction loss.
2) Datasets: To assess the performance of HiFormer, we
conduct evaluations using multiple UHD-related datasets.
UHD-LOL4K [22] is an extensive low-light outdoor dataset
featuring 8,099 images in 4K resolution, with 5,999 images
dedicated to training and 2,100 images utilized for testing.
UHD-LL [46] is a real-world low-noise/normal-clear paired
image dataset that contains 2,150 pairs of 4K UHD data

In recent years, some studies [5], [27], [74], [75] have

The results indicate that frequency domain processing is not

increases the inference time of our HiFormer.

most relevant to the V projection. This process significantly
reduces domain gaps and resolution misalignment between
the two branches. In contrast, simple concatenation fails to
establish such a refined modulation relationship, leading to
less efficient and more passive feature integration. In summary,

V. C ONCLUSION

By explicitly supplementing high-frequency cues, the model
gains an enhanced capacity to reconstruct fine-grained textural
details that are often lost during global feature aggregation,
thereby substantially improving the overall restoration quality,
particularly in regions rich in high-frequency content such as
edges and textures.
3) Effectiveness of Dual-Branch Learning: The dualbranch learning strategy constitutes the architectural core of
HiFormer, designed to synergistically address the distinct
yet complementary challenges of local high-frequency detail
preservation and global contextual modeling in UHD image
restoration. To rigorously assess the necessity and superiority
of this design, we conduct a comprehensive ablation study,
which involves either removing the corresponding branches or
replacing them with standard residual convolution blocks (ResBlocks). It is important to note that since ResBlocks do not
include depthwise separable convolutions and consist solely of
traditional convolutions, they incur a higher parameter count.
The quantitative results summarized in Tab. XII clearly
demonstrate the indispensability of both branches. Removing
any one of the branches leads to a significant decline in performance. The removal of the high-resolution branch results
in the most pronounced degradation, as it is responsible for
modeling local information and capturing fine-grained highfrequency details, which are crucial for texture clarity in
UHD images. Similarly, the removal of the low-resolution
branch also leads to a substantial decrease, highlighting the
importance of addressing long-range dependency modeling
and suppressing the flow of unimportant information over large
spatial extents in UHD content. In Fig. 10 (g)-(j), we present
the visualized spectral maps of these ablation results. It can be
observed that our method yields the most natural distribution
of spectral energy, with richer high-frequency information.
Additionally, we present the experimental results of replacing each branch with ResBlocks to avoid the parameter
imbalance caused by the removal of branches. The results
indicate that for the high-resolution branch, the proposed
directionally-sensitive large kernel decomposition and dedicated high-frequency mining operations are more effective
than standard convolution blocks in handling anisotropic
degradation and extracting key texture information. For the
low-resolution branch, the lack of global modeling capability
in ResBlocks leads to a decline in performance, validating that
the proposed HFESA outperforms local convolution operations
in capturing the non-local interactions necessary for coherent
global restoration.

In this paper, we proposed a novel high frequency enhanced
transformer for UHD image restoration, dubbed HiFormer.
HiFormer adopts a dual-branch learning strategy: the highresolution branch focuses on modeling local features and
preserving high-frequency details, while the low-resolution
branch addresses non-local interactions and artifact removal,
enhanced by the collaboration of high-frequency information.
By focusing on and enhancing high-frequency detail information, our method has achieved state-of-the-art results on
UHD low-light image enhancement, UHD image deraining,
UHD image dehazing, UHD image deblurring and UHD image
desnowing.
